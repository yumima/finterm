# Path: Day Trader Foundations

Tape reading, intraday setups, risk-per-trade discipline, and building the habits that separate survivors from statistics.

Estimated time: **5–7 hours of focused reading**, then weeks of deliberate practice on paper before live capital.

## Who This Track Is For

You want to trade intraday — opening and closing positions within the same session. You understand that day trading has an extremely high failure rate and you want to approach it with a structured framework, not guesswork. You're prepared to treat it as a skill that requires significant practice before it generates consistent returns. This track gives you the mental model; practice and screen time give you the edge.

## What "Done" Looks Like

Before entering a trade, you know: the entry price, the stop price, the R-multiple you're targeting, and the maximum position size given your risk parameters. You know your daily stop-loss — the dollar loss that triggers you to stop trading for the day. You do not take more than 2 setups simultaneously. You close the platform after hitting your daily loss limit.

## Key Mindset Shift

**Most day trading losses are self-inflicted, not market-inflicted.** The market doesn't know you exist. Your losses come from entering before a setup is confirmed, sizing too large for your comfort level, widening stops because you can't accept being wrong, or trading out of boredom rather than opportunity. The market rarely takes your money — you give it.

The hardest discipline in day trading is **doing nothing**. There will be many days when no setup that meets your criteria appears. The untrained instinct is to force a trade — to manufacture a reason to be in the market. Professional day traders describe their work as mostly waiting, punctuated by brief periods of precise execution. The willingness to do nothing on a day with no setups is itself an edge.

**The math requires a positive expectancy.** If your average win is 2R and your average loss is 1R, you only need to win more than 33% of trades to be profitable. If you're winning 40% but your average win is 0.8R and your average loss is 1.5R (because you let losses run and cut winners), you'll lose money with a 40% win rate. Track R-multiples, not win/loss percentages.
