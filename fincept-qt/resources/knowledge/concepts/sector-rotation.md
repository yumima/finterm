# Sector Rotation

Sector rotation is the practice of shifting capital between industry sectors as the economy moves through the **business cycle**. The logic is that different sectors of the economy are more or less sensitive to growth, inflation, and interest rates at different points in the cycle — and that these sensitivities are systematic enough to exploit.

## The 11 GICS sectors

The Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) divides the equity market into 11 sectors:

| Sector | Key characteristics | Cyclical or defensive? |
|---|---|---|
| **Financials** | Banks, insurers; profit from rates + credit | Cyclical (rate-sensitive) |
| **Consumer Discretionary** | Retail, autos, restaurants, media | Cyclical (income-sensitive) |
| **Industrials** | Machinery, transport, defense, aerospace | Cyclical (capex-driven) |
| **Technology** | Software, semis, hardware | Cyclical/growth (rate-sensitive) |
| **Materials** | Chemicals, metals, mining | Cyclical (commodity-driven) |
| **Energy** | Oil, gas, refiners | Cyclical (commodity-driven) |
| **Health Care** | Pharma, biotech, devices, MCOs | Defensive |
| **Consumer Staples** | Food, beverage, household products | Defensive |
| **Utilities** | Electricity, gas, water | Defensive (bond proxy) |
| **Real Estate** | REITs, commercial real estate | Defensive (rate-sensitive) |
| **Communication Services** | Telecom, digital media, streaming | Mixed (large-cap growth) |

## The rotation map

The standard rotation model, popularized by Merrill Lynch and Fidelity, maps sector outperformance against the four phases of the [[macro-cycles|business cycle]]:

```
EARLY CYCLE          MID CYCLE           LATE CYCLE          RECESSION
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Financials           Technology          Energy              Consumer Staples
Consumer Disc.       Industrials         Materials           Health Care
Industrials          Materials           Health Care         Utilities
Real Estate          Consumer Disc.      Consumer Staples
                                         Utilities
```

**Early cycle** (recovery): The Fed has cut rates; credit is easing; consumers and businesses begin spending again. Financials benefit from improving loan demand and wider NIMs as the curve steepens. Consumer Discretionary rebounds as employment recovers.

**Mid cycle** (acceleration): Growth is strong; earnings are rising broadly; Technology leads on high-revenue growth and operating leverage. Industrials benefit from rising capital expenditure. This is typically the longest phase.

**Late cycle** (deceleration / overheating): Inflation is rising; the Fed is hiking; input costs are elevated. Energy and Materials benefit from rising commodity prices. Defensives begin to outperform on relative basis as growth stocks de-rate.

**Recession** (contraction): Revenue and earnings fall broadly. Consumer Staples, Health Care, and Utilities hold up because their demand is inelastic — people still buy food, medicine, and electricity. These sectors also carry dividends that become attractive when bond yields start falling.

## Why it's not simple

The rotation model is a rough framework, not a precise clock. Several complications:

**Markets lead the economy by 6–12 months.** By the time economic data confirms "early cycle," the early-cycle sectors have already outperformed. You need to be positioned ahead of the shift.

**Sectors are not homogeneous.** "Technology" includes mega-cap quasi-monopolies (AAPL, MSFT), high-growth unprofitable software, and cyclical semiconductor equipment. Their behavior within the sector can diverge widely.

**The 2010s distorted the model.** In an era of near-zero rates, Technology dominated almost every cycle phase because discount rates were suppressed. High growth rates got valued extremely richly when risk-free rates were zero.

**Inflation complicates the late cycle.** When inflation is structural rather than cyclical (as in 2022), the rotation map breaks down. Energy massively outperformed in 2022 while Technology collapsed — but the recession never materialized as sharply as the rotation implied.

**Weight distortion.** The S&P 500 is now more than 30% Technology + Communication Services. "Underweight tech, overweight defensives" is a bold relative call with high career risk for active managers.

## Relative strength and momentum

Sector rotation is often implemented using **relative strength**: comparing sector returns against the broad market (SPY) or against each other. A sector with rising relative strength is gaining price momentum; falling relative strength means it's lagging.

12-month price momentum has historically been one of the most persistent factor effects in equities — rotating toward strong-momentum sectors and away from weak-momentum ones captures part of this effect.

## Rate sensitivity by sector

Interest rate changes affect sectors differently — an important overlay on the cycle rotation model:

| Sector | Rate ↑ effect | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Utilities | Negative | Bond proxy; valuations compress; dividend yield less competitive |
| Real Estate | Negative | Higher borrowing costs; cap rate expansion |
| Financials | Positive short-term | Wider NIM on deposits vs. loans |
| Technology (growth) | Negative | Long-duration cash flows discounted more heavily |
| Energy | Mixed | Higher rates slow economy, reduce demand; offset by inflation correlation |
| Consumer Staples | Negative | Bond proxy qualities compress |

## Practical implementation rules

- **Don't rotate all at once**: markets don't flip overnight; scale into rotations over weeks
- **Check fundamentals**: rotation is a screening framework, not a standalone thesis
- **Use relative strength as confirmation**: don't fight a sector with 12 months of negative relative return
- **Hedge implicit rate bets**: a "sell tech, buy energy" rotation is partly a rate trade
- **Watch sector credit spreads**: sector-level credit markets often confirm equity rotation signals
