# Currency and FX Markets

The foreign exchange market is the **largest financial market in the world** by trading volume — approximately $7.5 trillion per day. Unlike equities or bonds, FX has no central exchange; it operates as a decentralized OTC network of banks, central banks, corporations, asset managers, and speculators. Understanding FX is essential for any portfolio with international exposure and for reading global macro conditions.

## Exchange rate basics

An exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. Conventions vary:

- **Direct quote** (USD base): how many units of foreign currency buy one USD. Example: USD/JPY = 150 means 1 USD buys 150 yen.
- **Indirect quote** (USD terms): how many USD for one unit of foreign currency. Example: EUR/USD = 1.08 means 1 euro costs $1.08.
- **Cross rate**: exchange rate between two non-USD currencies, implied from their respective USD rates.

**Spot rate**: the current exchange rate for immediate delivery (T+2 settlement for most pairs).
**Forward rate**: the exchange rate locked in today for delivery at a future date.

## What drives exchange rates

Exchange rates are set by supply and demand — but the underlying drivers operate across very different time horizons.

**Short term (days to weeks)**: Risk appetite, headline news, central bank commentary, positioning data, technical levels.

**Medium term (weeks to months)**:

| Driver | Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Interest rate differentials** | Higher rates attract capital → currency appreciates | Carry trade; capital flows to higher-yielding assets |
| **Inflation differential** | Higher inflation → currency depreciates | Purchasing power erodes; PPP convergence |
| **Current account balance** | Surplus → appreciation; deficit → depreciation | Trade flows create net currency demand |
| **Capital flows** | FDI, equity/bond inflows → appreciation | Investors buy local currency to invest |
| **Risk sentiment** | Risk-off → safe haven currencies appreciate (USD, JPY, CHF) | Flight to quality |

**Long term (years to decades)**: Purchasing power parity, productivity differentials, demographic trends, reserve currency status.

## Purchasing power parity (PPP)

**PPP** states that exchange rates should, over the long run, equalize the price of identical goods across countries. If a basket of goods costs $100 in the US and £85 in the UK, the implied PPP exchange rate is $1.18/£.

The most famous illustration is the **Big Mac Index** (The Economist), which compares the cost of a Big Mac globally to identify overvalued and undervalued currencies.

PPP is a terrible short-term trading tool. Currencies can deviate from PPP for years or decades — the JPY was 40-50% undervalued on PPP in 2023–2024. But PPP provides a **gravitational center**: currencies that stray far from PPP tend to mean-revert eventually, driven by trade flow adjustments.

## The carry trade

The carry trade is one of the most persistent and well-documented FX strategies:

1. Borrow in a low-interest-rate currency (e.g., JPY at near 0%)
2. Convert to and invest in a high-interest-rate currency (e.g., BRL at 10%)
3. Earn the **interest rate differential** ("the carry")

The risk: the high-yielding currency depreciates and wipes out the carry gain. This is the **uncovered interest parity (UIP)** theory — which predicts the carry trade shouldn't work because exchange rate moves should offset rate differentials. In practice, UIP fails consistently in the short run; the carry trade generates positive returns with periodic severe drawdowns.

Carry trade **unwinds** are violent: when risk appetite turns, all carry positions unwind simultaneously, causing sharp appreciations in funding currencies (JPY, CHF) and collapses in yield currencies (BRL, ZAR, TRY). The August 2024 JPY squeeze was a textbook carry unwind.

## Safe haven currencies

In risk-off environments (market stress, geopolitical shocks), capital flows to:

- **USD** (US dollar): world's reserve currency; flight to safety in crisis
- **JPY** (Japanese yen): historically a funding currency; massive foreign assets held by Japan; repatriation accelerates during stress
- **CHF** (Swiss franc): political neutrality, strong institutions, current account surplus

These currencies typically **appreciate** during equity sell-offs and stress events. Tracking USD strength (DXY index) and JPY crosses (USD/JPY) is a real-time stress gauge.

## Central bank intervention

Governments and central banks intervene in FX markets when currency moves threaten economic stability:

**Direct intervention**: Central bank sells foreign reserves to buy domestic currency (supporting a weak currency) or buys foreign currency to weaken domestic currency (export competitiveness). The Bank of Japan intervened repeatedly in 2022–2024 as JPY weakened past 150.

**Jawboning**: Verbal statements signaling displeasure with currency moves; often the first tool before direct intervention.

**Capital controls**: Restricting foreign capital flows to prevent currency collapse. Used in EM crises (Malaysia 1998, Cyprus 2013, Argentina multiple times). Extreme tool; signals distress.

**Reserve management**: Selling USD reserves to defend a currency peg. Saudi Arabia's peg to the USD requires maintaining vast reserves to defend the rate. If reserves fall too low, a devaluation or peg break can be destabilizing.

## FX and the equity portfolio

For US-based investors, international equity returns have two components: local market returns and FX returns. A Japanese equity fund up 10% in yen terms is only up 4% if the yen depreciated 6% against the dollar.

FX **hedging** strips out currency exposure using FX forwards or swaps. Many international equity ETFs offer hedged and unhedged versions. The cost of hedging is approximately equal to the interest rate differential between the two currencies.

In practice:
- **Hedging makes sense** when your view is that currency movement will be unfavorable and the cost of hedging is low
- **Unhedged exposure** makes sense when the foreign currency is expected to appreciate (or when hedging costs are high)
- **A rising USD** is a persistent headwind for international equity returns when measured in USD

## Key FX indicators and cross-rates

| Indicator | What it measures | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **DXY** | USD vs. basket of 6 major currencies (EUR dominated) | Broad USD strength |
| **USD/JPY** | Yen cross | Risk sentiment; carry trade health |
| **EUR/USD** | Most traded pair globally | Eurozone conditions vs. US |
| **USD/CNH** | Offshore yuan | China capital flows; global EM sentiment |
| **USD/EM EM** baskets | EM currency health | Global risk appetite; commodity demand |
| **BBDXY** (Bloomberg) | Trade-weighted USD index | More comprehensive than DXY |

## Common FX mistakes for investors

- **Ignoring FX when investing internationally** — currency can add or destroy 30%+ of returns over a cycle
- **Treating USD strength as permanent** — the USD goes through long cycles of strength and weakness
- **Carrying EM exposure without understanding political/FX risk** — EM currencies can devalue 20–40% in a crisis
- **Not accounting for hedging costs** — when US rates are much higher than foreign rates, hedging international exposure can cost 3–5% per year
