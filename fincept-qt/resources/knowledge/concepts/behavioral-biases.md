# Behavioral Biases in Investing

The mental shortcuts ("heuristics") that helped your ancestors avoid being eaten quietly destroy returns in markets. Recognizing them in yourself is the foundation of investing discipline.

## The big nine

| Bias | What it makes you do | Antidote |
|---|---|---|
| **Loss aversion** | Hold losers too long; sell winners too early | Hard stops; predefined exit rules |
| **Anchoring** | Fixate on entry price as if it has meaning | Re-evaluate at fair value each day |
| **Recency bias** | Project recent trends as the new norm | Look at 10y+ history; mean-reversion |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seek info that supports your thesis | Active disconfirmation; pre-mortem |
| **Overconfidence** | Increase size after winning streak | Track expectancy; size by edge, not feel |
| **Herd behavior** | Buy what everyone else is buying | Do your own work; know your thesis |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | "I already lost $X, may as well wait" | Decide based on forward expectation only |
| **Endowment effect** | Overvalue things you own | Ask: would I buy this position today? |
| **Narrative fallacy** | Believe compelling stories without evidence | Demand quantifiable, falsifiable claims |

## Worked example — loss aversion

```
Trade A: Bought at $100, now $90 (−10%)
  You: "I'll just wait for it to come back to $100, then sell"
  
Trade B: Bought at $100, now $110 (+10%)
  You: "Better take this profit before it gives back"

Both decisions are wrong. The "right" decision is forward-looking:
  - Is current $90 still attractive vs better alternatives? If no, sell — don't anchor to $100.
  - Is $110 still attractive vs better alternatives? If yes, hold — don't anchor to "profit."
```

Loss aversion makes you do the opposite of optimal: ride losers down, cut winners short. Studies (Kahneman, Tversky, Odean) show retail investors sell winners 50% more often than losers.

## Worked example — confirmation bias

You buy NVDA at $400. Over the next month you:
- Read 5 bullish articles, ignore 5 bearish ones
- Listen to bullish podcasters, dismiss bearish analysts as "haters"
- Re-tweet your own bullish thesis, screenshot quotes that agree
- Treat any down day as "noise," any up day as "thesis confirmed"

By month 3, you've built an evidence wall against re-thinking. By month 6 if NVDA drops 20%, you double down because "the thesis is intact" — even though your information set is now systematically biased.

**Antidote**: every quarter, deliberately seek out the strongest bear case for each holding. Write it down. If it persuades you, act.

## Behavioral patterns in trading

| Phase | Bias-driven behavior |
|---|---|
| Bull market peak | Overconfidence + recency = max sizing at max prices |
| First crack | Disbelief; "buy the dip" reflex |
| Real decline | Loss aversion; stop honoring stops; "average down" |
| Capitulation | Panic-selling at the bottom |
| Recovery | Skepticism; sell into strength too early |
| New highs | Cycle repeats |

## How to actually fight biases

1. **Pre-commit to rules** — write entry, stop, exit before placing trade. Honor them.
2. **Trade journal** — review weekly. Pattern recognition on your own mistakes.
3. **Pre-mortem** — before each big trade: "imagine this trade lost everything; why?"
4. **Decision quality vs outcome quality** — judge your process, not just whether you made money
5. **Cooldown periods** — never make a major position decision within 24h of a market event
6. **External accountability** — explain your thesis to someone smarter; if they're skeptical, listen
7. **Statistical thinking** — focus on expectancy across many trades, not a single trade

## Famous behavioral disasters

- **Long-Term Capital Management (1998)** — overconfidence in models, sized positions for "5σ events" that arrived
- **2000 dot-com bubble** — narrative fallacy ("New Economy") + recency bias
- **2008 housing** — anchoring to "house prices never decline nationally" + herd behavior
- **2021 SPACs / meme stocks** — herd + recency + narrative fallacy + greater-fool theory
- **Crypto cycles** — every cycle is a textbook of all 9 biases at once

## Decision rules

- **Trades with high emotional load** → halve position size
- **After 3 losing trades in a row** → step away for 24h
- **After 5 winning trades in a row** → don't increase size; you're due for noise
- **Holding through a thesis-violating event** → sell first, re-evaluate later
- **Strong urge to act** → write the action down. Wait 30 minutes. Re-read. Often disappears.
