The mental shortcuts that helped your ancestors avoid being eaten quietly destroy returns in markets. Recognizing them in yourself is the foundation of investing discipline.

Biases are not character flaws. They're the default operating system every human ships with — fast, automatic, and deeply incompatible with markets. The job isn't to "be rational"; it's to build process that boxes the irrationality in.

## The big nine

Each row is a single failure mode and the one rule that disarms it.

| Bias | What it makes you do | Antidote |
|---|---|---|
| **Loss aversion** | Hold losers too long; sell winners too early | Hard stops; predefined exit rules |
| **Anchoring** | Fixate on entry price as if it had meaning | Re-evaluate at fair value each day |
| **Recency bias** | Project the last 6 months as the new norm | Look at 10y+ history; expect mean-reversion |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seek info that supports your thesis | Active disconfirmation; pre-mortem |
| **Overconfidence** | Increase size after a winning streak | Track expectancy; size by edge, not feel |
| **Herd behavior** | Buy what everyone else is buying | Do your own work; know your thesis |
| **Sunk cost fallacy** | "I already lost `$X`, may as well wait" | Decide on forward expectation only |
| **Endowment effect** | Overvalue what you already own | Ask: would I buy this position today? |
| **Narrative fallacy** | Believe compelling stories without evidence | Demand falsifiable, quantifiable claims |

## Worked example — loss aversion

```
Trade A   Bought at $100, now $90 (−10%)
          Reflex: "I'll wait for it to come back to $100, then sell"

Trade B   Bought at $100, now $110 (+10%)
          Reflex: "Better take this profit before it gives back"
```

Both reflexes are wrong. The optimal answer is forward-looking: at `$90`, is this still attractive vs better alternatives? At `$110`, is it still attractive? Anchoring to your entry price puts the past in charge of the future.

Studies (Kahneman, Tversky, Odean) show retail investors sell winners about **50% more often than losers** — the exact opposite of "let your winners run."

## Worked example — confirmation bias

You buy `NVDA` at `$400`. Over the next month you read five bullish articles and ignore five bearish ones. By month three you've built an evidence wall against rethinking. By month six, if the stock drops 20%, you double down because "the thesis is intact" — even though your information set is now systematically biased.

**Antidote:** every quarter, deliberately seek the strongest bear case for each holding. Write it down. If it persuades you, act.

## How to actually fight biases

- **Pre-commit to rules** — write entry, stop, and exit before placing the trade. Honor them.
- **Trade journal** — review weekly; pattern-match your own mistakes.
- **Pre-mortem** — before each big trade: "imagine this trade lost everything; why?"
- **Decision quality vs outcome quality** — judge process, not just whether you made money.
- **Cooldown periods** — never make a major position decision within 24h of a market event.
- **External accountability** — explain your thesis to someone smarter; if they're skeptical, listen.
- **Statistical thinking** — focus on expectancy across many trades, not a single trade.

## Famous behavioral disasters

- **`LTCM` (1998)** — overconfidence in models, sized for "5σ events" that arrived.
- **2000 dot-com bubble** — narrative fallacy ("New Economy") + recency bias.
- **2008 housing** — anchoring to "house prices never decline nationally" + herd behavior.
- **2021 SPACs / meme stocks** — herd + recency + narrative fallacy + greater-fool theory.
- **Crypto cycles** — every cycle is a textbook of all nine biases at once.

## Decision rules

- **High emotional load** — halve the position size.
- **Three losing trades in a row** — step away for 24h.
- **Five winning trades in a row** — don't increase size; you're due for noise.
- **Holding through a thesis-violating event** — sell first, re-evaluate later.
- **Strong urge to act** — write the action down, wait 30 minutes, re-read. It usually disappears.
