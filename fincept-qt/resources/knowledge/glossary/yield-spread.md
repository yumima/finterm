A **yield spread** is the difference in yield between two fixed-income instruments, typically expressed in basis points (bps) — most commonly comparing a corporate or credit bond to a benchmark risk-free Treasury of similar maturity.

Yield spreads are the primary measure of credit risk compensation in bond markets. A wider spread means investors demand more yield to compensate for credit risk; a tighter spread means investors are comfortable with the risk.

## Formula

```
Yield Spread = Bond Yield − Benchmark Treasury Yield

1 basis point (bp) = 0.01%
100 bps = 1 percentage point
```

## Worked example

```
10yr Treasury yield:           4.50%
10yr investment-grade bond:    5.20%
IG spread:                     +70 bps

10yr high-yield bond:          8.00%
HY spread:                     +350 bps

Same bond one month later (recession fears):
HY yield:                      9.50%
HY spread:                     +500 bps (spread widening = risk-off)
```

## Spread types

| Type | Definition | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Option-adjusted spread (OAS)** | Spread adjusted for embedded options (calls, puts) | MBS, callable bonds |
| **Z-spread (zero spread)** | Constant spread over the entire spot rate curve | More precise than single-point spread |
| **Nominal spread** | Simple yield difference | Quick comparison |
| **Asset swap spread** | Spread vs. SOFR in interest rate swap | Floating-rate comparison |
| **CDS spread** | Credit default swap premium | Pure credit risk, no rate risk |

## Spread by credit quality (typical levels)

| Rating | Typical Spread to Treasuries |
|---|---|
| AAA | 20–60 bps |
| AA | 30–80 bps |
| A | 50–120 bps |
| BBB | 80–200 bps |
| BB | 200–400 bps |
| B | 350–600 bps |
| CCC | 600–1500+ bps |
| Distressed / D | 1500–3000+ bps |

These ranges are highly cyclical — spreads compress in bull markets and blow out in recessions.

## Spread as a business cycle indicator

Credit spreads are a leading/coincident indicator of economic stress:

```
ICE BofA HY Index OAS (historical):
  2007 tightening: ~250 bps (peak confidence)
  2008–09 peak:    ~1900 bps (financial crisis)
  2020 COVID:      ~1100 bps (brief but sharp)
  2024 (tight):    ~300 bps (risk appetite high)
```

## Spread compression and carry

Bond investors often pursue spread compression: buying wider-spread bonds expecting spreads to tighten. The total return = yield income + price appreciation from spread tightening.

```
If HY spread tightens from 400 to 300 bps over one year (300 bps tightening):
On a 5-year bond, price appreciation ≈ 5 × 1% = ~5% price gain
Plus ~8% coupon income
Total return: ~13% vs Treasury return of ~4.5%
```

## Pitfalls

- Spread compression can be sudden and violent in either direction — widening spreads can cause capital losses that exceed coupon income.
- Single-name corporate spreads can blow out due to company-specific events (earnings miss, fraud, management change) regardless of market conditions.
- Spread to Treasury benchmark ignores the rate component — a spread can be stable while the underlying Treasury yield moves, changing the absolute yield.
