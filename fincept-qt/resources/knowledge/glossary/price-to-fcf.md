**Price-to-FCF** (Price-to-Free Cash Flow) is the ratio of a company's market capitalization to its annual free cash flow — measuring how many dollars investors pay for each dollar of cash profit the business generates after all reinvestment.

Unlike P/E, which uses accrual accounting earnings, P/FCF uses actual cash flow — a harder-to-manipulate, more fundamental measure of earning power.

## Formula

```
Price-to-FCF = Market Capitalization / Free Cash Flow
             = Share Price / Free Cash Flow per Share

Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures
```

## Worked example

```
Company:
  Market cap: $80B
  Operating cash flow (TTM): $12B
  Capital expenditures (TTM): $2B
  Free cash flow: $10B

Price-to-FCF = $80B / $10B = 8×
FCF Yield = 1/8 = 12.5%
```

A P/FCF of 8× means investors pay $8 for every $1 of free cash generated. At 12.5% FCF yield, the stock returns its full price in free cash every 8 years (ignoring growth).

## P/FCF vs. P/E comparison

```
Same company:
  Net income: $9B  → P/E = $80B/$9B = 8.9×
  FCF: $10B       → P/FCF = $80B/$10B = 8×

FCF > Net income: usually means non-cash D&A exceeds capex (asset-light business)
FCF < Net income: usually means capex exceeds D&A (capital-intensive expansion)
```

## Typical P/FCF ranges by sector

| Sector | Typical P/FCF | Note |
|---|---|---|
| Utilities | 15–25× | Regulated, high capex, stable FCF |
| Consumer staples | 15–25× | Steady cash flows |
| Mature software | 20–35× | High FCF margin, modest capex |
| Industrial | 10–20× | Moderate capex needs |
| Retail (mature) | 8–15× | Low margins, some capex |
| Energy (at cycle) | 5–10× | Commodity-dependent FCF |
| High-growth SaaS | 30–60×+ | Low current FCF; growth premium |

## EV-based version

For capital-structure-neutral comparison:

```
EV/FCF = Enterprise Value / Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)
```

This removes leverage effects and is preferred when comparing companies with different debt loads.

## When P/FCF diverges significantly from P/E

| FCF vs Earnings | Implication |
|---|---|
| FCF >> Earnings | Non-cash charges inflate expenses; business may be better than P/E suggests |
| FCF << Earnings | High capex investment; business is less FCF-generative than P/E suggests |
| FCF negative | Pre-profitability or heavy investment phase; P/FCF not useful |

## Pitfalls

- FCF is lumpy — a year with high capex (factory build, acquisition) depresses FCF and inflates P/FCF; use 3-year average.
- Maintenance capex vs. growth capex distinction matters; companies investing heavily in growth may look expensive on P/FCF but are building future cash flows.
- Stock-based compensation (SBC) is often not deducted from operating cash flow under GAAP — true economic FCF should subtract SBC.
