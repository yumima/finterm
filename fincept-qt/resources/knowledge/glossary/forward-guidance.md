**Forward guidance** is a central bank's communication about the likely future path of monetary policy — interest rates, balance sheet, or both — used to shape market expectations and amplify the effect of current policy.

By telling markets what to expect, a central bank can move long-term interest rates without actually changing short-term rates, because long rates are driven largely by expectations of future short rates.

## How it works

```
Short-term rate (today) + Expected path of future rates = Long-term rate
```

If the Fed commits to keeping rates low for "an extended period," long-term rates fall even before any policy action, loosening financial conditions immediately. This is sometimes called "open-mouth operations."

## Types of forward guidance

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Qualitative / open-ended** | Vague time commitment | "Rates will remain low for an extended period" (2009) |
| **Calendar-based** | Explicit date | "Rates stay near zero through at least mid-2015" (2011) |
| **State-contingent (threshold)** | Tied to economic data | "Rates stay low until unemployment < 6.5%" (2012) |
| **Outcome-based** | Depends on outcomes, not forecasts | "Until substantial further progress" (2020) |

## Worked example — 2020 COVID guidance

```
August 2020: FOMC introduces Flexible Average Inflation Targeting (FAIT)
  → Will not hike until: inflation averages 2% AND is on track to exceed 2% AND maximum employment
  
Effect: Fed funds futures shifted forward, 10yr Treasury yield stayed suppressed
2yr Treasury yield: near zero through 2021 despite rising inflation
```

This example also illustrates the risk: guidance that was too loose contributed to inflation being "transitory" expectations becoming entrenched.

## Credibility is everything

Forward guidance only works if markets believe it. When the Fed broke its own guidance aggressively (hiking 75 bps in 2022), forward guidance lost some credibility for a period — the terminal rate was repriced sharply higher with each meeting.

## Forward guidance vs. market pricing

The "dot plot" (Summary of Economic Projections) is the Fed's primary guidance tool. Markets frequently price a steeper or shallower path than the dots:

- **Market prices lower than dots**: markets bet Fed will cut faster (inflation optimism or growth pessimism).
- **Market prices higher than dots**: markets bet Fed will stay higher longer (inflation pessimism).

## Pitfalls

- Forward guidance creates moral hazard: markets become dependent on Fed signals and react violently to any deviation.
- "Tapering tantrum" (2013): Bernanke merely hinted at slowing QE; 10yr yields jumped 100 bps in weeks.
- Overly precise guidance reduces policy flexibility precisely when flexibility is most needed.
- International spillovers: Fed guidance moves global rates and currencies, not just domestic.
