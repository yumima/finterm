The **price-to-sales ratio (P/S)** measures how much investors pay for each dollar of a company's revenue — useful for valuing companies that have no earnings, where P/E is meaningless.

P/S is particularly common in early-stage software, biotech, and other high-growth industries where companies deliberately invest all revenues into growth, producing minimal or negative earnings.

## Formula

```
P/S = Market Capitalization / Annual Revenue
    = Share Price / Revenue Per Share

Or using enterprise value:
EV/Revenue = Enterprise Value / Annual Revenue
```

EV/Revenue is preferred for comparing companies with different debt levels, as it removes capital structure effects.

## Worked example

```
SaaS company:
  Market cap:    $5,000M
  Annual revenue: $500M
  P/S = $5,000M / $500M = 10×

Mature retailer:
  Market cap:    $8,000M
  Annual revenue: $40,000M
  P/S = $8,000M / $40,000M = 0.2×
```

The SaaS company at 10× revenue reflects high growth and margin expectations. The retailer at 0.2× reflects thin margins and commoditized business model.

## Sector norms for P/S

| Sector | Typical P/S Range | Margin Context |
|---|---|---|
| Grocery / food retail | 0.1–0.5× | 2–4% net margins |
| Automotive | 0.2–0.8× | 3–7% margins |
| Healthcare services | 0.5–1.5× | 5–10% margins |
| Industrial | 1–3× | 8–15% margins |
| Pharma | 3–8× | High margin, patent risk |
| Software (mature) | 5–15× | 25–40% FCF margins |
| High-growth SaaS | 10–30×+ | Reinvesting; near-zero current margins |
| Biotech (pre-revenue) | N/M → use pipeline NPV | No product revenue yet |

## P/S and margin expectations

P/S is meaningful only in the context of margins. Two companies at 10× P/S:
- Company A: 40% FCF margin → effectively 25× FCF (reasonable for software)
- Company B: 5% FCF margin → 200× FCF (very expensive)

A quick cross-check: P/E ≈ P/S / Net Margin.

```
P/S = 10×, Net margin = 20%
Implied P/E = 10 / 0.20 = 50×
```

## Rule of 40 context

In SaaS, the "Rule of 40" links growth and margin: Revenue Growth% + Profit Margin% should exceed 40%.

Companies above the Rule of 40 with high P/S are typically justified. Below Rule of 40 with high P/S is a red flag.

## Pitfalls

- P/S ignores profitability entirely; a company burning $300M/year at 10× P/S is burning its market cap.
- Revenue can be manipulated or boosted by unsustainable promotions, channel-stuffing, or aggressive recognition — always verify revenue quality.
- Comparing P/S across sectors is useless (a grocery chain cannot be compared to a SaaS company).
- EV/Revenue is strictly better than P/S for cross-company comparison because it accounts for debt.
