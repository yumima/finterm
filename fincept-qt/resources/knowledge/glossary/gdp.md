**GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** is the total monetary value of all finished goods and services produced within a country's borders over a specific time period — the broadest single measure of an economy's size and health.

GDP is the dominant benchmark for economic growth, recessions, and relative country comparisons. Markets, central banks, and governments monitor GDP trajectories obsessively because it drives earnings, employment, monetary policy, and fiscal decisions.

## Measurement approaches (all should give same result)

**Expenditure approach (most common):**
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)

C = Consumer spending (typically 65–70% of US GDP)
I = Investment (business capex, residential, inventories)
G = Government spending
X = Exports
M = Imports
(X−M) = Net exports (negative for US: trade deficit)
```

**Income approach:** GDP = wages + profits + rents + interest

**Production approach:** GDP = sum of value added by each industry

## Worked example — U.S. GDP (2024 approximate)

```
US GDP: ~$28.6 trillion (annualized)

Components:
  Consumer spending:  $20.1T  (70%)
  Business investment: $5.1T  (18%)
  Government:          $4.5T  (16%)
  Net exports:        −$1.1T  (−4%)
```

## Real vs nominal GDP

**Nominal GDP** uses current prices — affected by both output growth and inflation.

**Real GDP** is adjusted for inflation using a price deflator — measures actual economic output growth.

```
If nominal GDP grows 6%:
  Inflation: 3%
  Real GDP growth: ~3% (approximately nominal − inflation)
```

When economists say "the economy grew 2.5%," they mean **real** GDP.

## GDP and markets

| GDP Reading | Market Implications |
|---|---|
| 2 consecutive negative real GDP quarters | Technical recession; negative for equities, positive for bonds |
| GDP above potential (>3%) | Inflationary pressure; hawkish Fed |
| GDP at potential (~2%) | Goldilocks; neutral policy |
| GDP well below potential | Easing pressure; risk assets underperform |

GDP data and corporate earnings are highly correlated over time, making GDP growth the anchor for long-term equity return expectations.

## GDP limitations

- Quarterly release with 2 initial revisions — initial estimate can be revised significantly.
- GDP measures quantity of output, not quality of life or distribution of income.
- Underground economy not captured.
- Environmental degradation, unpaid household work not counted.

## GDP growth decomposition

```
GDP growth = Labor force growth + Productivity growth

US potential growth:
  Labor force growth: ~0.5%/year (aging)
  Productivity growth: ~1.5–2.0%/year
  Potential real GDP: ~2.0–2.5%/year
```

## Pitfalls

- GDP is released with a ~30-day lag (advance estimate) and revised twice after; markets react to each revision.
- "GDP growth" alone doesn't indicate health — debt-financed government spending inflates GDP temporarily.
- Emerging market GDP growth rates often look higher but reflect different starting points and structural factors.
