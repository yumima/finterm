# Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B)

```
P/B = Share Price / Book Value Per Share
```

**Book value** is what shareholders would theoretically receive if the company were liquidated at accounting values: assets minus liabilities, divided by share count.

## Worked example — JPMorgan, May 2026

```
Share price:          $200.00
Book value per share: $108.00
P/B:                  200 / 108 ≈ 1.85×
Tangible BV/share:    $90.00
P/TBV:                200 / 90 ≈ 2.22×
```

For banks, P/TBV (tangible book) is more honest than P/B because it strips out goodwill from past acquisitions.

## When it's useful

- **Banks and insurers**: their assets *are* mostly financial — book value approximates intrinsic value. P/B around 1 is "fair."
- **Asset-heavy businesses**: shipping, real estate, mining, REITs.
- **Distressed situations**: a stock trading well below book may signal either a bargain or that the market doesn't trust the asset values.

## When it's misleading

- **Tech and services companies**: their value is in software, brands, and people — none of which sit on the balance sheet. Microsoft's book value is a tiny fraction of its market cap, and that's fine.
- **Goodwill from acquisitions** can inflate book value without creating real shareholder wealth — strip it out (use TBV).
- **Share buybacks** reduce book value (you spend cash to retire equity), which can push P/B *up* even as the business is healthy.

## Sector-typical ranges

| Sector | Typical P/B |
|---|---|
| Big-bank healthy | 1.0–1.6× |
| Quality regional bank | 1.6–2.5× |
| Distressed bank | 0.5–0.9× |
| Insurers | 1.0–1.8× |
| Mature industrial | 1.5–3.0× |
| Tech / SaaS | 5–30×+ (mostly meaningless) |
| Mining / shipping | 0.6–1.5× (cycle-dependent) |

## Variants worth knowing

- **P/TBV** — Price to Tangible Book Value; excludes goodwill and intangibles
- **P/NAV** — Price to Net Asset Value; common for REITs and closed-end funds
- **Discount/Premium to BV** — same idea, expressed as % above/below

## Decision rules

- **Bank P/B < 1 + ROE < cost of equity** → likely fair (book overstated)
- **Bank P/B < 1 + ROE > cost of equity** → potentially undervalued; check loan book quality
- **Bank P/B > 2 + ROE > 15%** → quality compounder; deserves premium
- **Tech P/B** → ignore entirely; use other multiples

## Pair it with

A high [[roe|ROE]] justifies a high P/B. If two banks both trade at 2× book but one earns 15% on equity and the other 7%, the high-ROE bank is the better value despite the same multiple. The relationship: `Justified P/B ≈ (ROE − growth) / (cost of equity − growth)`.
