# Dividend Yield

```
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend per Share / Share Price
```

The cash return you'd earn from dividends alone, ignoring price changes — expressed as a percentage of what you pay today.

A stock at $100 paying $4 in annual dividends has a 4% dividend yield.

## Worked example — Coca-Cola

```
Quarterly dividend:    $0.485
Annualized dividend:   $1.94
Share price:           $62.00
Trailing yield:        1.94 / 62.00  ≈  3.13%

Payout ratio (vs $2.30 EPS):  $1.94 / $2.30  ≈  84%
FCF per share:                $2.10
FCF payout ratio:             $1.94 / $2.10  ≈  92%
```

KO yields 3.1% — solid for a defensive consumer staple. But payout ratios are very high (84% on EPS, 92% on FCF) — there's not much room for the dividend to grow without earnings growth.

## Two ways it gets quoted

- **Trailing yield**: last 12 months of dividends divided by today's price
- **Forward yield**: expected next 12 months of dividends divided by today's price

When companies cut or suspend dividends, the trailing number stays high until enough quarters roll off — making the stock *look* more attractive than it is.

## Yield ranges by category

| Category | Typical yield |
|---|---|
| S&P 500 (long-run avg) | ~2% |
| Tech (mega-cap) | 0.5–2% |
| Consumer staples | 2.5–4% |
| Utilities | 3–5% |
| REITs | 3–7% |
| Telecom | 4–7% |
| MLPs / pipelines | 5–10% |
| BDCs (business dev cos) | 8–14% |
| Yield trap territory | >8% with payout > 100% |

## Famous dividend cuts

| Company | Year | Cut | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| GE | 2018 | 92% | Power & insurance write-downs |
| Wells Fargo | 2020 | 80% | COVID stress + Fed restrictions |
| Disney | 2020 | 100% | COVID park closures |
| Boeing | 2020 | 100% | 737 MAX + COVID |
| Kraft Heinz | 2019 | 36% | Brand value impairment |

The pattern: a "safe" dividend isn't safe when the underlying business breaks.

## Variants worth knowing

- **Indicated yield** — most-recent dividend × 4 / share price; reacts faster than trailing
- **Yield on cost** — dividend / *your* cost basis; rises as you hold
- **Net yield** — after withholding tax (matters for international stocks)
- **Distribution yield** (REITs/MLPs) — includes return of capital, not just income

## A high yield can be a red flag

If a stock's price drops 50%, its dividend yield doubles — but the dividend itself is now much more likely to be cut. Many "high-yield value traps" are stocks where the market has already priced in the next dividend reduction.

Sanity-check questions:
- **Payout ratio** (dividends ÷ earnings): >80% suggests the dividend is fragile
- **Free cash flow coverage**: is the dividend funded by [[fcf|FCF]] or by debt?
- **Sector context**: REITs and MLPs structurally pay high yields — that's not the same as a dividend in distress
- **Dividend history**: a 25-year unbroken streak (Dividend Aristocrats) is a stronger signal than a 5-year one

## Decision rules

- **Yield > 6% with payout > 100%** → likely cut coming; avoid unless you understand why payout is sustainable
- **Yield < 2% on a slow-grower** → opportunity cost vs T-bills
- **Quality compounder yielding 2–4% with rising dividend** → core holding for income+growth
- **REIT yield + AFFO coverage > 1.2** → reasonable income vehicle

## Total return matters more

Two stocks with the same total return may have very different dividend yields. A 0%-yield growth stock that compounds at 12% beats a 4%-yield stock that compounds at 6% (4% dividends + 2% price). Yield is one input, not the whole picture.
