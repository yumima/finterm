The **expense ratio** is the annual percentage of a fund's assets charged to cover operating expenses — management fees, administrative costs, custody, legal, and other overhead. It is the primary ongoing cost of owning a mutual fund or ETF.

The expense ratio is deducted from the fund's assets daily (accrued), reducing NAV over time. It does not appear as a separate line item on brokerage statements; it is silently subtracted from returns.

## Formula

```
Expense Ratio = Annual Fund Operating Expenses / Average Net Assets

Example:
Annual expenses: $5,000,000
Average AUM: $1,000,000,000
Expense ratio: 0.50%
```

## How it affects your returns

```
Investment: $100,000
Gross index return: 8.00% per year
Expense ratio: 0.50%
Net return to investor: 7.50%

After 30 years:
  Gross: $100,000 × (1.08)^30 = $1,006,266
  Net:   $100,000 × (1.075)^30 = $854,401
  Lost to fees: $151,865 (~15% of final wealth)
```

## Expense ratio ranges by fund type

| Fund Type | Typical Range | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Broad passive ETF | 0.03%–0.10% | SPY (0.09%), VOO (0.03%), IVV (0.03%) |
| Sector ETF | 0.10%–0.20% | XLK, XLF |
| International ETF | 0.07%–0.35% | VEA, EEM |
| Bond ETF | 0.04%–0.15% | BND, AGG |
| Active ETF | 0.25%–0.75% | Varies widely |
| Smart beta / factor | 0.15%–0.40% | MTUM, QUAL |
| Traditional mutual fund | 0.50%–1.50% | Most actively managed |
| Hedge fund (informal) | 2.00% + 20% of profits | Not a registered fund ratio |

## Gross vs. net expense ratio

- **Gross expense ratio**: total fund expenses before any fee waivers.
- **Net expense ratio**: what investors actually pay after any temporary fee waivers.

Waivers are contractual and may expire, causing the net ratio to jump to the gross ratio — important to check for new funds where the issuer subsidizes early investors.

## What is NOT included

The expense ratio does not include:
- **Trading commissions** (now largely zero for ETFs at major brokers)
- **Bid-ask spread costs** when buying/selling
- **Sales loads** (for mutual funds with A/B/C share classes)
- **Transaction fees** charged by brokers for some mutual funds

## Pitfalls

- Low expense ratio does not equal good tracking — a fund can have a 0.05% ratio but poor index replication.
- Comparing a passive ETF's expense ratio to an active fund's ignores the active fund's potential alpha — but the active fund must beat by more than the expense difference after taxes.
- Fund expense ratios are not negotiable; however, institutional and admiral share classes have lower ratios than retail classes for the same fund.
