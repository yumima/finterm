The **PEG ratio (Price/Earnings-to-Growth)** attempts to value a stock by adjusting the P/E ratio for the expected earnings growth rate — in theory, providing a "growth-adjusted" valuation that makes fast-growing and slow-growing companies more comparable.

A P/E of 30 might seem expensive, but if earnings are growing 30% per year, a PEG of 1.0 suggests it's fairly priced. A P/E of 15 with 5% growth (PEG = 3.0) would be more expensive on this basis.

## Formula

```
PEG Ratio = P/E Ratio / Expected EPS Growth Rate (%)

Or equivalently:
PEG = (Price / EPS) / Growth Rate
```

## Worked example

```
Company A: Mature industrial
  P/E: 12×
  Expected EPS growth: 4%/year
  PEG = 12 / 4 = 3.0  (expensive on PEG despite low P/E)

Company B: High-growth software
  P/E: 35×
  Expected EPS growth: 35%/year
  PEG = 35 / 35 = 1.0  (fairly valued on PEG despite high P/E)

Company C: Hypergrowth startup
  P/E: 80×
  Expected EPS growth: 50%/year
  PEG = 80 / 50 = 1.6  (slightly expensive on PEG)
```

## PEG interpretation rules

| PEG Ratio | Signal |
|---|---|
| < 0.5 | Potentially cheap relative to growth |
| 0.5–1.0 | Attractive; common guideline for value in growth |
| ~1.0 | "Fair value" — popularized by Peter Lynch |
| 1.0–2.0 | Moderate premium; growth must deliver |
| > 2.0 | Expensive relative to growth |
| N/M | Negative P/E or negative growth: not meaningful |

Peter Lynch's rule of thumb: a company is attractively valued if its PEG ≤ 1.0.

## Forward PEG vs. trailing PEG

Both P/E variants can be used in PEG:
- **Trailing PEG**: Uses last 12 months EPS + trailing growth rate. More concrete but backward-looking.
- **Forward PEG**: Uses next 12-month EPS estimates + forward growth rate. More useful for valuation.

Most practitioners use a 3–5 year forward EPS CAGR as the denominator.

## Limitations and criticisms

- PEG assumes linear relationship between P/E and growth — in practice, higher growth companies deserve non-linear premium.
- Growth rate estimates are highly uncertain; analyst estimates for 3-5 year growth are notoriously inaccurate.
- PEG breaks down for companies with negative EPS (where P/E is not meaningful).
- Quality of growth matters: 20% growth financed by debt is worth less than 20% organic growth.
- Doesn't account for debt levels, return on capital, or competitive moat.

## PEG vs. other metrics

| Metric | Adjusts for Growth | Adjusts for Debt | Adjusts for Quality |
|---|---|---|---|
| P/E | No | No | No |
| PEG | Yes | No | No |
| EV/EBITDA | No | Yes | No |
| EV/(EBITDA×ROIC) | Partially | Yes | Partially |

## Pitfalls

- Never use PEG without understanding what growth estimate drives the denominator.
- PEG is most useful for comparing companies *within a sector* that have similar capital structures.
- A PEG of 1.0 for a cyclical company using peak earnings is misleading — the P/E expands dramatically in downturns.
