# Earnings Per Share (EPS)

The portion of a company's profit allocated to **each outstanding share** of common stock.

```
Basic EPS   = Net Income / Weighted Avg Shares Outstanding
Diluted EPS = Net Income / (Weighted Avg Shares + dilutive securities)
```

Diluted EPS includes shares that *would exist* if all stock options, warrants, and convertible bonds were exercised. **It is the figure most analysts use** because it captures real ownership dilution.

## Worked example — Apple FY26

```
Net income:                $99.8B
Diluted shares outstanding: 15.85B
Diluted EPS:               99.8 / 15.85  ≈  $6.30
```

If Apple buys back 200M shares (taking diluted shares to 15.65B), the same $99.8B net income produces:

```
EPS:  99.8 / 15.65  ≈  $6.38   (+1.3%)
```

Same business — better EPS, purely from a smaller denominator.

## Why it matters

EPS feeds directly into the most common valuation metric ([[pe-ratio|P/E]]) and is the headline number when companies report earnings. Beating or missing the consensus EPS estimate can move the stock 5–10% in minutes.

## What can distort it

- **Buybacks** reduce share count, mechanically raising EPS without any business improvement.
- **One-time items** (asset sales, write-downs, settlements) can swing reported EPS dramatically. Look at "EPS ex-items" or "adjusted EPS" alongside GAAP.
- **Tax rate changes** flow straight to net income and EPS.
- **Stock-based compensation** dilutes share count; companies "managing dilution" via aggressive buybacks mask the cost.

## Variants worth knowing

- **Basic EPS** — ignores dilution; almost never the right number for valuation
- **Diluted EPS** — standard for analysts and valuation
- **Adjusted / Pro-forma EPS** — non-GAAP; can strip out things that aren't really one-time
- **Operating EPS** — excludes financial items
- **Cash EPS** — net income + D&A per share; bridges to cash generation

## Decision rules

- **Beat by >5% with revenue beat** → strong, often rallies
- **Beat by >5% with revenue miss** → suspicious — likely buyback or cost-cut driven; check sustainability
- **Miss by >5%** → typically drops; magnitude depends on guidance change
- **Persistent gap GAAP vs adjusted >25%** → red flag; understand what's being adjusted
- **Trailing EPS deeply negative + cash burn rising** → solvency check time

## Practical reading

When you see "Q3 EPS $1.20 vs $1.05 estimate," that means the company earned 15¢ more per share than analysts expected. The market reaction depends on *why* — was it from real growth, a tax windfall, or an aggressive buyback? Check the call transcript for guidance and revenue mix before celebrating.
