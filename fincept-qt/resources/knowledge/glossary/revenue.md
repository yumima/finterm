**Revenue** (also called sales or top line) is the total amount of money a company earns from its primary business activities — before any expenses are deducted — representing the starting point of the income statement and the scale of the business.

Revenue is called the "top line" because it sits at the top of the income statement. Every other profitability metric (gross profit, EBITDA, net income) is derived from revenue after subtracting various costs.

## Revenue recognition basics

Revenue is recognized when it is **earned**, not necessarily when cash is received (accrual accounting):

```
SaaS company signs $120,000 annual contract Jan 1:
  Cash received: $120,000 upfront on Jan 1
  Revenue recognized: $10,000/month over 12 months (ASC 606)
  
Q1 financial statements:
  Cash: +$120,000 (received)
  Revenue: +$30,000 (3 months × $10,000) (earned)
  Deferred revenue: $90,000 (liability until earned)
```

## Revenue types

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Subscription / recurring | Fixed periodic payments | SaaS, streaming, insurance |
| Transactional | Per-transaction fee | E-commerce, payments, brokerage |
| Service | Billed by time or project | Consulting, legal, advertising |
| Product | Sale of physical goods | Manufacturing, retail, CPG |
| Licensing | IP rights payments | Software, pharma royalties |
| Interest / investment | Return on financial assets | Banks, BDCs |

## Revenue metrics and analysis

**Year-over-year (YoY) growth**: Comparison to same period last year.

```
Q1 2026 revenue: $5.2B
Q1 2025 revenue: $4.6B
YoY growth: ($5.2B − $4.6B) / $4.6B = 13.0%
```

**Revenue run rate**: Annualizing current quarterly revenue.

```
Q1 revenue $1.3B → Annual run rate: $1.3B × 4 = $5.2B
```

**Constant currency revenue**: Strips out foreign exchange effects.

## Revenue in valuation context

Revenue anchors multiple valuation approaches:

| Metric | Formula | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Price/Sales | Market Cap / Revenue | Unprofitable companies |
| EV/Revenue | EV / Revenue | Cross-company capital-structure neutral |
| Gross margin | Gross Profit / Revenue | Pricing power, product economics |
| EBITDA margin | EBITDA / Revenue | Operating efficiency |
| Net margin | Net Income / Revenue | Bottom-line profitability |

## Revenue quality indicators

High-quality revenue has:
- **Predictability**: Recurring/subscription > transactional.
- **Diversity**: Many customers, no single >10% concentration.
- **Organic growth**: Not from acquisitions that inflate comparisons.
- **Low deferred**: Not inflated by pulling forward future revenue.

Red flags:
- Channel stuffing (shipping excess inventory to distributors).
- Related-party transactions.
- Rapid growth in receivables vs. revenue growth (collections declining).
- Revenue growth outpacing cash collections.

## Pitfalls

- Revenue is easily manipulated: channel stuffing, premature recognition, barter transactions.
- Two companies with identical revenue can have very different economic value based on margin, growth sustainability, and capital requirements.
- Revenue growth alone without profitability roadmap is insufficient — "burning cash to grow revenue" is only valuable if unit economics support eventual profitability.
