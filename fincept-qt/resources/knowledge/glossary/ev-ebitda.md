# EV / EBITDA — The Enterprise Multiple

```
Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Debt − Cash + Minority Interest + Pref Equity
EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA
```

EV/EBITDA tells you **how many years of operating cash earnings the entire business is priced at**, ignoring how it's financed.

## Worked example — Apple, May 2026

```
Market cap:           $2,750B
Total debt:           $109B
Cash & equivalents:   $63B
Enterprise Value:     2,750 + 109 − 63  ≈  $2,796B
TTM EBITDA:           ~$130B
EV/EBITDA:            2,796 / 130  ≈  21.5×
```

A buyer would pay ~21.5 years of current operating cash earnings to acquire Apple outright (debt + equity). For comparison, the S&P 500 trades at ~14× and a typical mature industrial at 8–10×.

## Why it beats P/E for some comparisons

[[pe-ratio|P/E]] is distorted by a company's capital structure: a debt-heavy company has lower equity, so EPS gets divided across fewer shares, inflating P/E. EV/EBITDA strips this out — both the debt is added back into the numerator (EV) *and* interest expense is excluded from the denominator (EBITDA).

This makes it the **standard multiple in M&A and LBO analysis**: it answers "what would a buyer pay for the whole business?"

## Sector-typical ranges

| Sector | Typical EV/EBITDA |
|---|---|
| Utilities | 8–12× |
| Mature industrial | 6–10× |
| Consumer staples | 12–18× |
| Healthcare | 10–18× |
| Mega-cap tech | 15–25× |
| Growth SaaS | 15–40×+ |
| Distressed / cyclical trough | 3–6× |
| Bubble territory | 50×+ |

## Historical context

Median S&P 500 EV/EBITDA: ~10–11× over 30 years. The 2020–2021 average ran at 14–15× as low rates pushed multiples up. M&A premium typically adds 20–35% to standalone EV/EBITDA, so transaction multiples are usually higher than trading multiples.

## Variants worth knowing

- **EV / Forward EBITDA** — standard for forward-looking valuation
- **EV / EBIT** — penalizes capital-heavy businesses (no D&A add-back)
- **EV / Sales** — for unprofitable companies; common in growth software
- **EV / FCF** — most rigorous (uses real cash, not accounting EBITDA)

## Decision rules

- **EV/EBITDA significantly below sector + clean balance sheet** → potential value
- **EV/EBITDA significantly above sector + decelerating revenue** → caution
- **EV/EBITDA stable + EV/FCF rising** → CapEx is climbing; check sustainability
- **EV/EBITDA much lower than P/E** → company is heavily leveraged

## Caveats

- [[ebitda|EBITDA itself can be misleading]] — capital-intensive businesses (telecom, airlines) need a maintenance-CapEx adjustment.
- For banks and insurers, EV/EBITDA is **not used** (their interest expense is operating, not financing). Use P/E or P/B.
- Two companies with identical EV/EBITDA can have very different free-cash-flow conversion. Always cross-check with [[fcf|free cash flow]].
- "Adjusted EBITDA" reported by management often strips out stock-based comp and other recurring costs — use GAAP figures for true comparison.
