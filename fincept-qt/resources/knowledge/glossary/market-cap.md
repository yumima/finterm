# Market Capitalization

```
Market Cap = Share Price × Shares Outstanding
```

The total dollar value of all of a company's shares. The simplest measure of a company's "size" from a stock-market perspective.

## Worked example

```
Apple, May 2026:
  Share price:        $175
  Shares outstanding: 15.85B
  Market cap:         175 × 15.85B  =  $2,773B (≈ $2.77T)

Plus:
  Net debt (debt − cash):  $46B
  Enterprise value:        $2,819B (~$2.82T)
```

The mega-cap is so large that even hundreds of billions of debt or cash barely move the EV needle. For smaller companies the gap matters more.

## Standard size buckets (US-style)

| Bucket | Approx range | Typical traits |
|---|---|---|
| **Mega cap** | > $200B | Apple, Microsoft, MSCI World heavyweights |
| **Large cap** | $10B – $200B | S&P 500 core |
| **Mid cap** | $2B – $10B | S&P 400, growth + value mix |
| **Small cap** | $300M – $2B | Russell 2000, more volatile |
| **Micro cap** | $50M – $300M | Often illiquid, less analyst coverage |
| **Nano cap** | < $50M | Tiny floats, OTC, high risk |

## How size affects investing

| Size | Liquidity | Analyst coverage | Vol (typical) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mega | Excellent | 25–40 analysts | 18–25% | Hard for active managers to beat |
| Large | Excellent | 15–25 | 22–32% | Most efficient market segment |
| Mid | Very good | 8–15 | 28–38% | Sweet spot for some managers |
| Small | Good | 3–8 | 35–50% | More inefficient pricing |
| Micro | Limited | 0–3 | 50–80% | High research edge possible; high illiquidity |
| Nano | Poor | 0 | 80%+ | Fraud risk; pump-and-dump territory |

## What market cap doesn't tell you

Market cap is **what the equity is worth**, not what the *company* is worth. A heavily indebted company can have a small market cap but a huge enterprise — see [[ev-ebitda|Enterprise Value]] for the better whole-business measure.

It also doesn't tell you ownership concentration. A $5B market cap company where the founder holds 80% has only $1B of "float" actually trading — a meaningful difference for institutional buyers.

## Free float

The portion of shares actually available to public trading, excluding insider holdings, government stakes, and locked-up blocks. Index providers use float-adjusted market cap so the index isn't distorted by shares that never trade.

## Variants worth knowing

- **Float-adjusted market cap** — used by S&P 500, MSCI; reflects actually-tradable shares
- **Fully diluted market cap** — includes potential dilution from options, convertibles, RSUs
- **Public float value** — float × price; tradable dollar value
- **Enterprise Value** — market cap + net debt + minority interest + preferred (the "buyout price")

## Decision rules

- **Don't compare leveraged vs unleveraged companies on market cap** alone — use EV
- **Float < $500M** → expect significant slippage; size positions accordingly
- **Insider ownership > 50%** → pay attention to alignment but watch for unilateral decisions
- **Index inclusion thresholds** matter: an addition to S&P 500 mechanically attracts ~7% of float in passive demand
