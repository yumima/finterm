**Net income** (also called net profit, net earnings, or the "bottom line") is what remains of a company's revenue after all expenses — costs of goods sold, operating expenses, interest, depreciation, taxes, and non-recurring charges — have been deducted.

Net income is the formal "profit" reported by a company. It is what drives earnings per share, is the starting point for the cash flow statement, and is what analysts multiply by P/E to determine stock valuations.

## Income statement structure

```
Revenue (top line)
− Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
= Gross Profit

− Operating Expenses (SG&A, R&D)
= EBIT (Operating Income)

− Interest Expense (+ Interest Income)
= Pretax Income (EBT)

− Income Tax Expense
= Net Income (bottom line)

÷ Diluted Shares Outstanding
= EPS (Earnings Per Share)
```

## Worked example

```
Revenue:          $10,000M
COGS:             −$4,000M  (60% gross margin → $6,000M gross profit)
SG&A:             −$2,000M
R&D:              −$1,000M
EBIT:              $3,000M  (30% operating margin)
Interest expense:  −$200M
Pretax:            $2,800M
Tax (21%):         −$588M
Net Income:        $2,212M  (22.1% net margin)

Diluted shares: 1,000M
EPS: $2.212
```

## GAAP vs. non-GAAP ("adjusted") net income

Most large companies report both:

| Measure | Common Adjustments |
|---|---|
| GAAP net income | Includes all items as reported |
| Non-GAAP / "adjusted" | Excludes stock-based comp, impairments, restructuring charges |

```
GAAP net income:          $1,500M
Add back: Stock comp     +$400M
Add back: Restructuring  +$100M
Non-GAAP net income:      $2,000M
Non-GAAP EPS:             $2.00 vs. GAAP EPS $1.50
```

Investors should understand both but watch for habitual "one-time" charges that recur every year — they're not one-time.

## Net income vs. cash flow

Net income and operating cash flow often differ significantly:

```
Net income:          $1,000M
+ D&A:               +$500M   (non-cash charge added back)
− Capex:             −$800M   (cash not in net income)
− Working capital:   −$200M   (cash tied up in operations)
= Free Cash Flow:     $500M
```

Net income can overstate cash generation for capex-heavy businesses.

## Drivers of net income growth

1. **Revenue growth**: Top-line expansion.
2. **Gross margin expansion**: Better pricing or lower input costs.
3. **Operating leverage**: Revenue grows faster than fixed costs.
4. **Tax rate changes**: One-time tax benefits (deferred tax asset reversals).
5. **Share buybacks**: Reduce denominator, grow EPS without growing absolute net income.

## Pitfalls

- Net income is the most manipulated line on the income statement via accruals, estimates (depreciation lives, bad debt reserves), and one-time adjustments.
- Goodwill impairments, restructuring, and M&A charges regularly distort net income; use adjusted figures and verify the adjustments.
- A declining share count from buybacks can grow EPS even when absolute net income is flat or falling.
- Non-GAAP adjustments are not audited; companies have full discretion in what they exclude.
