# Free Cash Flow (FCF)

The cash a business generates from operations after spending what it needs to stay in business.

```
FCF = Cash Flow from Operations − Capital Expenditures
```

This is **what's actually left over** for shareholders, debt repayment, dividends, buybacks, or acquisitions.

## Worked example — Apple FY26

```
Operating cash flow:    $124.0B
Capital expenditures:    $11.5B
Free cash flow:         $112.5B
FCF margin (vs $400B revenue): 28.1%
FCF per share:          $7.10
FCF yield (at $175):    $7.10 / $175  ≈  4.05%
```

A 4% FCF yield is roughly comparable to a 10-year Treasury — reasonable for a mature mega-cap, expensive vs historical norms.

## Why it beats reported earnings

Net income is shaped by accounting choices: depreciation schedules, accruals, deferrals, non-cash charges. Cash either arrives in the bank or it doesn't. A company can report rising EPS while burning cash for years (see: many high-growth IPOs).

## Two flavors

- **FCFF — Free Cash Flow to the Firm**: cash before any debt service. Used in DCF valuation of the entire enterprise.
- **FCFE — Free Cash Flow to Equity**: FCFF after interest and net debt repayment. The cash truly available to equity holders.

## What to watch for

- **CapEx vs. depreciation**: If CapEx is consistently below depreciation, the company is shrinking its asset base. Sometimes intentional, sometimes a red flag.
- **Working capital swings**: A growing business that ties up cash in inventory or receivables can have weak FCF *even when healthy*. Look at multi-year averages.
- **One-time tax refunds or large customer prepayments** can flatter a single quarter.
- **Stock-based compensation**: not in CapEx but is a real cost; subtract SBC for "true" FCF in many tech companies.

## FCF yield benchmarks

| FCF yield | Read |
|---|---|
| < 2% | Expensive (or hyper-growth) |
| 2–4% | Mature growth, fairly priced |
| 4–6% | Reasonable to attractive |
| 6–10% | Cheap if cash flow is durable |
| > 10% | Either deep value or impending FCF collapse |

## Variants worth knowing

- **Levered FCF (LFCF / FCFE)** — after interest payments
- **Unlevered FCF (UFCF / FCFF)** — before financing
- **Owner Earnings** — Buffett's variant: net income + D&A − maintenance CapEx
- **Cash conversion ratio** — FCF / Net Income; >100% is high-quality cash gen

## Decision rules

- **FCF growing faster than revenue** → operating leverage; strong signal
- **FCF flat or declining as revenue grows** → margin compression or working capital drag
- **FCF − dividends − buybacks > 0 sustained** → financial flexibility
- **FCF persistently below dividends paid** → dividend at risk

## When FCF is misleading

- Heavy ramp-up CapEx (data centers, factories) makes near-term FCF look bad even when long-run is great
- M&A-driven growth distorts year-over-year comparisons
- Lumpy customer cash collection in B2B (especially government contracts)

Always look at **3- to 5-year averages** to smooth single-year noise.
