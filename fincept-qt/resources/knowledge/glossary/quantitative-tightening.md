**Quantitative tightening (QT)** is the process by which a central bank shrinks its balance sheet — the reverse of quantitative easing — by allowing maturing bonds to roll off without reinvestment or, more aggressively, by actively selling assets.

QT removes liquidity from the financial system, puts upward pressure on long-term interest rates, and is used alongside rate hikes to tighten financial conditions beyond what short-term rate changes alone achieve.

## Mechanism

```
Bonds mature → Fed does NOT reinvest proceeds → Bank reserves shrink
→ Long-term bond supply effectively increases → Yields rise → Financial conditions tighten
```

Passive QT (runoff only) is the standard approach; active asset sales are rare and more disruptive.

## Fed's QT programs

| Program | Period | Peak Balance | Runoff Cap |
|---|---|---|---|
| QT1 | Oct 2017 – Sep 2019 | $4.5T | $50B/month |
| QT2 | Jun 2022 – present | $9.0T | $95B/month |

QT1 ended when overnight repo markets seized in September 2019, suggesting reserves had fallen too far. The Fed restarted asset purchases to restore market functioning.

## Worked example — QT2 (2022)

```
Balance sheet peak:    $8.97T (Apr 2022)
Monthly runoff cap:    $60B Treasuries + $35B MBS = $95B
Balance sheet (Dec 2024): ~$6.9T
Total reduction:       ~$2.1T over ~2.5 years
```

## QT vs rate hikes

QT is sometimes called "background tightening" because it acts through the term premium and reserve availability rather than directly setting overnight rates. Most estimates suggest $500B–$1T of QT is roughly equivalent to one 25 bps rate hike on financial conditions, though the relationship is uncertain.

## Transmission to markets

- **Term premium rises**: less Fed demand for long-dated bonds means higher yields demanded by private buyers.
- **Bank reserves shrink**: banks hold fewer excess reserves, potentially tightening lending.
- **Mortgage rates**: MBS runoff reduces Fed support for the housing market.
- **Risk assets reprice**: higher long-term discount rates compress equity valuations.

## Pitfalls

- QT has a hard-to-detect floor — when reserves fall "too low," repo markets can seize. The Fed monitors reserve adequacy closely.
- MBS roll off slowly because homeowners prepay less when rates rise; the Fed's MBS balance sheet shrinks slower than Treasuries.
- QT communicates tightening intent but markets focus more on the policy rate path than the pace of runoff.
