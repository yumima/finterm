The **federal funds rate** is the target interest rate set by the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) at which banks lend reserve balances to each other overnight.

It is the most influential short-term interest rate in the U.S. financial system — effectively the price of money — and serves as the starting point for virtually all other interest rates: mortgages, corporate loans, credit cards, and bond yields.

## How it works

Banks are required to hold reserves with the Federal Reserve. Banks with excess reserves lend to banks with shortfalls overnight in the "fed funds market." The FOMC sets a target range (e.g., 5.25%–5.50%) and uses open market operations to keep the effective fed funds rate (EFFR) within that band.

```
FOMC Target Rate → OMO / Interest on Reserves → EFFR stays in range
```

The Fed's primary tools:
- **Interest on Reserve Balances (IORB)** — rate paid to banks on reserves, forming the floor.
- **Overnight Reverse Repo (ON RRP)** — allows money funds to park cash at the Fed, setting the lower bound.
- **Open market operations** — buying/selling Treasuries to adjust reserve supply.

## Worked example — rate hike cycle 2022–2023

```
Start (Mar 2022): 0.00%–0.25%
Peak (Jul 2023):  5.25%–5.50%
Cumulative hike:  +525 bps in 16 months
```

A 30-year mortgage rate moved from ~3.2% to ~7.8% over the same period — roughly 2× the fed funds move due to term premium and credit spread widening.

## Policy stance framework

| Rate vs Neutral | Stance | Typical Context |
|---|---|---|
| Well below neutral | Accommodative | Recession, crisis response |
| Near neutral | Neutral | Stable growth, ~2% inflation |
| Well above neutral | Restrictive | Fighting inflation |

The "neutral rate" (r*) is unobservable but estimated by the Fed at roughly 2.5% nominal (0.5% real + 2% inflation target).

## Transmission to markets

- **Short-term rates** (T-bills, money markets) move nearly 1:1 with Fed funds.
- **Long-term rates** (10yr Treasury) reflect expectations of future Fed funds + term premium.
- **Equities** reprice via the discount rate: higher rates → lower present value of future earnings.
- **USD** typically strengthens when the Fed raises rates faster than other central banks.

## Pitfalls

- The fed funds rate is a target, not a law. The EFFR occasionally drifts a few bps from the target midpoint.
- Rate hikes work with "long and variable lags" (Milton Friedman) — full economic impact takes 12–18 months.
- Cutting rates does not guarantee economic stimulus — banks may not pass lower rates to borrowers if credit conditions tighten.
