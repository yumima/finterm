**Quantitative easing (QE)** is a monetary policy tool in which a central bank purchases large quantities of financial assets — primarily government bonds — to inject money into the financial system and push down long-term interest rates.

QE is used when conventional rate cuts are exhausted (policy rate near zero) or when financial markets need emergency liquidity. The Fed has deployed QE four times: 2008–2014, March 2020, and the COVID expansion through 2022.

## Mechanism

```
Fed buys Treasuries/MBS → Sellers receive cash → Cash flows into risk assets / lending
→ Long-term yields fall → Borrowing costs fall → Economic stimulus
```

When the Fed buys bonds, it creates new bank reserves (not "printing money" in the physical sense). Sellers — typically banks and asset managers — receive cash, which then seeks higher-yielding investments, compressing credit spreads and equity risk premiums.

## Key metrics

- **Balance sheet size** — the Fed's assets grew from $900B (2008) → $4.5T (2015) → $9.0T (2022 peak).
- **Duration removed** — QE's secondary effect is removing long-duration supply from the market, pushing the term premium negative.
- **MBS vs Treasuries** — MBS purchases additionally targeted mortgage rates.

## Worked example — QE1 (2008–2010)

```
Fed purchased: $1.25T MBS + $300B Treasuries + $175B agency debt
30yr mortgage rates: 6.5% (Oct 2008) → 4.7% (Dec 2009)
10yr Treasury:       4.0% → 3.7%
```

## QE vs rate cuts

| Tool | Affects | Transmission | Limits |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rate cuts | Short-term rates | Fast, direct | Zero lower bound |
| QE | Long-term rates | Slower, indirect | Diminishing returns |
| Forward guidance | Rate expectations | Expectations channel | Credibility dependent |

## Criticisms and risks

- Asset price inflation: QE disproportionately benefits holders of financial assets.
- Unwinding (QT) is difficult and can cause market volatility.
- May reduce market discipline ("Fed put") and encourage excessive risk-taking.
- Transmission to the real economy is weaker than rate policy transmission.

## Pitfalls

- QE does not cause hyperinflation automatically — reserves sit in the banking system, not consumer wallets, unless accompanied by fiscal deficits and credit expansion.
- The "wealth effect" from asset price inflation reaches a smaller share of households than policymakers assume.
- QE in one country attracts capital inflows, appreciating the currency — a negative for exporters.
