**Buyback yield** (or repurchase yield) is the annualized rate at which a company reduces its share count through stock buybacks, expressed as a percentage of its market capitalization.

Combined with [[dividend-yield|dividend yield]], it forms the **total shareholder yield** — the total cash returned to shareholders per dollar of market value.

## Formula

```
Buyback Yield = Annual Shares Repurchased × Current Price / Market Cap
             = Annual Buyback Spend ($) / Market Cap

Total Shareholder Yield = Dividend Yield + Buyback Yield
```

## Worked example

```
AAPL (approximate 2024 figures):
Market Cap:       $2,750B
Annual buybacks:  $90B
Annual dividends: $15B

Buyback yield:          $90B / $2,750B = 3.3%
Dividend yield:         $15B / $2,750B = 0.5%
Total shareholder yield: 3.3% + 0.5%  = 3.8%
```

Apple returned ~$105B to shareholders in a year, at a combined yield of 3.8% — a meaningful return despite the low advertised dividend yield.

## Why buybacks often dominate dividends

- **Tax efficiency**: Capital gains (if you sell into the buyback) may be taxed lower than dividend income.
- **Flexibility**: Buybacks can be suspended without the stigma of a dividend cut.
- **EPS accretion**: Fewer shares increase EPS mechanically, supporting share price.

## Buyback quality — the accretion test

Not all buybacks create value. A buyback at a price above intrinsic value destroys value:

```
Value-creating buyback: Company buys at $50, intrinsic value = $70 → accretive
Value-destroying buyback: Company buys at $70, intrinsic value = $50 → dilutive
```

Warren Buffett's benchmark: "Buybacks are sensible when shares trade significantly below conservative intrinsic value."

## Buyback yield screening

Buyback yield is useful as a valuation screen:

| Combined Yield | Context |
|---|---|
| < 1% | Low return; likely growth-oriented |
| 1–3% | Moderate capital return |
| 3–6% | Strong; value-oriented or mature company |
| > 6% | Very high; check if sustainable (leverage?) |

High buyback yields combined with rising debt can signal financial engineering rather than genuine cash generation.

## Pitfalls

- Buybacks financed by debt add leverage risk; the yield is not truly "free" if funded by borrowing.
- Companies often buy most aggressively near market tops when they have cash — the worst time to repurchase.
- Dilutive stock-based compensation can offset buybacks entirely: net buyback yield = gross buyback − SBC issuance.
- Gross buyback yield overstates shareholder return if options/RSU issuance is large (common in tech).
