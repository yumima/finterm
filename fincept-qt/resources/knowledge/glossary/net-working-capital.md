**Net working capital (NWC)** is the difference between a company's current assets (primarily receivables and inventory) and current liabilities (primarily payables), representing the short-term operational liquidity cushion.

NWC is a measure of a company's operational efficiency and short-term financial health. Changes in NWC directly affect cash generation — it is a critical bridge between reported earnings and actual free cash flow.

## Formula

```
Net Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities

Operating NWC (more precise for cash flow analysis):
  = (Accounts Receivable + Inventory) − Accounts Payable
  (excludes cash and short-term debt which are financing items)
```

## Worked example

```
Company balance sheet:
  Accounts receivable: $120M
  Inventory:           $80M
  Prepaid expenses:    $15M
  Cash:                $50M
  Total current assets: $265M

  Accounts payable:    $90M
  Accrued liabilities: $40M
  Current portion LTD: $20M
  Total current liab.: $150M

Reported NWC = $265M − $150M = $115M
Operating NWC = ($120M + $80M) − $90M = $110M (excludes cash and current debt)
```

## NWC and free cash flow

The **change in NWC** is subtracted from operating profit to calculate FCF:

```
FCF = EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate) + D&A − Capex − ΔNWC

Growing NWC = cash outflow (tied up in operations)
Shrinking NWC = cash inflow (releasing working capital)
```

A company growing rapidly often requires increasing NWC, which can make it cash-flow negative even when profitable.

## NWC efficiency metrics

| Metric | Formula | Lower = Better (usually) |
|---|---|---|
| Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) | AR / (Revenue/365) | Faster collections |
| Days Inventory Outstanding (DIO) | Inventory / (COGS/365) | Less inventory held |
| Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) | AP / (COGS/365) | Slower payments (extend suppliers) |
| Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC) | DSO + DIO − DPO | Days cash is tied up in operations |

```
Amazon's CCC is historically negative:
  DSO ≈ 10 days (instant credit card payments)
  DIO ≈ 40 days
  DPO ≈ 75 days
  CCC = 10 + 40 − 75 = −25 days (suppliers fund Amazon's growth)
```

## NWC peg in M&A transactions

In M&A deals, a "working capital peg" defines the expected level of NWC at closing. The seller must deliver the business with NWC at the agreed target:
- NWC above peg → buyer pays more.
- NWC below peg → purchase price is reduced.

This prevents sellers from draining receivables or building payables just before closing.

## Pitfalls

- NWC includes non-cash items; don't equate positive NWC with having cash.
- Companies with negative cash conversion cycles (like retailers with fast inventory turns and slow payables) can be very cash efficient despite modest NWC.
- Increasing NWC with revenue growth is expected and normal — only unexpected increases signal inefficiency or revenue recognition issues.
- Watch for NWC manipulation: aggressively extending payables or accelerating collections before quarter-end inflates reported NWC.
