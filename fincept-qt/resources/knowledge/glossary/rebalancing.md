**Rebalancing** is the process of realigning a portfolio's actual asset allocation back to its target allocation — by selling assets that have grown above target weight and buying those that have fallen below.

Rebalancing enforces systematic discipline: it forces investors to sell high (trim outperformers) and buy low (add to underperformers) — a counterintuitive but mechanically sound approach to maintaining intended risk exposure.

## Why portfolios drift

```
Target portfolio:  60% equities / 40% bonds

After a strong equity bull market:
  Stocks: +30%, now $78,000 (from $60,000)
  Bonds:  +3%, now $41,200 (from $40,000)
  Total:  $119,200

Actual allocation:
  Equities: $78,000 / $119,200 = 65.4%
  Bonds:    $41,200 / $119,200 = 34.6%
  → Portfolio is 5.4% too heavy in stocks → higher risk than intended
```

## Rebalancing execution

```
To restore 60/40:
  Target equities: $119,200 × 60% = $71,520
  Current equities: $78,000
  Sell: $78,000 − $71,520 = $6,480 of equities

  Buy: $6,480 of bonds
  Result: $71,520 equities / $47,680 bonds = 60.0% / 40.0%
```

## Rebalancing methods

| Method | Trigger | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| Calendar (monthly/quarterly/annual) | Fixed schedule | Simple, predictable | Ignores market conditions |
| Threshold | Drift > 5% or 10% | Responds to reality | Requires monitoring |
| Combined | Calendar + threshold | Best of both | Slightly more complex |

Most research finds **annual rebalancing** is nearly as effective as more frequent rebalancing in long-term portfolios, with lower transaction costs.

## Rebalancing bonus — myth vs. reality

The "rebalancing bonus" (idea that rebalancing generates extra return) is real in some conditions but not universal:
- Rebalancing benefits from assets that mean-revert (range-bound markets, negatively correlated assets).
- In trending markets, rebalancing costs return by trimming winners and buying laggards.
- The main benefit is **risk reduction** (keeping allocation at target), not consistently higher returns.

## Tax considerations

In taxable accounts, selling winners creates capital gains. Strategies to minimize tax drag:
1. **Use new contributions**: Direct new money to underweight assets instead of selling.
2. **Rebalance in tax-advantaged accounts**: Use IRA/401k where no tax is triggered.
3. **Tax-loss harvesting**: Offset gains from winners by selling losers (if available).
4. **Dividend reinvestment directed to underweight**: Some brokers allow targeted reinvestment.

## Behavioral rebalancing benefit

Beyond mechanics, rebalancing provides discipline:
- Forces selling overweight assets at their peak (sells fear of missing out).
- Forces buying underweight assets after declines (buys through fear).
- Keeps portfolio risk at a designed level — prevents "accidental concentration."

## Pitfalls

- Over-rebalancing (monthly in trending markets) generates unnecessary tax and transaction costs.
- Rigid rebalancing to an inappropriate target allocation is worse than having the right allocation in the first place.
- Rebalancing across highly correlated assets (e.g., U.S. large cap and U.S. mid cap at 50/50) provides minimal diversification benefit.
