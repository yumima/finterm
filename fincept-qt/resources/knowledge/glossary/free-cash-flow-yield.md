**Free cash flow yield (FCF yield)** is the ratio of a company's free cash flow to its market capitalization — the inverse of the price-to-FCF multiple, expressing what percentage of the stock's price is earned as actual cash profit.

FCF yield is often considered more reliable than earnings yield because free cash flow is harder to manipulate than reported earnings and represents cash that management can actually deploy.

## Formula

```
FCF Yield = Free Cash Flow / Market Capitalization × 100

Or equivalently:
FCF Yield = 1 / Price-to-FCF ratio
```

## Worked example

```
MSFT (approximate):
Free Cash Flow (TTM): $68B
Market Cap: $3,100B

FCF Yield = $68B / $3,100B = 2.2%

AAPL:
FCF (TTM): $101B
Market Cap: $2,750B
FCF Yield = $101B / $2,750B = 3.7%

S&P 500 (approximate):
Aggregate FCF Yield: ~3.5–4.0%
10yr Treasury Yield: ~4.5%
```

A FCF yield below the risk-free rate means investors are paying a premium for growth expectations.

## Comparison to alternative yield measures

| Metric | Numerator | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| Earnings yield | Net income | Common, easy to find | Accruals, non-cash items |
| FCF yield | Free cash flow | Cash-based, harder to manipulate | Capex-intensive co's show low FCF |
| EBITDA yield | EBITDA | Pre-leverage, comparable across cap structures | Ignores capex and taxes |
| Buyback yield | Buyback spend | Shows actual return activity | Company-decision dependent |

## FCF yield vs. bond yield

When FCF yield approaches or falls below bond yields, the equity risk premium compresses — stocks look expensive relative to bonds:

```
FCF yield 2% vs. 10yr Treasury 4.5%
→ Equity offers less "yield" than risk-free bonds
→ Stocks need significant growth to justify valuation

FCF yield 5% vs. 10yr Treasury 4.5%
→ Equity offers 50 bps premium for risk taken
→ More reasonable risk-reward
```

## Enterprise FCF yield (EV-based)

For capital structure-agnostic comparison, use:

```
EBITDA yield = EBITDA / Enterprise Value
FCF to firm yield = FCFF / Enterprise Value
```

This removes leverage effects, allowing comparison across companies with different debt levels.

## Pitfalls

- FCF is volatile year-to-year due to capex lumping — use 3-year average FCF for more stable yield calculation.
- For capital-intensive businesses (utilities, telecoms, airlines), low FCF yield is structural, not necessarily expensive.
- For asset-light businesses (software, consulting), FCF yield understates value generation because low capex inflates FCF vs. peers.
- FCF yield doesn't distinguish between high FCF from shrinking capex (concerning) vs. operational improvement (genuine).
