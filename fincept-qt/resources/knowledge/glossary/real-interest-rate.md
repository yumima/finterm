The **real interest rate** is the nominal interest rate adjusted for inflation — the return on capital after accounting for the erosion of purchasing power.

It answers the practical question: after inflation, how much more can you actually buy with your money next year?

## Formula

**Approximate (Fisher equation):**

```
Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate
```

**Exact (Fisher equation):**

```
Real Rate = (1 + Nominal Rate) / (1 + Inflation Rate) − 1
```

The approximation works well when rates are below ~10%. Above that, use the exact formula.

## Worked example

```
Nominal 10yr Treasury yield:  4.50%
CPI inflation rate:           3.00%

Approximate real rate: 4.50% − 3.00% = 1.50%
Exact real rate: (1.045) / (1.030) − 1 = 1.46%
```

A bond investor earns 4.50% nominally, but only 1.46–1.50% in real (inflation-adjusted) purchasing power.

## Negative real rates

When inflation exceeds the nominal rate, the real rate is negative — money lenders effectively subsidize borrowers.

```
2020–2021 scenario:
Nominal 10yr yield:    0.70%
CPI inflation:         5.40%
Real rate:            −4.70%
```

Negative real rates are historically unusual outside wartime and crisis periods. They:
- Incentivize borrowing and risk-taking.
- Punish savers and holders of fixed income.
- Typically support gold, TIPS, real assets, and equities.

## Real rates and equity valuation

The real risk-free rate is the foundation of the equity discount rate:

```
Discount Rate = Real Risk-Free Rate + Inflation + Equity Risk Premium
```

Rising real rates directly compress equity valuations by raising the discount rate applied to future earnings.

| Real Rate Level | Equity Implication |
|---|---|
| Deeply negative (< −2%) | Strong multiple expansion support |
| Near zero (−1% to +1%) | Moderate support |
| +1% to +2% | Neutral to modest headwind |
| > +2% | Meaningful valuation compression |

## Market-based vs. ex-post

- **Ex-ante real rate**: nominal rate minus *expected* future inflation (forward-looking; TIPS yield is the closest proxy).
- **Ex-post real rate**: nominal rate minus *actual* inflation (backward-looking; what investors actually earned).

The TIPS yield is the best real-time measure of the ex-ante 10-year real interest rate.

## Pitfalls

- Using headline CPI vs core PCE gives different real rate readings; the Fed uses PCE.
- Real rates can be negative for extended periods without triggering immediate inflation — the relationship has long lags.
- International real rate differentials drive carry trades and currency flows, not just domestic investment decisions.
