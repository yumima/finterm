A **benchmark** is a standard index or reference portfolio used to evaluate the performance of an investment portfolio, strategy, or fund — the baseline against which returns and risk are measured.

Every investment decision implies a benchmark comparison. Choosing the right benchmark is one of the most important — and often most overlooked — aspects of performance evaluation.

## Why benchmarks matter

Benchmarks provide the reference point for:
1. **Alpha calculation**: Portfolio return − Benchmark return.
2. **Risk assessment**: Tracking error, information ratio.
3. **Manager evaluation**: Is the manager adding value for the fees charged?
4. **Asset allocation**: Does the portfolio match intended exposures?

```
Fund return: +14%
Benchmark (S&P 500) return: +16%
Alpha: −2% (underperformance)
Before fees, the fund would have been better as an index fund
```

## Benchmark selection criteria

A good benchmark is:
- **Investable**: Can be passively replicated.
- **Measurable**: Prices published regularly.
- **Appropriate**: Matches the portfolio's investment universe and risk profile.
- **Pre-specified**: Agreed upon before the measurement period.

## Benchmark gaming and "closet indexing"

Managers under pressure to beat benchmarks may:
- **Hug the index**: Hold most of the benchmark weights with small deviations → low tracking error, low chance of large underperformance, low chance of meaningful outperformance.
- **Game the benchmark**: Take risk in off-benchmark securities not reflected in the benchmark.

"Active share" measures how different a portfolio is from its benchmark. Low active share (< 60%) indicates closet indexing.

## Personal benchmarks

For individual investors, appropriate benchmarks depend on goals:
- Retiree drawing down: inflation + 3% may be more relevant than S&P 500.
- Endowment: Inflation + 5% (spending rate + real growth).
- Tactical trader: Risk-free rate (are you beating just holding cash?).

## Peer group benchmarks

Institutional funds also benchmark against peer groups (top quartile of similar funds). This creates benchmark drift — the peer group's behavior becomes self-referential.

## Pitfalls

- Using an inappropriate benchmark: comparing a large-cap blend fund to the Russell 2000 makes performance look spectacular or terrible based on market cap cycle, not skill.
- Changing benchmarks retroactively to make performance look better is a red flag.
- Survivorship bias in peer benchmarks: failed funds leave the peer group, making historical peer comparisons too flattering.
- "Beating the benchmark" on a risk-adjusted basis (information ratio) is more meaningful than raw excess return.
