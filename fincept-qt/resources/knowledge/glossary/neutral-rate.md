The **neutral rate** (also called r-star or r*) is the theoretical short-term interest rate that neither stimulates nor restrains economic growth — the rate consistent with full employment and stable inflation in equilibrium.

It is unobservable. No market prints "r*." Policymakers must estimate it from models, and disagreements about its level drive fundamental disagreements about how tight or loose monetary policy actually is.

## Conceptual framework

```
Real Policy Rate (= Nominal Rate − Inflation) vs r*

Real Rate > r*  → Restrictive policy  → Economy slows
Real Rate < r*  → Accommodative       → Economy stimulated
Real Rate ≈ r*  → Neutral             → Economy at trend
```

## Worked example — 2023

```
Fed Funds Rate:         5.25%–5.50%
Fed's r* estimate:      0.5% real
Inflation (PCE):        ~3.5%
Estimated neutral rate: 0.5% + 3.5% = 4.0% nominal

Real rate: 5.375% − 3.5% = 1.875% real
Policy stance: ~1.375% above estimated r* → restrictive
```

## Why r* matters so much

If r* has risen structurally (due to deglobalization, fiscal deficits, AI investment demand), then:
- A 5% Fed Funds rate may be only mildly restrictive, not very tight.
- The Fed will need to cut less aggressively to "neutral."
- Long-term bonds should price higher yields structurally.

Conversely, if r* is still near 0% (secular stagnation thesis), then 5% rates are extremely tight.

## Estimates and disagreement

| Source | Nominal Neutral Estimate |
|---|---|
| FOMC median dot plot | ~2.5–3.0% |
| Laubach-Williams model | 0.7–1.5% real (~2.7–3.5% nominal) |
| Market-implied (OIS forward) | Dynamic; changes with every meeting |
| Academia (Holston-Laubach-Williams) | ~1.0% real |

## Why r* shifted

Factors pushing r* lower (secular stagnation):
- Aging demographics reduce investment demand.
- Global savings glut.
- Lower trend productivity growth.

Factors pushing r* higher (2020s debate):
- Large fiscal deficits crowding out private capital.
- AI-driven capex boom.
- Deglobalization increasing capital needs domestically.
- Green energy transition investment.

## Pitfalls

- Policymakers can be wrong about r* for years, setting policy too tight or loose without knowing it.
- r* is estimated with wide confidence intervals; the range is often ±1–2%.
- "Real-time" estimates of r* are revised significantly after the fact, making contemporaneous policy decisions harder.
