The **debt-to-equity ratio (D/E)** measures a company's financial leverage by comparing total debt (liabilities) to total shareholder equity — revealing how much of the company is financed by debt versus owner funds.

A higher D/E ratio means more leverage, which amplifies returns in good times and losses in bad times. It is a primary measure of financial risk and capital structure.

## Formula

```
D/E Ratio = Total Debt / Total Shareholders' Equity

Or more precisely (excluding non-debt liabilities):
Financial D/E = (Short-term debt + Long-term debt) / Total Equity
```

## Worked example

```
Balance sheet:
  Short-term debt:      $200M
  Long-term debt:     $1,800M
  Total debt:         $2,000M
  Total equity:       $1,000M

D/E Ratio = $2,000M / $1,000M = 2.0×
(Company is financed 2/3 by debt, 1/3 by equity)
```

## D/E by sector

D/E ratios vary dramatically by industry due to different capital intensity and cash flow stability:

| Sector | Typical D/E | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Utilities | 1.5–3.0× | Regulated, stable cash flows support leverage |
| Airlines | 2.0–5.0× | High fixed assets (planes), cyclical revenue |
| Banks | 8–15× | Leverage is the business model; different context |
| Real estate (REIT) | 1.0–2.0× | Property as collateral |
| Technology | 0.1–0.5× | Asset-light; strong cash flow, low need for debt |
| Healthcare | 0.3–1.0× | Mixed capital needs |
| Consumer discretionary | 0.5–2.0× | Wide range by sub-sector |
| Energy (integrated) | 0.3–0.7× | Commodity cycles demand conservative leverage |

## D/E and return on equity (ROE)

The DuPont formula shows how leverage amplifies ROE:

```
ROE = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Financial Leverage

Financial Leverage = Assets / Equity = 1 + D/E
```

A company with 15% ROA and 2× D/E ratio has ROE boosted to higher level — at the cost of risk.

## Interest coverage — the crucial complement

D/E alone doesn't show ability to service debt:

```
EBIT (earnings before interest/taxes): $300M
Interest expense:                       $200M
Interest coverage ratio:                1.5×  (minimal; risky)

Compare:
Same D/E ratio, EBIT = $1,000M
Interest coverage = 5.0× (very comfortable)
```

A company with high D/E but strong cash flow (utilities, telecoms) is safer than a cyclical company with moderate D/E and volatile earnings.

## Pitfalls

- Book equity is often very different from market equity (market cap); the book D/E ratio can be misleading for companies trading far above book value.
- D/E doesn't distinguish between high-interest and low-interest debt — a $1B loan at 2% is very different from $1B at 10%.
- Goodwill from acquisitions inflates the equity denominator, making D/E look lower than economic leverage.
- Off-balance-sheet liabilities (operating leases, pension obligations) are not always in D/E; IFRS 16 helped but legacy analysis may miss these.
