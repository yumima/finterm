A **carry trade** is a strategy of borrowing in a low-interest-rate currency (or asset) and investing in a higher-interest-rate currency (or asset), earning the interest rate differential (the "carry") as profit.

Carry trades work when exchange rates remain stable or move favorably. They can unwind violently when the funding currency appreciates or when risk sentiment deteriorates globally.

## FX carry trade mechanics

```
Japanese Yen carry trade (classic):
  1. Borrow JPY at 0.1% annual interest (Japan's near-zero rates)
  2. Convert JPY to USD
  3. Invest in U.S. Treasury bonds at 4.5% annual yield
  4. Net carry = 4.5% − 0.1% = 4.4% annually

Risk: If JPY appreciates 5% vs USD, you lose 5% on FX while earning 4.4% carry
     → Net loss: −0.6%
```

## Common carry trade pairs

| Funding (low-yield) | Investment (high-yield) | Historical carry |
|---|---|---|
| JPY (yen) | USD, AUD, NZD | 3–5% |
| CHF (Swiss franc) | Emerging market FX | 4–8% |
| EUR (low-rate periods) | USD, EM currencies | 2–4% |
| USD (low-rate periods) | BRL, TRY, MXN | 8–15% |

## Fixed income carry

Carry in fixed income can also mean:

```
Buy a 10yr bond yielding 4.5%
Finance it by borrowing overnight at 5.0% Fed Funds rate → negative carry

Buy a high-yield bond at 8%
Finance it overnight at 5.0% → carry = 3% (positive carry)
```

A positively sloped yield curve creates positive carry for long-duration bond holders; an inverted yield curve creates negative carry.

## The carry trade risk premium

Carry returns are often described as "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller":

```
Typical carry trade return profile:
  Year 1: +4.2% carry
  Year 2: +3.8% carry
  Year 3: +4.1% carry
  Year 4 (risk-off event): −25% as carry unwinds globally
  Net 4-year return: roughly −13%
```

Carry trades are positively skewed in frequency (win most months) but negatively skewed in magnitude (occasional catastrophic losses). The famous "carry crash" of 2008 and August 2015 (yen appreciation) illustrate this.

## Why carry trades unwind violently

When risk sentiment deteriorates:
1. Investors sell high-yield currencies and assets.
2. They repurchase funding currencies (JPY, CHF) to repay loans.
3. Funding currency appreciation creates losses for all carry traders simultaneously.
4. This forces more selling → cascading unwind.

August 2024 yen carry unwind: BOJ rate hike → JPY surged → global risk assets sold off 5–10% within days.

## Carry vs. momentum vs. value

Carry, momentum, and value are three of the most widely documented return premia in asset markets:

- **Carry** rewards patience; punishes crisis periods.
- **Momentum** rewards trend-following; punishes reversals.
- **Value** rewards fundamental discipline; punishes growth/trend markets.

Professional systematic investors combine all three for diversification.

## Pitfalls

- Carry trades are highly correlated with each other and with broad risk sentiment — in crises, everything carries off simultaneously.
- Emerging market carry (buying EM currencies) adds political risk, convertibility risk, and liquidity risk on top of interest rate differential.
- Leverage amplifies carry trade returns and disasters proportionally.
