**Earnings yield** is the ratio of a company's earnings per share to its stock price — the inverse of the P/E ratio — expressing what percentage of the stock's price is earned as profit.

Earnings yield makes equity valuations directly comparable to bond yields, enabling the classic equity-versus-bond decision framework.

## Formula

```
Earnings Yield = EPS / Price × 100
              = 1 / P/E Ratio × 100
```

## Worked example

```
Stock: P/E = 20×
Earnings Yield = 1/20 = 5.0%

If 10-year Treasury yields 4.5%:
Equity risk premium = 5.0% − 4.5% = 0.5%

Stock: P/E = 25×
Earnings Yield = 1/25 = 4.0%
Equity risk premium = 4.0% − 4.5% = −0.5%
(stocks yield less than bonds → expensive relative to bonds)
```

## The Fed Model

The "Fed Model" (attributed to Alan Greenspan) compares the S&P 500 earnings yield to the 10-year Treasury yield:

```
When Earnings Yield > Treasury Yield → Equities cheap vs bonds
When Earnings Yield < Treasury Yield → Equities expensive vs bonds
```

This model was influential in the 1980s–90s. Criticism: inflation affects bonds and equities differently, so the nominal comparison is misleading.

## Earnings yield by market context

| S&P 500 P/E | Earnings Yield | vs. 4.5% Treasury |
|---|---|---|
| 15× | 6.7% | +2.2% premium (attractive) |
| 20× | 5.0% | +0.5% premium (fair) |
| 25× | 4.0% | −0.5% discount (expensive) |
| 30× | 3.3% | −1.2% discount (very expensive) |

## Cyclically adjusted earnings yield (CAPE yield)

The Shiller CAPE divides inflation-adjusted price by 10-year average inflation-adjusted earnings:

```
CAPE Yield = 1 / CAPE Ratio

If CAPE = 35: CAPE yield = 2.86%
If CAPE = 20: CAPE yield = 5.0%
```

CAPE yield is less distorted by earnings cycles. Historically, low CAPE yields have predicted poor long-term returns.

## Earnings yield vs. FCF yield

Earnings yield uses accounting earnings; [[free-cash-flow-yield|FCF yield]] uses actual cash generated. For capital-light businesses they're similar. For capital-intensive businesses, FCF yield is lower than earnings yield (capex exceeds depreciation), making earnings yield deceptively optimistic.

## Pitfalls

- Using trailing P/E (and thus trailing earnings yield) at cycle peaks overstates earnings — the yield looks better than it really is on a normalized basis.
- Negative earnings make earnings yield negative and meaningless.
- The earnings yield vs. bond yield comparison assumes inflation is fairly priced — in low-inflation regimes this works; in high-inflation it breaks down because equities have real-return characteristics bonds don't.
