The **coupon rate** is the annual interest rate stated on a bond — expressed as a percentage of the bond's face value (par value) — that determines the fixed dollar payments made to bondholders until maturity.

The term "coupon" originates from historical paper bonds that had detachable coupons that holders clipped and redeemed for interest payments.

## Formula

```
Annual Coupon Payment = Coupon Rate × Par Value

Semiannual payment (standard for U.S. bonds):
Coupon Payment = (Coupon Rate / 2) × Par Value
```

## Worked example

```
Bond: 10-year Treasury, $1,000 par, 4.5% coupon
Annual coupon: 4.5% × $1,000 = $45.00/year
Semiannual coupon: $22.50 every 6 months
Total coupons over life: 20 payments × $22.50 = $450

Plus: $1,000 principal at maturity
Total cash received: $1,450
```

## Coupon rate vs. current yield vs. YTM

These three related but different measures of bond return are frequently confused:

| Measure | Formula | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| **Coupon rate** | Fixed; stated at issuance | Annual % of par |
| **Current yield** | Annual coupon / Current price | Income return at today's price |
| **YTM** | IRR of all cash flows | Total return if held to maturity |

```
Bond: 4.5% coupon, $1,000 par
Current price: $950 (trading at a discount)

Coupon rate:    4.5%
Current yield:  $45 / $950 = 4.74%
YTM:            ~5.0%  (includes capital gain from $950 to $1,000)
```

When a bond trades below par: Coupon rate < Current yield < YTM.
When a bond trades above par: Coupon rate > Current yield > YTM.

## Types of coupon structures

| Type | Description | Common Use |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed rate | Same coupon every period | Most bonds |
| Floating rate | SOFR + spread; resets periodically | CLOs, FRNs |
| Step-up | Coupon increases over time | Corporate with call protection |
| PIK (Payment-in-Kind) | Interest paid in additional bonds | LBO mezzanine debt |
| Zero coupon | No periodic payments; issued at discount | Treasuries (STRIPS), munis |

## Effect of interest rates on coupon bonds

When market interest rates rise above the coupon rate, the bond must fall in price to become competitive:

```
4.5% coupon bond → market rates rise to 6%
Bond price falls until YTM = 6%
New price ≈ $878 (for 10-year maturity)

Percentage price drop: (878−1000)/1000 = −12.2%
```

Longer maturity bonds experience larger price changes for the same rate move — this is [[duration|duration]] risk.

## Pitfalls

- Coupon rate ≠ yield — a high coupon rate bond trading at a premium may offer a lower YTM than a low coupon rate bond trading at a discount.
- For callable bonds, the relevant yield is yield-to-call (YTC), not YTM — if the issuer calls the bond early, the investor loses future coupon payments.
- Zero coupon bonds have no reinvestment risk (no coupons to reinvest) but maximum duration risk.
