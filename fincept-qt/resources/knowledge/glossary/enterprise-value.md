**Enterprise value (EV)** is the total value of a business — equity plus debt minus cash — representing what an acquirer would actually pay to own the entire company free and clear.

Market capitalization measures only equity value. Enterprise value adds all claims on the business (debt, preferred equity, minority interests) and subtracts the cash an acquirer would receive, giving a capital-structure-neutral measure of total firm value.

## Formula

```
Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Total Debt + Minority Interest + Preferred Equity − Cash

Simplified:
EV = Market Cap + Net Debt

Where:
Net Debt = Total Debt − Cash & Equivalents
```

## Worked example

```
Company:
  Share price: $50
  Shares outstanding: 200M
  Market cap: $10,000M

  Total debt: $3,000M
  Cash:       $500M
  Net debt:   $2,500M
  Preferred:  $200M

Enterprise Value = $10,000M + $3,000M + $200M − $500M = $12,700M
```

A buyer paying $50/share (total $10B) still must service $3B in debt and gets $500M cash = $12.7B total cost.

## Why EV matters for valuation multiples

EV-based multiples remove capital structure effects, making companies with very different debt loads comparable:

| Multiple | Numerator | Denominator |
|---|---|---|
| EV/EBITDA | Enterprise Value | EBITDA |
| EV/Revenue | Enterprise Value | Revenue |
| EV/EBIT | Enterprise Value | EBIT |
| EV/FCF | Enterprise Value | Free Cash Flow to Firm |

Two identical businesses — one financed all-equity, one with 50% debt — will have the same EV/EBITDA but very different P/E ratios. EV multiples capture the true operational value.

## Cash-rich vs debt-heavy EV comparison

```
Company A (cash-rich):
  Market cap: $10B
  Cash: $3B, Debt: $0
  EV: $7B

Company B (debt-laden):
  Market cap: $10B
  Cash: $0, Debt: $3B
  EV: $13B
```

Same market cap, but Company B costs nearly 2× as much to acquire. P/E would show them as equally valued; EV shows the true difference.

## Adjustments for unusual items

Full EV calculation may also include:
- **Operating leases**: Post-IFRS 16/ASC 842, leases are capitalized on the balance sheet.
- **Underfunded pension liabilities**: Represents a debt-like obligation.
- **Earn-out provisions**: Contingent future payments in M&A.
- **Options and warrants**: Diluted share count for fully-diluted equity value.

## Pitfalls

- Comparing EV/EBITDA across sectors without adjusting for capex intensity is misleading (capital-light vs heavy industries).
- EV can be distorted by large amounts of "stranded" cash (in overseas subsidiaries, restricted) that isn't truly accessible.
- In negative-enterprise-value situations (EV < 0): company has more cash than market cap + debt — often a deep-value signal or a red flag.
- Minority interest is often forgotten; omitting it understates EV for companies with significant non-wholly-owned subsidiaries.
