**EV/EBITDA** measures enterprise value as a multiple of operating earnings before non-cash and financing charges — it is the preferred valuation multiple for comparing companies across capital structures.

```
Enterprise Value (EV):
EV = Market Cap + Total Debt − Cash & Cash Equivalents
   + Minority Interest + Preferred Stock

EV/EBITDA = EV / EBITDA

EBITDA = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization
       = Operating Income + D&A
       = Net Income + Interest + Taxes + D&A
```

## Worked example — comparing two industrial companies

```
                    Company A   Company B
Share price         $40.00      $60.00
Shares out (mn)     500         200
Market cap          $20,000 mn  $12,000 mn
Total debt          $8,000 mn   $1,000 mn
Cash                $1,500 mn   $3,000 mn

EV = 20,000 + 8,000 − 1,500  EV = 12,000 + 1,000 − 3,000
   = $26,500 mn                  = $10,000 mn

EBITDA              $2,500 mn   $1,200 mn

EV/EBITDA           26,500/2,500 = 10.6×   10,000/1,200 = 8.3×
P/E                 22×          14×
```

On a P/E basis, Company A looks 57 % more expensive. On EV/EBITDA, the premium narrows to 28 % — because Company A carries more debt that inflates its apparent earnings yield disadvantage on a per-share basis. EV/EBITDA removes the distortion.

## What the result means

Typical EV/EBITDA ranges by sector (2025–2026):

| Sector              | Typical range |
|---|---|
| Technology (mature) | 15 – 25×      |
| Healthcare          | 12 – 20×      |
| Consumer staples    | 10 – 15×      |
| Industrials         | 8 – 12×       |
| Energy              | 4 – 8×        |
| Telecom             | 5 – 8×        |
| Utilities           | 8 – 12×       |

## Variants

- **EV/EBIT** — excludes depreciation; better for asset-light businesses where D&A is minimal.
- **EV/EBITA** — adds back amortization only; common for companies with large acquisition-related intangible amortization.
- **EV/Revenue (EV/Sales)** — used when EBITDA is negative (early-stage, pre-profitability).
- **EV/EBITDA − CapEx (EV/EBITDA-capex)** — better for capital-intensive businesses; approximates free cash flow.

## Common mistakes

- Treating EBITDA as a cash flow proxy for capex-heavy businesses — a pipeline or manufacturing company with $500 mn EBITDA and $450 mn capex has almost no free cash flow; EV/EBITDA will look misleadingly cheap.
- Not using market-value debt — if bonds trade at 70 cents on the dollar, using face value overstates debt in EV.
- Including operating lease debt (post-IFRS 16 / ASC 842) but not adjusting EBITDA — add back lease payments to EBITDA to stay consistent.
- Comparing across GAAP and IFRS reporters without adjustment — D&A treatment and lease capitalization differ.
