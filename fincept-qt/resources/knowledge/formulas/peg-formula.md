**The PEG ratio** adjusts the [[pe-formula|P/E]] for the company's expected earnings growth rate, providing a growth-normalized valuation — the idea is that a high-P/E stock may be cheap if it grows fast enough.

```
PEG = P/E / Expected EPS Growth Rate (%)

Forward PEG = Forward P/E / Long-term EPS Growth Rate
Trailing PEG = Trailing P/E / TTM EPS Growth Rate
```

Both numerator and denominator should use the same EPS basis (GAAP or adjusted). Growth rate is typically expressed as a percentage (e.g., 15, not 0.15).

## Worked example — two growth stocks

```
                Stock A     Stock B
Share price     $100        $80
EPS (TTM)       $2.00       $4.00
Trailing P/E    50×         20×
5-yr EPS CAGR   40 %        12 %

PEG = 50/40 = 1.25     PEG = 20/12 = 1.67
```

Stock A appears expensive at 50× but has a **lower PEG (1.25)** than Stock B (1.67) — once growth is factored in, Stock A is the more reasonably priced of the two. This is the PEG's core insight.

## What the result means

Peter Lynch popularized the rule that a **PEG of 1.0** means the stock is fairly valued for its growth rate — below 1.0 is potentially undervalued, above 1.0 is potentially overvalued.

| PEG range  | Conventional interpretation |
|---|---|
| < 0.5      | Potentially deeply undervalued vs growth (or growth overestimated) |
| 0.5 – 1.0  | Attractive; growth justifies the multiple |
| 1.0        | "Fair value" by Lynch's rule of thumb |
| 1.0 – 2.0  | Premium; requires a quality or durability argument |
| > 2.0      | Expensive even after growth adjustment |

## Variants

- **Forward PEG** — uses next-year P/E and a 3–5 year consensus growth estimate; more forward-looking.
- **PEGY** — divides by (growth rate + dividend yield %); credit for dividends in the denominator; used for mature dividend payers where yield is part of total return.
- **EV/EBITDA / EBITDA growth** — enterprise-value equivalent of PEG; avoids capital structure distortions.

## Common mistakes

- Using a single-year growth estimate instead of a multi-year CAGR — one good year inflates the denominator and makes the PEG look artificially attractive.
- Applying PEG to mature, low-growth companies — a company growing at 3 % and trading at 12× has a PEG of 4, which is not meaningful; PEG works best for growth companies (15–40 % EPS growth).
- Taking analyst consensus growth forecasts at face value — analysts systematically overestimate long-run growth; apply a haircut for companies in competitive industries.
- Ignoring quality — two companies with identical PEGs can have wildly different return on equity, balance sheet strength, and earnings volatility.
