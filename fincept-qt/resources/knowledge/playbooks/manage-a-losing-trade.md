# How to Manage a Trade Going Against You

A trade is moving against you. This is the moment where most traders make their worst decisions: averaging into losing positions without a plan, holding indefinitely because "it'll come back," or panicking and selling at the worst moment. Having a decision framework before this happens is the difference between a professional response and an emotional one.

## When to Use This

Use this whenever a position has moved against you by a meaningful amount, or you're within 50% of your pre-defined stop level. Do not improvise — if you haven't thought about this before the trade, you're already behind.

---

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Separate the Trade Question from the Thesis Question

The first and most important step is to ask: **has anything happened that invalidates your original thesis, or is the stock just lower?**

Price going down is not thesis invalidation. If you bought a stock because you believed earnings would accelerate, and the stock is down 8% with no new information, your thesis is intact. The trade is simply in the normal noise zone.

If, however, the stock is down 8% because earnings disappointed, or because the competitive landscape changed, or because management guided down — that's thesis invalidation. These require different responses.

**Rule**: Never make sizing decisions based on price alone. Make them based on thesis status.

### Step 2: Evaluate Three Options Explicitly

When a trade moves against you, you have three choices. Write them out with specific prices and rationale:

**Option A: Cut the position.** Appropriate when: (1) thesis is invalidated; (2) you're at or near your pre-defined stop; (3) you need capital for higher-conviction opportunities; (4) you don't want to be in this position anymore regardless of analysis.

**Option B: Hold the position.** Appropriate when: (1) thesis is intact; (2) you're within expected volatility range; (3) your position size is still within risk parameters. No action required — but this must be a deliberate choice, not passive avoidance of a painful decision.

**Option C: Add to the position ("averaging down").** This is the most dangerous option and requires the strictest criteria: (1) thesis is definitively intact and has become more compelling at lower prices; (2) you have a specific price target and stop level for the new tranche; (3) adding does not put your total position size above your maximum allocation; (4) you can afford to be wrong on the add. Averaging down to reduce cost basis is not a strategy — it's rationalizing a mistake.

### Step 3: If You Add, Treat It as a New Trade

If you decide to add to a losing position, the add must be evaluated as an independent trade:
- What is the entry price?
- What is the stop for this tranche?
- What is the profit target?
- What is the R-multiple?

If you would not enter this trade fresh at today's price with this stop, you should not add just because you're already in.

### Step 4: Set a New Maximum Loss Level

If you've held through a drawdown and are still in the position, reset your maximum loss level explicitly. "I will exit this position if it falls another X%." Write this down. Do not change it unless you have new information that explicitly warrants a different stop — not just because the stock fell to your stop level.

### Step 5: Hedging as an Alternative to Cutting

In some circumstances — you believe in the position long-term but the short-term risk is elevated — a partial hedge is appropriate. This might mean buying a put, reducing size by 30–50%, or putting on a correlated short in the sector. A hedge is not the same as cutting; it reduces risk while preserving upside if your thesis plays out.

Use hedges for: position sizing that exceeds your normal risk but you have strong conviction; earnings/events where the binary risk is elevated; market-level risk (you want to stay long the stock but reduce beta into uncertainty).

## Common Mistakes

- **Averaging down because "it's cheaper" without re-evaluating the thesis.** Cheaper doesn't mean better. A $40 stock is not more attractive than a $50 stock unless the business is at least as good at $40.
- **Refusing to cut because it "makes the loss real."** The loss is already real. The only question is whether you continue to express it with capital.
- **Setting a new stop below the current price after the original stop is hit.** This is what a stop is for. Moving it to avoid the loss is not risk management.
- **Hoping for a bounce to exit at breakeven.** Breakeven is a psychologically meaningful but financially meaningless price. It has no connection to the business value or trade dynamics.

---

## What Success Looks Like

When you exit a losing trade, you can articulate why — thesis invalidated, stop hit, better opportunity. When you hold through a drawdown, you have a written rationale and a new stop level. You never add to a position without a formal entry/stop/target for the new tranche. Your losses are planned, not accidental.
