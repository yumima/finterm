# How to Read an Earnings Release

A typical Q3 earnings release has 30+ pages of MD&A, 8 pages of GAAP tables, and a press release with carefully chosen headline numbers. Most retail readers stop at the press release — that's where management *wants* you to stop. Don't.

## The 5-minute first pass

Open the press release. In order:

1. **Headline EPS — beat / miss / inline?**
   - Beat by 5%+ usually rallies; miss by 5%+ usually drops. Magnitude matters less than direction.

2. **Revenue — beat / miss / inline?**
   - **A revenue miss with an EPS beat is suspicious.** Often it means cost cuts or share buybacks fueled the EPS — not real growth.

3. **Forward guidance**
   - This often moves the stock more than the print itself. Lowered guidance with a "beat" can crash the stock.

4. **Same-store sales / units / DAUs**
   - Industry-specific KPIs often matter more than headline GAAP numbers. Retail: same-store sales. SaaS: net retention, ARR. Banks: net interest margin.

5. **Tone of the press release**
   - "Challenging environment," "macro headwinds," "transformation underway" — corporate-speak red flags.

## Worked example — interpreting a "beat"

```
Acme Corp Q3 release headlines:
  EPS:        $1.20 (vs $1.05 estimate)         → beat by 14%
  Revenue:    $980M (vs $1,020M estimate)       → MISS by 4%
  Guidance:   FY range lowered $4.40 → $4.20   → cut

Stock pre-market: ▼ −7%
```

Despite the headline beat, the market sees:
- Revenue missed → demand softening
- EPS beat funded by cost cuts and buybacks
- Guidance cut → it's getting worse
- Total return: bearish

A beginner who only saw "EPS BEAT!" would have bought into a falling knife.

## The 20-minute deep read

Open the actual 10-Q filed alongside (SEC EDGAR or Investor Relations).

### Read the MD&A first

Management's Discussion & Analysis is where they explain the numbers in narrative form. Look for:
- **What changed vs. last quarter** in management's own words
- **Risk factors added or removed** — companies must update these when material
- **Subsequent events** — anything that happened after quarter-end but before filing

### Then the cash flow statement

Compare:
- **Net Income** vs. **Operating Cash Flow** — should track over time. Persistent gap = accrual quality issue.
- **Operating Cash Flow** vs. **Capital Expenditures** — the difference is [[fcf|FCF]].
- **Stock-based compensation** as % of revenue — high SBC can mask GAAP cost of growth.

### Footnotes

Skim. Look for:
- Customer concentration (one customer >10% of revenue)
- Lease commitments (especially for retail, restaurants)
- Litigation reserves
- Goodwill impairment
- Off-balance-sheet arrangements

## Industry-specific KPIs that matter more than EPS

| Industry | Watch this |
|---|---|
| SaaS / subscription | ARR growth, net retention >100%, gross margin, magic number |
| Retail | Same-store sales (SSS), traffic, ticket size, inventory days |
| Banks | Net interest margin (NIM), loan growth, charge-offs, CET1 |
| Insurance | Combined ratio, reserve releases/strengthens, investment yield |
| Energy E&P | Production, lifting cost, decline rate, reserve replacement |
| REITs | FFO/AFFO per share, occupancy, lease spreads, dividend coverage |
| Auto OEMs | Units, ASP, mix, capacity utilization |
| Tech (consumer) | DAU/MAU, ARPU, CAC, LTV |
| Pharma | Pipeline progression, biggest-product run-rate, patent cliffs |
| Restaurants | SSS, units opened, food cost % |

## Listen to the earnings call

The Q&A section is usually more revealing than prepared remarks. Listen for:

- **What analysts ask repeatedly** — that's where market concern is
- **Management's specificity** — vague answers to numerical questions are bad signs
- **Use of "noise" or "transitional"** — sometimes true, often spin
- **Length of pauses** before answers
- **Hostile-vs-friendly analyst dynamic** — does the panel skew bullish or bearish?

## The post-earnings stock move and what it means

| Pre-print setup | Post-print reaction | Likely cause |
|---|---|---|
| High IV, beats | Sells off | "Sell the news" + vol crush |
| Beats but guides down | Sells off hard | Forward signal trumps backward beat |
| Misses but guides up | Rallies | Forward signal positive |
| In-line with cautious guidance | Mild move | Already priced in |
| Big beat in down sector | Outsized rally | Relative strength play |
| Beat with no guidance change in volatile sector | Could go either way | Watch the call |

## Common traps

- **"Adjusted" everything**: companies that strip out the same charges every quarter aren't reporting one-time items.
- **FX-neutral growth**: useful context but real growth is in actual dollars.
- **Backlog growth without revenue growth**: contracts may not convert.
- **Guidance lowered "due to macro"**: sometimes legitimate, often deflection.
- **Earnings beat 4 quarters running**: estimates may have been managed lower; check magnitude trend.

## Decision rules

- **Beat + raise + KPI inflection** → strong signal; consider adding
- **Beat + lower guide** → bearish; the future matters more than the past
- **Miss + reaffirm** → market may overreact; opportunity to add if thesis intact
- **Miss + cut + management changes tone** → trim or exit; thesis breaking
- **Big move on no apparent news** → wait 24h for the call recap before reacting
