# How to Build an Investment Thesis With Conviction

Most retail investors hold positions without a real thesis — just a vague feeling that "it should go up." When price moves against them, they have nothing to anchor against, and they bail at the worst time.

A **proper thesis is specific, falsifiable, and time-bound**. It tells you when you're right, when you're wrong, and what to do.

## The structure of a useful thesis

```
1. WHAT — the position (ticker, side, size)
2. WHY — one-sentence reason
3. EXPECTED OUTCOME — specific price/event/timeframe
4. WHAT WOULD PROVE ME WRONG — specific facts (not feelings)
5. POSITION SIZE — based on conviction and risk
6. EXIT TRIGGERS — when to sell (winning or losing)
```

If you can't fill in all 6, you don't have a thesis — you have a hunch.

## Worked example — short-form thesis

```
WHAT:    Long Costco (COST), 4% portfolio weight
WHY:     Membership fees provide growing high-margin revenue stream;
         membership renewal >90% indicates strong moat
EXPECTED:  $X stock price in 12 months at unchanged P/E + EPS growth
DISPROOF: Renewal rate drops below 88% for 2 quarters, OR
          same-store sales growth turns negative for 2 quarters, OR
          management materially changes membership pricing strategy
SIZING:   4% portfolio, willing to take 25% drawdown
EXIT:     P/E > 35× without earnings acceleration → trim
          Thesis disproven → exit fully
          Held 5+ years for compounding tax efficiency
```

Concrete and falsifiable. You'll know in months, not years, if you're wrong.

## The five-step process

### Step 1 — Identify the inefficiency

Why does this opportunity exist? Markets aren't perfectly efficient, but they're efficient enough that "I think it'll go up" isn't a thesis. You need:

- **Information edge** — you understand something the market doesn't
- **Time horizon edge** — you can hold longer than the marginal seller
- **Behavioral edge** — you're acting against herd panic / euphoria
- **Structural edge** — you can buy what others can't (small caps, illiquid, etc.)

If none of these apply, you probably don't have an edge.

### Step 2 — State the thesis falsifiably

Bad: "Apple is a great company"
Good: "Apple's Services revenue will grow >15%/year for next 3 years, supporting current 28× P/E"

Bad: "I think this will rally"
Good: "Stock will trade above $120 by Dec 2026 if FY26 revenue exceeds $5B"

Falsifiability is the test. If your thesis can't be wrong, it's not a thesis.

### Step 3 — Pre-mortem (the most-skipped step)

Imagine it's 2 years from now and the trade has lost 50%. Write down what happened.

```
Possible failure modes for COST thesis:
  - Recession hits middle-class spending hard
  - New regulation on membership-club model
  - Amazon Prime introduces direct-competitor warehouse model
  - Costco fails to navigate generational management transition
  - Key supplier (poultry, etc.) goes bankrupt; reputational risk
```

For each, ask: how likely (rough %)? Would I see it coming? Is it survivable at my position size?

If you can't see a failure mode, you don't understand the position well enough.

### Step 4 — Size by conviction

| Conviction level | Position size | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Speculative | 1–2% | Mostly wrong is OK |
| Reasonable | 3–5% | Above-average odds |
| Strong | 5–8% | Multiple catalysts aligned |
| Highest | 8–15% | Multi-quarter homework + clear edge |
| Buffett-style concentrated | 20%+ | Decades of conviction; rarely justified |

Never size for upside; size for downside survivability.

### Step 5 — Define exits before entering

Three exit triggers, all pre-decided:

- **Target hit** (e.g., P/E > 35× → trim 50%)
- **Time-based** (e.g., if no progress in 18 months, exit and reallocate)
- **Thesis-broken** (any of the disproof conditions from Step 2)

Without pre-defined exits, you'll either ride winners too long or hold losers because of sunk cost.

## Conviction calibration

Rate your conviction on each new thesis 1–10. Track over time:

- **Conviction 8 trades** that work out → good calibration
- **Conviction 4 trades** that work out at higher rate than 8 → over-confidence at the top
- **Conviction 8 trades** that fail consistently → systematic blind spot

This is the most valuable form of self-knowledge in investing — and almost no retail traders do it.

## Update protocols

Theses change. The question is whether you're updating because of new information or because price went against you.

```
Legitimate update: company missed guidance on the metric your thesis depended on
Suspect update:    "well, even if EPS misses, the brand is still strong"

Legitimate update: new competitor announced disruptive product
Suspect update:    "the chart looks oversold"

Legitimate update: management replaced unexpectedly
Suspect update:    "this seems like a good entry now that it's down 30%"
```

The test: would this new fact change your thesis if you didn't already own the stock? If yes, update. If no, you're rationalizing.

## Common thesis failures

- **Vagueness** — "growth story" with no metric
- **Already priced in** — your edge isn't an edge if everyone knows
- **Survivorship narrative** — "next FAANG" is almost always wrong
- **Confirmation echo chamber** — only consuming bull-case content
- **Confusing brand with business** — Tesla is a great brand; was its 2021 valuation a great business?
- **Timing dependence** — thesis requires specific event by specific date (high failure rate)

## Decision rules

- **Write the thesis BEFORE buying** — never after
- **Document falsification criteria** — concrete, observable
- **Pre-commit position size and exit triggers** — written, dated
- **Quarterly thesis review** — is each holding's thesis still intact?
- **Quarterly bear-case search** — actively read the strongest counter-arguments
- **Annual conviction-vs-result calibration** — improve your self-knowledge

## In finterm

Portfolio thesis notes (or Notes screen) is the place to record this. Each holding should have a written thesis with the structure above. Quarterly review forces re-confrontation with original assumptions vs current reality.
