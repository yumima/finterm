# How to Research a Sector From Scratch

Sector research is top-down analysis applied to a defined industry. Whether you're building a thematic position, screening for individual stocks, or assessing macro rotations, understanding a sector's structure — its economics, key metrics, and cycle position — is the foundation. This playbook gives you a repeatable framework for any sector.

## When to Use This

Use this when you're entering a sector you don't know well, doing a periodic review of a sector you already track, or trying to understand whether a macro theme (rising rates, commodity supercycle, consumer stress) will help or hurt a specific industry.

---

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Understand the Business Model Economics

Start with a single question: **how does a company in this sector make money?**

- What does the company sell, and to whom?
- Is revenue recurring (SaaS, utilities) or transactional (retail, auto)?
- What drives gross margin? (Scale? Patents? Commodity prices? Labor?)
- What does the capital structure require? (Asset-heavy like airlines/railroads vs. asset-light like software?)

The answer determines what metrics matter most. For airlines: load factor, RASM, fuel cost. For software: ARR growth, net revenue retention, Rule of 40. For banks: net interest margin, loan loss provisions, capital ratios. Don't use generic metrics across all sectors.

### Step 2: Map the Value Chain

Who are the key players at each stage?

- **Upstream (suppliers)**: what are the input costs, and who controls them?
- **The company itself**: where does it fit in the chain — commodity producer, manufacturer, distributor, retailer?
- **Downstream (customers)**: who buys, and what's their pricing power versus the sector?

This map identifies where margin pools sit and who captures them. A sector with strong suppliers and strong customers (e.g., mid-tier retailers between branded manufacturers and price-sensitive consumers) will structurally earn thin margins.

### Step 3: Identify the Top 3–5 Sector Metrics

Research analyst reports and industry publications always track a small set of sector-specific KPIs. For each sector, identify these:

- **Retail**: same-store sales growth (SSSG), sales per square foot, inventory turns
- **Semiconductors**: utilization rates, book-to-bill ratio, ASP trends
- **Banks**: NIM, NPL ratio, CET1 capital ratio
- **Energy**: realized prices, production growth, all-in sustaining cost (AISC)
- **REITs**: FFO, cap rates, occupancy, same-store NOI growth
- **Healthcare**: patient volumes, payer mix, drug pricing, R&D pipeline value

Find these metrics for the top 5 companies in the sector. Plot them over 5 years. This tells you where you are in the sector cycle.

### Step 4: Identify Cyclicality and Current Cycle Position

Is this a cyclical or secular industry?

- **Cyclical**: earnings and revenue move substantially with the economic cycle (industrials, materials, autos, banks, energy). The key question is: where are we in the cycle?
- **Secular growth**: driven by multi-year structural trends regardless of the cycle (cloud computing, genomics). The key question is: is the growth rate sustainable?

For cyclical sectors, build a simple model:
- Where are current sector margins vs. normalized margins?
- Is the industry consolidating (fewer, stronger players) or fragmenting?
- What's the inventory and capacity utilization picture?

### Step 5: Identify the 2–3 Catalysts That Would Change the Investment Case

For each sector, there are a small number of variables whose direction determines whether the sector is a buy, hold, or sell. Be explicit:

- "Semiconductors: if utilization returns above 80%, pricing power recovers and EPS revisions will be positive"
- "Regional banks: if the yield curve steepens and credit losses stay contained, NIM expansion drives earnings beats"

Write these down before you own anything. These become your monitoring criteria.

### Step 6: Screen for the Best Names Within the Sector

Now and only now apply stock-specific criteria:
- Which companies have best-in-class metrics on the sector KPIs?
- Which have the cleanest balance sheets for a downturn?
- Which are priced at the most attractive valuation relative to their sector position?

The goal is to be invested in the best company in a favorable sector, not a mediocre company in a sector you like.

## Common Mistakes

- **Using the same metrics across all sectors.** P/E is nearly useless for banks; EV/EBITDA is wrong for financials; you need sector-specific tools.
- **Confusing a good sector with a good investment.** The best-known, most-loved sectors are often the most expensively priced. A less-followed sector in early cycle recovery can outperform by far.
- **Ignoring the cycle position.** Buying a deeply cyclical sector at peak margins is a value trap. The same sector at trough margins is often the best value.
- **Failing to monitor your catalysts.** Once invested, the sector thesis must be updated against the catalysts you identified. If they don't materialize, reassess.

---

## What Success Looks Like

You can explain the economics of the sector in two minutes, identify where it is in the cycle, name the 3–5 key metrics that matter, and make a clear distinction between why the sector is attractive and which specific names within it represent the best risk/reward.
