# How to Evaluate a Stock

A 30-minute checklist before you commit real capital. Not a substitute for deep work, but enough to weed out obvious bad ideas and prepare smart questions.

## 1. Understand the business (5 min)

- **What does the company actually sell?** If you can't explain it in two sentences, you're not ready.
- **Who pays them?** Consumer, business, government, advertiser?
- **Recurring or transactional?** Subscription revenue is worth more than one-off sales.
- **Pricing power?** Can they raise prices without losing customers?
- **Switching cost / network effect?** Strong moats compound; weak ones erode.

Read the company's most recent earnings call transcript — management answers these implicitly.

## 2. Check the financial fundamentals (10 min)

In finterm, open Equity Research → Financials. Look at the last 5 years:

- **Revenue growth** — accelerating, steady, or decelerating?
- **Gross margin trend** — rising means pricing power; falling means competition or input costs
- **[[fcf|Free Cash Flow]]** — is the business actually generating cash? Compare to net income (big persistent gap = yellow flag)
- **Net debt / EBITDA** — leverage; >3× for non-utility deserves scrutiny
- **[[roe|ROE]]** — is the business earning a good return on shareholder capital?
- **Stock-based comp** as % of revenue — high SBC masks GAAP profitability in tech

## 3. Sanity-check valuation (5 min)

Don't try to pinpoint fair value. Just ask: is this priced as if the business is *broken*, *normal*, or *exceptional*?

- [[pe-ratio|P/E]] vs. its 5-year history
- [[pe-ratio|P/E]] vs. closest peers
- [[ev-ebitda|EV/EBITDA]] vs. peers
- Free cash flow yield ([[fcf|FCF]] ÷ market cap) — anything over 5% is interesting
- PEG ratio (P/E ÷ growth%) — <1.0 traditionally cheap

If valuation is near 5-year highs *and* growth is decelerating, you're paying premium prices for a slowing business. Be cautious.

## 4. Look for red flags (5 min)

| Red flag | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Frequent "one-time" charges | They aren't one-time |
| Adjusted earnings >> GAAP consistently | Aggressive add-backs |
| Auditor change in last 10-K | Investigate why |
| CFO turnover in last 12 months | Often precedes restatements |
| Insider selling clustering | Some normal; a wave is not |
| Revenue growth without operating income growth | Scaling unprofitably |
| Receivables growing faster than revenue | Possible channel-stuffing |
| Inventory rising while sales flat | Demand softening |
| Goodwill > tangible book + sector down | Impairment risk |
| Dividend > FCF for multiple quarters | Likely cut coming |

## 5. Define your thesis and exit (5 min)

Before buying, write down:

- **Why am I buying this** — in one sentence
- **What would prove me wrong** — specific facts (e.g., "if revenue growth drops below 15% for two quarters")
- **Position size** — see [[size-a-position]]
- **Time horizon** — quarter, year, multi-year
- **What's my exit signal** — distinct from "what would prove me wrong"

If you can't write these clearly, you don't have a thesis — you have a feeling. That's how money gets lost.

## Worked example — short-form thesis

> **Ticker:** Costco (COST)
> **Thesis:** Member retention >90%, fee growth from membership hikes is durable margin expansion. Buying at 30× P/E versus 5y avg of 32×.
> **Disconfirmation:** Membership renewal rate drops below 88%, OR same-store sales growth turns negative for 2 consecutive quarters.
> **Sizing:** 4% of portfolio; willing to take 25% drawdown.
> **Time horizon:** 3+ years.
> **Exit signal:** P/E > 40× without earnings acceleration, OR thesis broken per above.

Concrete and falsifiable. You'll know in months, not years, if you were wrong.

## Quality checklist (Charlie Munger / Buffett style)

- ✓ I understand this business
- ✓ It has a durable competitive advantage
- ✓ Management is honest and capable (read 5+ years of letters)
- ✓ I can buy it at an attractive price
- ✓ I'd be comfortable holding it 5+ years even if the market closed

If any "no", reconsider.

## Time-budget extension (deep dive)

For positions you'll size meaningfully (>5% of portfolio), add:

- **2 hrs**: Read latest 10-K MD&A + risk factors
- **2 hrs**: Listen to last 4 earnings call Q&A sessions
- **1 hr**: Read 2 competitor 10-Ks for context
- **2 hrs**: Build a 3-statement model (revenue → income → balance → cash)
- **1 hr**: Sensitivity analysis on key assumptions (revenue growth, margin)

For 10%+ position sizes, double everything.

## What this checklist does *not* replace

- Reading the actual 10-K (especially MD&A and risk factors)
- Listening to at least one earnings call
- Understanding the industry's competitive dynamics
- Modeling a 3-statement DCF if you're going to hold meaningful size
- Stress-testing what happens to the position in a 30%+ market drawdown

But it will save you from the most common mistakes.
