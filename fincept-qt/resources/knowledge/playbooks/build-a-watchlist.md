# How to Build a Watchlist

A watchlist is the bridge between idea generation and decision-ready research. Most amateur watchlists fail because they're either too small (you miss opportunities) or too big (you can't track anything meaningfully).

**Target size: 15–30 names**, broken into tiers.

## Step 1 — Source ideas

Don't rely on one channel. Mix:

- **Screeners** — quantitative filters (revenue growth > 20%, FCF positive, market cap > $1B). Finviz is the popular free one; in finterm, Markets has built-in screening.
- **News** — companies in headlines for non-stupid reasons (new product, regulatory tailwind, key personnel)
- **Earnings calendar** — quarterly results that beat or missed dramatically often create entry windows
- **Peer expansion** — once you understand one company, study its competitors
- **Public investor letters** — quarterly letters from concentrated funds (Pershing Square, Greenlight, etc.) often disclose new positions
- **13-F filings** — what hedge funds bought / sold last quarter (lagging but useful for hunting grounds)
- **Insider buying** — clusters of open-market buys at multi-year highs from insiders (rare and meaningful)

Skip: friends' tips, social media tickers, anything described as "the next [previous winner]."

## Step 2 — Quick triage

For each candidate, spend 5 minutes:

- Does the business make sense to you?
- Is it profitable, or has a clear path?
- Is liquidity adequate (typically >$10M average daily volume)?
- Is the float reasonable (avoid micro-caps unless you specialize)?
- Does it pass a basic accounting smell test (cash conversion, leverage, dilution)?

Companies that fail this triage don't make it onto the watchlist.

## Step 3 — Tier the watchlist

| Tier | What it means | Action | Typical size |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Active** | Could buy this week if price is right | Daily check; alerts on key levels | 5–10 names |
| **B — Watch** | Compelling but not actionable yet (price too high, catalyst unclear) | Weekly check | 10–15 names |
| **C — Backburner** | Worth knowing about; not on the menu now | Monthly review | 5–15 names |

If you have 30 A-tier names, you're not really watching them — re-tier.

## Step 4 — Define what you're watching for

For each name, write **one sentence** describing the trigger:

- "Buy if it pulls back to $80 (5-yr P/E low)"
- "Buy if next quarter shows ARR re-accelerating to >25%"
- "Buy if management is replaced"
- "Buy if no acquisition by Q3, after which the strategic option expires"
- "Buy if forward yield exceeds 5% with payout still <80% of FCF"

Without explicit triggers, you'll either buy reactively at peaks or stare at falling prices wondering whether to act.

## Worked example — A-tier watchlist row

```
Ticker:           V (Visa)
Tier:             A (could buy this week)
Why interesting:  Quality compounder; 18%+ ROIC; 50%+ operating margin; takes a slice of every card swipe
Current price:    $280
5y P/E avg:       30×
Current P/E:      29×
Buy trigger:      Pull-back to $250 (P/E ~26×) — ~10% from here
Cancel trigger:   Loss of network effect (Apple Pay/CBDC disrupts), or P/E > 35×
Position size:    Initial 4% of portfolio at trigger; max 8% on adds
Last reviewed:    2026-04-15
Notes:            Earnings 2026-05-08 — wait through print before buying
```

Concrete and actionable. The triggers tell you what to do without thinking.

## Step 5 — Maintenance

- **Weekly**: review A-tier triggers, news, recent earnings
- **Monthly**: re-tier; drop names that no longer compel you
- **Quarterly**: clean sweep — does the original thesis for each name still hold?
- **Annually**: review what hit a trigger, what you did, and what the result was

A stale watchlist is worse than no watchlist — it makes you think you're researching when you're just scrolling tickers.

## Idea sources by quality

| Source | Quality | Volume | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Berkshire annual letter | High | Low | Once a year; gold standard |
| Pabrai/Klarman/Greenblatt letters | High | Low | Concentrated funds; transparent |
| Top 1% hedge fund 13-Fs | Medium | Medium | Lagged 45 days |
| Quantitative screeners | Medium | High | Sift, then dig |
| WSJ / FT long-form | Medium | Medium | Sometimes spots an inflection |
| Twitter/X finance accounts | Mixed | Very High | High noise; rare gems |
| YouTube finance | Low | Very High | Mostly hype; avoid as primary |
| r/wallstreetbets | Very Low | Very High | Sentiment indicator only |
| Friends/family tips | Very Low | Variable | Almost never works |

## Decision rules

- **A-tier > 10 names** → re-tier; you can't watch that many actively
- **B-tier > 20 names** → cull; review which still compel you
- **Total > 50 names** → reset; pick your 30 best, demote rest
- **A-tier name unchanged for 6+ months** → demote to B
- **Trigger hits but you didn't act** → reflect; trigger was wrong or your conviction was

## Common mistakes

- **Adding without removing** — watchlist bloat kills attention
- **Chasing momentum** — names that just doubled rarely belong on a buy-watchlist; they belong on a study list
- **Skipping the boring** — the best watchlist names are often unsexy compounders with predictable cash flow
- **No exit triggers** — you also need to know when to drop a name from the list, not just when to buy
- **Treating watchlist as portfolio** — they are different; watchlist is the funnel, portfolio is what you actually own
- **Confirmation bias in maintenance** — re-read your original notes, not your latest reading
