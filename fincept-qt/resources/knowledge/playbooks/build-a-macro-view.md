# How to Build a Top-Down Macro Investment View

A macro view is not a prediction — it is a probabilistic assessment of which economic regime is most likely over your investment horizon, and which assets historically perform well in that regime. Done correctly, it turns economic data from background noise into an actionable investment framework.

## When to Use This

Use this at the start of each quarter (or more frequently during regime transitions) to update your macro thesis, inform asset allocation tilts, and identify the data points that would change your view.

---

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify the Current Regime

Regimes are defined by two variables: **growth trajectory** (accelerating vs. decelerating) and **inflation trajectory** (rising vs. falling). The four regime quadrants:

| Growth | Inflation | Regime | Favored Assets |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accelerating | Low/Falling | Goldilocks / Risk-On | Equities, EM, credit |
| Accelerating | Rising | Reflation / Late Cycle | Commodities, TIPS, value stocks |
| Decelerating | Rising | Stagflation | Commodities, cash, short duration |
| Decelerating | Falling | Recession / Risk-Off | Treasuries, gold, quality equities |

**How to identify**: Look at the direction of change, not the level. An economy decelerating from 3% growth to 1.5% growth is in the "decelerating" bucket even if growth is still positive.

### Step 2: Assess the Leading Indicators

Leading indicators typically turn 3–6 months before GDP inflects:

- **ISM Manufacturing PMI**: above 50 = expansion; below 50 = contraction; direction of change matters more than level
- **Yield curve**: 2s/10s spread; inversion typically precedes recession by 12–18 months
- **Credit spreads**: widening = tightening financial conditions; leading signal for equity stress
- **Consumer confidence and leading employment indicators** (initial jobless claims, temp employment)
- **M2 money supply growth** (leading for inflation by 12–18 months)

### Step 3: Check the Central Bank Position

Central bank policy is the primary transmission mechanism from macro to markets:

- **Are they hiking or cutting?** Determines risk-free rate direction, which reprices all asset classes
- **Are they ahead of or behind the inflation curve?** A behind-the-curve Fed is a hawkish surprise risk; ahead-of-curve is a dovish surprise opportunity
- **What does the futures market price?** Compare current policy rate to forward-implied rate 12 months out. If futures price 2 cuts and you think 4 cuts, that's a duration-long thesis.

### Step 4: Build the Cross-Asset Implication Map

Translate the regime + central bank position into asset class tilts:

**Equities:**
- Sector positioning: late cycle favors energy/materials/financials; early cycle favors tech/consumer discretionary
- Factor positioning: value outperforms in rising rate/inflation regimes; quality outperforms in decelerating/risk-off
- Geographic positioning: EM outperforms in weak USD/reflationary environments; DM outperforms in risk-off

**Fixed Income:**
- Duration: short duration in rising rate regimes; long duration in recession/risk-off
- Credit quality: IG over HY in late cycle and recession; HY can outperform in early reflationary cycle
- TIPS vs. nominals: TIPS outperform when real yields are falling and inflation is rising

**Currencies:**
- USD: strengthens in risk-off and rate-differential environments; weakens in reflationary risk-on
- Commodity currencies (AUD, CAD, NOK): outperform in commodity/reflation cycles

**Commodities:**
- Energy: late-cycle and geopolitical-risk outperformer
- Gold: outperforms in real rate decline and systemic risk environments
- Industrial metals: early-to-mid cycle, China-demand-driven

### Step 5: Define Your Falsification Criteria

For each major position in the macro view, write down: **what data would tell me I'm wrong?**

- "If CPI prints above 4% in Q3, my 'inflation moderating' thesis is wrong and I need to reduce duration"
- "If ISM stays above 52 for three months, my 'late cycle deceleration' thesis is wrong and I should add cyclical exposure"

Without these criteria, a macro view becomes a narrative you defend rather than a thesis you test.

### Step 6: Size for Uncertainty, Not Conviction

Macro calls have a lower hit rate than they feel in the moment. Even experienced macro investors are right roughly 55–60% of the time on regime calls. Size positions accordingly:

- Position sizing should be **inversely proportional to consensus confidence** in the view. If everyone agrees the Fed is cutting, the trade is already priced.
- Use tilts (overweight/underweight vs. benchmark) rather than concentrated bets
- Scale into regime views as confirming data accumulates; don't front-run before confirmation

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Confusing level with direction.** An economy growing at 2% but decelerating from 4% is in a different regime than one growing at 2% and accelerating from 0%.
- **Over-anchoring on the most recent data.** One hot CPI print doesn't change a trend; three do.
- **Ignoring what's priced in.** A correct macro view expressed in an asset that already reflects that view produces zero return.
- **Treating the macro view as permanent.** Regimes shift. Update your view quarterly at minimum, or when multiple leading indicators contradict your thesis.

---

## What Success Looks Like

You can state your current regime assessment, name three leading indicators consistent with it, identify two asset class tilts that express the view, and specify exactly what data would cause you to revise the view. The macro framework updates your asset allocation, not your individual stock picks.
