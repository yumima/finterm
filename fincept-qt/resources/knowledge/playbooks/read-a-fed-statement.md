# How to Read a Fed Statement and Press Conference

Eight times a year, the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) releases a policy statement and holds a press conference. These are among the highest-impact scheduled market events on the calendar. Understanding how to read them — what's changed, what matters, and what markets will react to — turns a confusing wall of policy language into actionable information.

## When to Use This

Use this process on every FOMC meeting day (meetings are typically in January, March, May, June, July, September, November, and December). Also use it when reading FOMC minutes (released three weeks after each meeting) or Fed speeches from key officials.

---

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Find the Previous Statement Immediately

Before reading the new statement, pull up the previous one side by side. **The Fed communicates through changes in language**, not through the statement in isolation. The key question is: what was added, removed, or softened compared to last time?

Specific phrases to watch:
- Changes in the inflation characterization (is it "elevated," "moderating," or "returning to target"?)
- Changes to the labor market description ("robust," "solid," "balanced")
- The phrase "ongoing increases" vs. "some additional" vs. "any additional" — these are calibrated policy signals
- Deletions of language about specific conditions for action (a deletion often matters more than an addition)

### Step 2: Read the Dot Plot (If Available)

At four of the eight annual meetings (March, June, September, December), the FOMC releases the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP), including the "dot plot" — a chart showing where each anonymous FOMC member expects rates to be at year-end for the next three years and "longer run."

What to look at:
- **Median projection**: where is the median dot at end-year? This is the consensus forecast for the terminal rate.
- **Dot dispersion**: wide dispersion means high uncertainty and disagreement. Tight clustering means confidence.
- **Changes from the previous dot plot**: median moved higher = hawkish surprise; lower = dovish surprise.

### Step 3: Watch the Opening Statement for Tone

The Chair's opening remarks (read verbatim) set the tone. Listen for:
- **First mention of risks**: "risks are broadly balanced" vs. "risks to the upside/downside" — this signals the bias for future action
- **Conditionality language**: "data-dependent" means they're not pre-committing; "we expect further firming may be appropriate" is a forward commitment
- **Acknowledgment of market tightening**: when financial conditions tighten significantly (via credit spreads or equity selloff), the Fed sometimes acknowledges this as partial substitute for rate hikes

### Step 4: The Q&A Is Where Real Information Lives

Press conference Q&A produces the actual signal. Watch for:
- **"Sufficiently restrictive"** — magic phrase that has historically preceded pauses
- **When asked about cuts**: hawkish if deflected; dovish if explicitly acknowledged
- **Divergence from the statement**: the Chair occasionally says something more specific than the statement, which moves markets in real time
- **The most hawkish question**: journalists compete to ask the most extreme version of the rate-hike question. How the Chair responds to the most aggressive framing tells you where the floor is.

### Step 5: Separate the Policy Decision from the Path

Markets rarely move on whether the Fed raises/cuts *today* — that's usually priced in. The movement comes from **the path implied going forward**. A 25bps hike with a dovish press conference is net easing; a 25bps hike with a hawkish press conference is net tightening.

The CME FedWatch tool shows market-implied probabilities for each meeting. On meeting day, check what's priced in before the statement, and compare the post-statement pricing to understand whether the outcome was a dovish or hawkish surprise.

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Trading the initial knee-jerk reaction.** The first five minutes after the statement are noise — algorithms reacting to words, not to comprehension. Experienced traders often wait 20–30 minutes for the signal to stabilize.
- **Ignoring the dot plot shift.** The policy rate decision is the headline; the dot plot shift is often the story. A hold with a higher median dot is a hawkish hold.
- **Not comparing to the previous statement.** Reading the new statement in isolation misses the entire communicative purpose.
- **Assuming "data-dependent" means uncertainty.** Data-dependent often means the Fed has a clear bias but is maintaining optionality. Context — recent inflation and employment data — tells you which direction they're leaning.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Within 15 minutes of the press conference ending, you can characterize the outcome as a hawkish/dovish surprise or in-line, explain specifically why (pointing to changed language or dot plot shift), and have a view on which asset classes the surprise favors: rate-sensitive equities, duration, the dollar, or credit.
