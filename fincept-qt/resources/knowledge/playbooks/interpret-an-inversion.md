# How to Interpret a Yield Curve Inversion

The financial press loves to scream "RECESSION IMMINENT" the moment 2s10s goes negative. The signal is real but more nuanced than the headlines.

## What an inversion actually is

When the yield on a shorter-dated Treasury (commonly 2-year, sometimes 3-month) **exceeds** the yield on a longer-dated Treasury (commonly 10-year), the curve is "inverted" at that spread.

The most-watched spread is **2s10s = 10Y − 2Y yield**. When this goes negative, financial Twitter activates.

## What history says

Every US recession in the past ~50 years was preceded by a 2s10s inversion. **But:**

- The lead time varies wildly: **6 to 24 months**
- A short, shallow inversion may un-invert before any recession begins
- The 1998 inversion did not produce an immediate recession (came in 2001)
- 2022 inversion → recession debate continues

## Track record (US, post-1970)

| Inversion start | Depth | Duration | Recession start | Lag |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Aug 1978 | −158 bps | 17 mo | Jan 1980 | 17 mo |
| Sep 1980 | −221 bps | 11 mo | Jul 1981 | 10 mo |
| Dec 1988 | −44 bps | 13 mo | Jul 1990 | 19 mo |
| Jun 1998 | −10 bps | 1 mo | (no recession; came in 2001) | n/a |
| Feb 2000 | −51 bps | 11 mo | Mar 2001 | 13 mo |
| Dec 2005 | −19 bps | 18 mo | Dec 2007 | 24 mo |
| Aug 2019 | −5 bps | 4 mo | (COVID 2020 — exogenous) | n/a |
| Jul 2022 | up to −108 bps | ~2 yr | Debated | — |

**Pattern**: deeper and longer inversions correlate with sooner/larger recessions. Shallow brief inversions are noise.

## What the inversion is *actually* telling you

Long rates reflect long-run expectations of growth + inflation + a term premium. Short rates reflect current Fed policy. An inversion means:

> "The market thinks future short rates will be *lower* than today's — likely because the Fed will need to cut to support a slowing economy."

That's a recession *forecast by the market*, not a guarantee. The forecast can be wrong.

## A 4-step playbook when you see an inversion

### Step 1 — Confirm the signal

Don't react to a single day's print. Wait for:
- 2s10s closes below zero for 5+ consecutive trading days
- Or 3M-10Y also inverts (preferred by NY Fed model)
- Or both inversions persist for >1 month

### Step 2 — Layer in confirming indicators

Inversion + any of these = serious signal:
- **HY credit spreads widening** > 100 bps from cycle tights
- **Unemployment rising** > 0.5% from cycle low (Sahm Rule)
- **ISM Manufacturing PMI** < 48 for 2+ months
- **Conference Board Leading Economic Index** declining 6+ months
- **Bank lending standards tightening** (Senior Loan Officer Survey)

If 3+ of these flash with inversion, recession probability jumps from "noise" to "real."

### Step 3 — Examine your own situation

Three honest questions:
1. **Cash runway**: 6–12 months of expenses in liquid assets?
2. **Duration risk**: how leveraged are you to long-rates / growth stocks?
3. **Job security**: are you in a sector that gets cut early?

A 30-year-old saving for retirement should mostly ignore inversions. A 60-year-old with concentrated equity exposure should not.

### Step 4 — Adjust positioning incrementally

| Confidence in signal | Action |
|---|---|
| Just inverted | Stay course; re-read the Sahm Rule level |
| Inverted + 1 confirm | Trim equity beta by 10–20%; raise cash buffer |
| Inverted + 2–3 confirms | Reduce equity to long-term allocation; consider bond duration |
| Inverted + un-inverting + Sahm triggered | Defensive positioning; sector rotation to staples/healthcare |
| Recession officially called | Don't panic-sell; equity bottoms before recession ends |

## The de-inversion is often the bigger signal

Counterintuitively: a yield curve that's been inverted and **steepens back to positive** has historically been a closer recession trigger than the initial inversion. It usually means the Fed has started cutting in response to deteriorating data.

| Cycle | Un-invert date | Recession start | Lag |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1989–90 | May 1989 | Jul 1990 | 14 mo |
| 2000 | Feb 2001 | Mar 2001 | 1 mo |
| 2007 | Jun 2007 | Dec 2007 | 6 mo |
| 2019 | Oct 2019 | (COVID) | 5 mo |

When a long-inverted curve un-inverts, that's the moment to take recession positioning seriously.

## What to do (and not do)

### Don't:
- Liquidate to cash the day of inversion
- Ignore your investment horizon — if you're 25 and saving for retirement, recessions come and go
- Trade derivatives based on a single signal you saw on cable news
- Confuse "inversion" with "the bottom is in" — equities often top *after* inversion

### Do:
- **Re-examine portfolio risk**: are you positioned for a 30%+ equity drawdown if it comes?
- **Check duration exposure**: long-duration assets (long bonds, growth stocks) underperform when rate expectations shift
- **Stress-test cash needs**: do you have 6–12 months of expenses in liquid assets?
- **Watch credit spreads**: HY-IG spread widening alongside curve inversion is a stronger combined signal
- **Watch unemployment**: U-3 troughing (or the **Sahm Rule** triggering) typically precedes recession by 2–3 months

## What to monitor in finterm

- **Economics screen** → 2s10s and 3M-10Y spreads, time series
- **Gov Data** → raw Treasury par yields
- **News** → FOMC commentary, BLS releases (NFP, CPI)
- **Macro context**: inflation expectations, credit spreads, unemployment

A yield curve inversion is one of many recession indicators. It's a signal worth respecting — but never the only one to act on.
