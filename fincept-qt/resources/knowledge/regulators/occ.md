# OCC — Office of the Comptroller of the Currency

The **Office of the Comptroller of the Currency** is an independent bureau within the U.S. Department of the Treasury. It charters, regulates, and supervises all **nationally chartered banks** — the institutions with "National" in their name or "N.A." (National Association) after it: JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A., Bank of America, N.A., Citibank, N.A., and Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. are all OCC-supervised entities.

The Comptroller of the Currency is appointed by the President for a five-year term. The OCC is self-funded through assessments on the banks it supervises — it does not rely on congressional appropriations.

## Who it covers

The OCC supervises approximately 1,100 national banks and federal savings associations, which collectively hold about 70% of total U.S. commercial banking assets. If a bank has a federal charter (vs. a state charter), the OCC is its primary prudential regulator.

The OCC does **not** regulate:
- State-chartered banks that are Federal Reserve members (those go to the Fed)
- State-chartered non-member banks (FDIC)
- Broker-dealers, investment advisers, or exchanges (SEC/FINRA)

## Key rules and functions every trader should know

**National bank charters** — the OCC grants the charter that allows a bank to operate nationwide, accept deposits, make loans, and engage in a range of financial activities. Charter revocation is the ultimate enforcement tool.

**Derivatives activities** — nationally chartered banks are significant participants in derivatives markets (interest rate swaps, currency forwards, credit default swaps). The OCC publishes a quarterly **Derivatives Report** covering notional exposure, credit exposure, and trading revenue for the largest bank holding companies. JPMorgan alone holds trillions in notional derivatives.

**Capital requirements** — the OCC implements Basel III capital adequacy standards for national banks: Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1), Tier 1 capital, and Total Capital ratios must exceed minimum thresholds. The OCC, Fed, and FDIC jointly finalized enhanced capital rules for large banks.

**Permissible activities / preemption** — one of the OCC's most commercially significant powers is its ability to preempt state laws for national banks. This has been used to allow nationally chartered banks to charge interest rates permissible in their home state regardless of where the borrower lives.

**Fintech charters** — the OCC has attempted to create a "special purpose national bank charter" for fintech companies, allowing them to operate nationally without a full bank license. This has faced legal challenges from state banking regulators.

## Why traders care

1. **Bank earnings context** — understanding which regulator a bank answers to helps interpret earnings calls. National banks face OCC exam cycles, with supervisory findings in MRA (Matters Requiring Attention) and MRIA (Matters Requiring Immediate Attention) categories.
2. **Derivatives data** — the OCC's quarterly derivatives report is a key source of aggregate data on bank derivatives exposure, trading revenue, and credit risk.
3. **Capital rules affect ROE** — tighter OCC capital requirements compress returns on equity for the largest banks, affecting valuations and buyback capacity.
4. **Consent orders as trading signals** — a major OCC consent order signals operational risk problems and management distraction; can suppress bank stock performance for extended periods.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **Loan-to-one-borrower limits** — national banks cannot lend more than 15% of capital to a single borrower (25% with collateral); violations are a common enforcement finding.
- **CRA compliance** — Community Reinvestment Act obligations require banks to serve low- and moderate-income communities; poor CRA ratings can block acquisitions.
- **Fiduciary activities** — national banks serving as trustees must meet OCC fiduciary standards; conflicts of interest and self-dealing are enforcement priorities.
- **Third-party risk** — the OCC holds banks responsible for the risk management practices of their critical vendors and fintechs; "the bank cannot outsource its obligations."
