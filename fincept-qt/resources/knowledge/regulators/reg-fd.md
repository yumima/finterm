# Regulation FD — Fair Disclosure

**Regulation FD** (Fair Disclosure) is an SEC rule enacted in 2000 that prohibits public companies from selectively disclosing **material non-public information (MNPI)** to certain market participants without simultaneously making the same information available to the general public.

Before Reg FD, it was common practice for companies to brief favored analysts or large institutional investors with guidance or updates before releasing that information publicly. The SEC concluded this gave sophisticated investors an unfair advantage over retail investors.

## Who it covers

Regulation FD applies to:

- **Public companies** (issuers with securities registered under Section 12 or Section 15 of the Exchange Act)
- **Company insiders** making disclosures: senior officers, directors, investor relations staff, and anyone acting on behalf of the company

It applies when disclosing to:
- Broker-dealers and their employees
- Investment advisers and managers
- Investment companies (mutual funds, hedge funds)
- Holders of the company's securities who might trade on the information

It does **not** apply to:
- Disclosures to persons who owe a duty of confidentiality (e.g., lawyers, investment bankers in an M&A process, under appropriate NDA)
- Ordinary-course business disclosures to customers, suppliers, or employees
- Foreign private issuers (they have separate disclosure rules)

## What triggers Reg FD

The key concepts are **materiality** and **selectivity**:

- **Material information** is information a reasonable investor would consider important in making an investment decision. Classic examples: earnings guidance changes, merger discussions, major contract wins or losses, regulatory approvals or rejections, executive departures.
- **Selective disclosure** means telling some but not all simultaneously.

If a CFO mentions on a private call with analysts that "Q3 is tracking below our guidance range," that's a Reg FD violation unless it's simultaneously disclosed publicly (via Form 8-K or press release).

## Intentional vs. unintentional disclosure

The rule distinguishes:

- **Intentional selective disclosure** — the company must disclose simultaneously to the public (same day, via 8-K or equivalent)
- **Unintentional selective disclosure** — the company must disclose publicly "promptly" after discovering the disclosure occurred (same trading day if before 4pm, or before market open the next day)

## Why traders care

1. **Analyst calls and non-deal roadshows** — companies can discuss results one-on-one with analysts only after earnings are publicly disclosed. The post-earnings management access is carefully structured to avoid Reg FD violations.
2. **Conference Q&A** — investor day and industry conference presentations are typically broadcast publicly (webcast) precisely because of Reg FD concerns.
3. **IR channel monitoring** — sophisticated investors listen carefully to management tone and language shifts in public venues, knowing these are the only channels of communication since Reg FD.
4. **Trading on selective disclosures** — if you receive MNPI from a company insider and trade on it, you may have both a Reg FD violation (on the company's side) and an insider trading violation (on yours).
5. **Expert networks** — a persistent gray area: research consultants using former employees to provide "channel checks" can inadvertently transmit MNPI if those employees share confidential information.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **"Off the record" guidance** — companies sometimes say things informally that they don't intend as disclosures; the SEC considers whether reasonable investors would view the information as material regardless of the speaker's intent.
- **Earnings pre-announcements** — if a company informally pre-announces to analysts that results will miss, that's a violation; the proper path is an immediate 8-K.
- **Social media** — the SEC clarified in 2013 (after the Netflix/Reed Hastings case) that social media posts can satisfy Reg FD disclosure requirements if the channel is sufficiently widely followed and disclosed as an investor communications channel.
- **Due diligence processes** — sharing financial projections with a potential acquirer under NDA is permissible; the confidentiality agreement carves out Reg FD's reach.
- **Mosaic theory** — combining many pieces of public information to reach a non-public conclusion is generally legal; the line is crossed when non-public information is one of the inputs.
