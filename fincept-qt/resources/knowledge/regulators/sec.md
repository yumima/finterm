# SEC — U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission

The **Securities and Exchange Commission** is the primary federal regulator of U.S. securities markets. Created by the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 in the aftermath of the 1929 crash, its mission is to protect investors, maintain fair and orderly markets, and facilitate capital formation. Nearly every public company, broker-dealer, investment adviser, and exchange in the U.S. operates under its oversight.

## Who it covers

The SEC regulates:

- **Public companies** — required to file 10-K (annual), 10-Q (quarterly), and 8-K (material events) reports
- **Broker-dealers** — firms that execute trades on behalf of clients or for their own account
- **Investment advisers** — registered with the SEC if they manage over $100M in assets (state-regulated below)
- **Exchanges and ATS** — NYSE, Nasdaq, and alternative trading systems
- **Mutual funds and ETFs** — under the Investment Company Act of 1940
- **Hedge funds and private equity** (indirectly) — via Form PF and Regulation D exemptions

## Key rules every trader should know

**Regulation S-K** governs the non-financial disclosures in public filings — the MD&A section, risk factors, and executive compensation tables you read in 10-Ks.

**Regulation FD (Fair Disclosure)** prohibits companies from sharing material non-public information with select investors without simultaneously releasing it to the public. If a CFO tells a hedge fund manager about a guidance miss before the press release, that's an SEC violation.

**Rule 10b-5** is the foundational anti-fraud rule. It prohibits any untrue statement of material fact, any scheme to defraud, or any act that operates as a fraud in connection with the purchase or sale of any security. Most insider trading prosecutions hang on Rule 10b-5.

**Regulation T** (jointly with the Federal Reserve) sets the initial margin requirement for equity purchases at 50% — meaning you can borrow at most half the value of a stock purchase on margin.

**Rule 10b5-1 plans** allow insiders to pre-schedule stock sales on a set formula or schedule, creating an affirmative defense against insider trading allegations. The SEC tightened these rules in 2023 to require cooling-off periods of 90–120 days.

**Short-sale rules** — Rule 10b-21 requires delivery of securities before short sale, and Regulation SHO governs locate requirements and failure-to-deliver thresholds.

## Why traders care

1. **Earnings timing** — SEC rules force material announcements before or after market hours, protecting against trading on advance knowledge of results.
2. **8-K filings** — major corporate events (acquisitions, CEO changes, guidance updates) trigger immediate 8-K filings that move stocks.
3. **13F filings** — institutional managers with >$100M must disclose equity holdings quarterly; these are the basis for many "smart money" tracking strategies.
4. **Short interest data** — SEC Rule 13f-2 (effective 2025) requires institutional short sellers to report positions, increasing transparency.
5. **Enforcement risk** — civil penalties, disgorgement, and trading bans are real consequences for violations; criminal referrals go to the DOJ.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **Tipping material non-public information** to friends or family is itself illegal, even if you don't trade — the tipper can be liable for the tippee's profits.
- **Selective disclosure** by IR teams happens subtly — detailed Q&A with analysts that reveals guidance before the public call can violate Reg FD.
- **10b5-1 plan abuse** — creating a plan immediately before known bad news and selling into it is a red flag the SEC now scrutinizes aggressively.
- **Beneficial ownership reporting** — crossing 5% ownership in any class of equity triggers a Schedule 13D/13G filing requirement within 10 days; missing deadlines draws SEC attention.
- **"Pump and dump"** schemes targeting micro-cap stocks remain the most common retail investor harm; the SEC issues trading suspension orders frequently.

## Enforcement

The SEC can bring civil enforcement actions and impose fines, disgorgement of profits, and industry bars. Criminal charges require referral to the Department of Justice. The SEC's Division of Enforcement uses quantitative surveillance tools to flag unusual options activity and trading patterns ahead of announcements — the statistical case often comes before the human investigation.
