# CFTC — Commodity Futures Trading Commission

The **Commodity Futures Trading Commission** is the federal regulator of U.S. futures, options on futures, and over-the-counter derivatives markets. Created by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission Act of 1974, the CFTC operates independently of the SEC (which covers equities and equity options). The two agencies have overlapping authority in some areas — notably single-stock futures and crypto — that has historically created jurisdictional tension.

## Who it covers

The CFTC regulates:

- **Futures exchanges** — CME Group, ICE, CBOE Futures Exchange, and others (called Designated Contract Markets, or DCMs)
- **Swap dealers and major swap participants** — banks and financial firms dealing in interest rate swaps, credit default swaps, commodity swaps
- **Commodity pool operators (CPOs) and commodity trading advisors (CTAs)** — managed futures funds and their managers
- **Introducing brokers and futures commission merchants (FCMs)** — firms that accept orders and hold customer margin for futures

**What the CFTC does not regulate:** equity securities, equity options, and Treasury securities (SEC and Fed cover those). Crypto regulation is contested — the CFTC asserts jurisdiction over crypto commodities (Bitcoin, Ether) and their futures; the SEC asserts securities law over tokens it deems securities.

## Key rules every trader should know

**Position limits** — the CFTC sets speculative position limits on physically-settled commodity contracts (agricultural, energy, metals) to prevent any single trader from cornering a market. Limits vary by commodity and delivery month. Exemptions apply for bona fide hedgers (e.g., airlines hedging jet fuel).

**Swap reporting and clearing (Dodd-Frank Title VII)** — the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act required most standardized OTC swaps to be:
1. Reported to a swap data repository (SDR)
2. Cleared through a central counterparty (CCP)
3. Executed on a swap execution facility (SEF) if standardized enough

This was a direct response to the opacity of the OTC derivatives market revealed in 2008.

**Large trader reporting** — any trader exceeding CFTC-set thresholds must file daily position reports. This data feeds the **Commitments of Traders (COT) report**, published weekly, showing commercial hedger, large speculator, and small trader positions.

**Margin for futures** — the CFTC oversees performance bond (initial and maintenance margin) levels set by exchanges. Unlike equity margin (a loan), futures margin is a good-faith deposit against potential losses.

## Why traders care

1. **COT report** — published every Friday for data as of Tuesday, the COT is widely used to gauge positioning in commodity and FX futures. Extreme net-long or net-short positions by non-commercials can signal crowded trades.
2. **Position limits create squeeze constraints** — the CFTC's position limit regime was directly implicated in the Hunt Brothers silver corner (1980) and the LME nickel squeeze (2022, different jurisdiction).
3. **NFA registration** — all futures professionals must be registered with the **National Futures Association (NFA)**, the CFTC's SRO equivalent, analogous to FINRA for securities.
4. **Crypto futures** — Bitcoin (CME BTC futures) and Ether futures are CFTC-regulated products. Spot crypto ETFs, however, went through SEC approval.
5. **Whistleblower program** — like the SEC's, the CFTC's program offers 10–30% of sanctions above $1M to whistleblowers; has resulted in nine-figure penalties.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **Wash trading** — entering offsetting orders to create artificial volume is prohibited; common in illiquid crypto markets and periodically detected in futures.
- **Spoofing** — placing large orders with intent to cancel before execution to move prices was criminalized in Dodd-Frank; multiple high-profile prosecutions since 2015.
- **Exceeding position limits** — even unintentionally; firms must have real-time position monitoring systems.
- **Misuse of hedger exemptions** — claiming hedge exemptions to circumvent speculative limits without a bona fide underlying business exposure is a CFTC enforcement priority.
- **Customer fund segregation** — FCMs must keep customer margin strictly segregated from firm capital; commingling is a criminal violation (MF Global 2011 is the canonical failure).
