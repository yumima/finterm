# Federal Reserve Board — The Fed

The **Federal Reserve Board (FRB)** is the governing body of the U.S. Federal Reserve System, the central bank of the United States. The Board consists of seven governors appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, serving staggered 14-year terms. The Fed operates through 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks and has an independent mandate to pursue maximum employment and price stability — the "dual mandate" established by Congress.

## Who it covers

The Federal Reserve regulates and supervises:

- **Bank holding companies and financial holding companies** — including the largest Wall Street firms (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley all operate under holding company structures)
- **State-chartered member banks** (state banks that join the Federal Reserve System)
- **Foreign bank branches and agencies** operating in the U.S.
- **Systemically Important Financial Institutions (SIFIs)** — banks above $100B in assets face enhanced prudential standards

The FDIC covers state non-member banks; the OCC covers nationally-chartered banks. The Fed is the umbrella regulator for holding companies.

## Key rules and functions every trader should know

**Monetary policy and the FOMC** — the Federal Open Market Committee meets 8 times per year and sets the federal funds rate target. This is the single most market-moving policy decision in the world. Markets price in rate expectations via fed funds futures; Fed communications (speeches, minutes, the Summary of Economic Projections / "dot plot") move every asset class.

**Regulation T** — the Fed's Reg T sets the **initial margin requirement for equity purchases at 50%**. When you buy on margin, you must put up at least half in cash. FINRA and broker house margin rules then set ongoing maintenance requirements (typically 25–30%).

**Stress testing (DFAST and CCAR)** — under Dodd-Frank, the Fed runs annual stress tests on large banks, simulating severe recession and market shock scenarios. Results directly constrain bank capital distributions (dividends, buybacks) and affect bank stock valuations.

**Reserve requirements** — the Fed historically required banks to hold a percentage of deposits in reserve; in March 2020 the Fed reduced reserve requirements to zero for all depository institutions, relying instead on interest on excess reserves (IOER, now IORB) to control the floor of the funds rate.

**Quantitative easing and tightening (QE/QT)** — the Fed's balance sheet expanded from ~$900B in 2008 to ~$9T in 2022 through asset purchases (QE), and has since been reduced through QT (allowing bonds to mature without reinvestment). The size and composition of the balance sheet affects long-term interest rates and liquidity conditions.

**Payment systems oversight** — the Fed operates Fedwire (large-value payments) and FedACH (batch payments), and cleared the path for FedNow instant payments in 2023.

## Why traders care

1. **Rate decisions dominate everything** — the fed funds rate affects the discount rate for every asset, from bond prices to equity valuations to real estate.
2. **Fed communications are tradeable** — every FOMC statement, press conference, and speech from Fed governors affects markets. The Fed has formalized "forward guidance" as a policy tool.
3. **Bank stress test results** — released annually, they set the floor for how aggressively major banks can return capital. Stock buyback announcements follow immediately.
4. **Reg T defines your leverage** — the 50% initial margin requirement applies to any margin account at a broker.
5. **Fed balance sheet / repo market** — the repo market (overnight lending between banks and dealers) is the plumbing of financial markets; Fed intervention in repo (as in September 2019 and March 2020) signals stress.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **Regulation T violations** — buying more equity than allowed under 50% initial margin results in a "Reg T call" that must be met within a specific time frame.
- **Bank secrecy / BSA compliance** — banks supervised by the Fed must maintain robust anti-money-laundering (AML) programs; failures result in massive consent orders.
- **Capital requirement gaming** — risk-weighting manipulation (assigning low risk weights to high-risk assets) is a persistent supervisory concern.
- **Overreliance on Fed backstops** — the Fed's role as lender of last resort creates moral hazard; "the Fed will rescue us" was the implicit assumption behind SVB's duration bet.
