# Insider Trading Rules

**Insider trading** refers to buying or selling a security in breach of a fiduciary duty or other relationship of trust while in possession of material, non-public information (MNPI) about that security. It is illegal under Rule 10b-5 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and is prosecuted as both a civil and criminal offense.

The United States has the most aggressive insider trading enforcement regime in the world, combining SEC civil enforcement with DOJ criminal prosecution. Penalties include disgorgement of profits, civil fines up to three times the profit gained, and criminal imprisonment up to 20 years.

## Who it covers

**Classical insider trading** — corporate insiders (officers, directors, employees) who trade on MNPI they obtained through their position. Classic example: a CFO who sells stock after learning the company will miss earnings but before the announcement.

**Misappropriation theory** — outsiders who breach a duty owed to the *source* of the information (not necessarily to the company or its shareholders). Example: a lawyer working on a merger trades on the deal before announcement, breaching duty to their law firm client.

**Tippers and tippees** — the person who discloses MNPI (tipper) and the person who receives and trades on it (tippee) can both be liable. The tippee's liability generally requires knowing the tip came from a breach of duty and that the tipper received some personal benefit.

## Key legal provisions

**Rule 10b-5** — the foundational anti-fraud rule. Prohibits any "device, scheme, or artifice to defraud" or "any act, practice, or course of business which operates or would operate as a fraud" in connection with securities transactions.

**Section 10(b)** — the statutory basis; Rule 10b-5 is the SEC's implementing regulation.

**The STOCK Act (2012)** — Stop Trading on Congressional Knowledge Act. Explicitly makes insider trading laws applicable to members of Congress, their staff, and executive branch employees. Requires congressional members and senior staff to file trade disclosures within 45 days of a transaction over $1,000.

**Rule 10b5-1 plans** — allow insiders to trade on a pre-arranged schedule or formula that was established while they were not in possession of MNPI. If the plan is legitimate, it provides an affirmative defense. The SEC tightened requirements in 2023:
- Mandatory cooling-off periods (90 days for officers/directors, or until the next quarterly earnings, whichever is longer; up to 120 days for certain insiders)
- Certifications required at plan adoption
- Limits on single-trade plans (only one per year)

## What is material non-public information

**Material** = information a reasonable investor would consider important in deciding whether to buy, sell, or hold. The test is whether there is a substantial likelihood the information would be viewed as significantly altering the "total mix" of available information.

Examples of material information:
- Unannounced earnings results or guidance changes
- Pending merger, acquisition, or major contract
- Regulatory approval or rejection of a key product
- Senior executive departure or appointment
- Cyber breach, litigation developments, or restatement

**Non-public** = not yet released to the general public through official channels (press release, 8-K, earnings call).

## Why traders care

1. **Expert networks** — a legitimate practice (paying former industry experts for insight) becomes illegal if those experts share confidential company information. The line is actively litigated.
2. **Options activity before events** — the SEC monitors unusual options volume before M&A announcements, earnings, and FDA decisions. Statistically anomalous positioning triggers investigations.
3. **Hedge fund information edges** — many "information advantages" sought by hedge funds walk the MNPI line. Legal channel checks, industry contacts, and government intelligence work are legitimate; confidential tips from insiders are not.
4. **Accidental receipt of MNPI** — if an investor receives MNPI through no action of their own (e.g., a company employee calls and volunteers information), they should not trade and should consult counsel. The SEC may investigate regardless of intent.
5. **Congressional trading** — STOCK Act filings are public record and widely tracked. Significant congressional trades in sectors where members have oversight roles attract media and regulatory scrutiny.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **"I didn't know it was material"** is a weak defense — the SEC applies an objective standard, not a subjective one.
- **Tipping without trading** — informing a family member or friend about MNPI, even without personally trading, can result in civil liability for the profits they make.
- **Front-running client orders** — a broker who trades ahead of a large client order is both an insider trading violation and a breach of fiduciary duty.
- **Social media leaks** — tweeting or posting MNPI (even to "expose" a company) can constitute illegal disclosure.
- **10b5-1 plan timing** — plans adopted right before bad news (or canceled right before the required disclosure of bad news) invite scrutiny even if technically compliant.
