# FINRA — Financial Industry Regulatory Authority

**FINRA** is a self-regulatory organization (SRO) that oversees broker-dealers in the United States. It is not a government agency — it was created by the brokerage industry and authorized by Congress under the Securities Exchange Act to self-police. The SEC oversees FINRA, and FINRA oversees the ~3,500 broker-dealers and ~620,000 registered representatives operating in U.S. markets.

## Who it covers

FINRA regulates any firm or individual that:

- Buys or sells securities on behalf of customers (broker)
- Buys or sells securities for its own account (dealer)
- Is registered as a representative (Series 7, 63, 65, etc.)

It does **not** directly regulate investment advisers (that's the SEC/state regulators), futures (CFTC/NFA), or exchanges themselves.

## Key rules every trader should know

**Suitability rule (Rule 2111)** — brokers must have a reasonable basis to believe a recommendation is suitable for a customer given their risk tolerance, investment objectives, and financial situation. This was the standard for decades.

**Reg BI (Regulation Best Interest)** replaced FINRA's suitability standard in 2020 — requires brokers to act in the customer's "best interest," a higher bar than mere suitability. Firms must document their reasoning and disclose conflicts.

**Pattern Day Trader (PDT) rule (FINRA Rule 4210)** — if you execute 4 or more day trades within 5 business days in a margin account, and those trades represent more than 6% of total trading activity, you are classified as a "pattern day trader" and must maintain a minimum of **$25,000** in equity. Falling below triggers a day trading call; the account is restricted until the minimum is restored.

**Margin requirements** — FINRA sets minimum maintenance margin at 25% for long positions (many brokers use 30%), and 30% for short positions. Initial margin of 50% follows Regulation T. If your account falls below maintenance margin, you receive a **margin call** and must deposit funds or have positions liquidated.

**FINRA BrokerCheck** — the public database of registered brokers, their employment history, and any disciplinary actions. Every investor should check their broker here.

## Why traders care

1. **PDT rule shapes retail trading strategy** — the $25K minimum is a hard constraint; accounts below it are restricted to 3 day trades per rolling 5-day period.
2. **Margin calls can force selling at the worst time** — maintenance margin requirements trigger automatic liquidation if not met within the timeframe the broker specifies.
3. **Complaint data is public** — FINRA Arbitration handles disputes between clients and brokers; outcomes are searchable.
4. **Rule 4210 governs options margin** — specific requirements apply to spreads, naked calls, and complex positions that differ substantially from equity margin.
5. **Short interest reporting** — FINRA publishes twice-monthly short interest data for equities, which traders use to gauge squeeze potential.

## Common violations and gotchas

- **PDT trap** — a common mistake: making 3 day trades in week 1, then a 4th in week 2 when the 5-day window still includes earlier trades. The count resets on a rolling basis.
- **Good faith violations** (in cash accounts) — selling a security bought with unsettled funds, then buying again before settlement, can result in the account being restricted to settled-funds-only for 90 days.
- **Margin account requirement** — the PDT rule only applies to margin accounts. Cash accounts can still day trade but only with settled funds, which takes T+1 to settle.
- **Churning** — excessive trading to generate commissions is a FINRA violation; can result in regulatory action and arbitration liability.
- **Free riding** — buying securities without sufficient settled funds and then selling before paying is prohibited under FINRA rules.
