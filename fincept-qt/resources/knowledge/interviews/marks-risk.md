# Howard Marks — Second-Level Thinking and Market Cycles

## Who he is

Howard Marks co-founded Oaktree Capital Management in 1995, building it into the world's largest distressed debt investor with over $150B under management. Before Oaktree he ran the high-yield and convertible bond groups at TCW, generating extraordinary returns on deeply distressed assets during the S&L crisis and early 1990s recession.

Marks is arguably the most articulate living practitioner on the subject of risk. His **memos to clients** — written since 1990, made public since 2004, and collected in two books (*The Most Important Thing* and *Mastering the Market Cycle*) — constitute the most sophisticated public writing on investment risk and market cycles available.

His January 2000 memo "bubble.com" warned of the dot-com bubble's unsustainability weeks before it peaked. His September 2007 memo "The Race to the Bottom" identified the conditions for the credit crisis before it broke.

## Core thesis

Marks's central insight is the distinction between **first-level thinking** and **second-level thinking**:

- **First-level thinking**: "This is a good company — buy the stock." "The economy is slowing — sell."
- **Second-level thinking**: "This is a good company, but everyone knows it and the stock reflects it at a premium. Is the premium excessive?"

The market is the consensus of all participants' first-level analysis. To beat the market, your thinking must be different from consensus *and correct*. This is harder than it sounds: being different and right simultaneously is rare; being different and wrong just looks contrarian.

## Key principles

1. **Risk is not volatility — it is the permanent loss of capital.** Marks is emphatic on this: using standard deviation as a risk measure confuses outcome with input. True risk is the probability of not achieving your investment objective, which may or may not correlate with volatility. The safe asset and the risky asset cannot be distinguished by last year's price movement.

2. **You can't measure risk ex-ante; you can only recognize it ex-post.** Risk is invisible until it crystallizes. But there are observable *proxies*: stretched valuations, excessive leverage, low risk premiums, widespread complacency, and "everyone knows" narratives. When these are present, risk is high even if recent volatility is low.

3. **Understanding the market cycle is more important than predicting the economy.** Marks doesn't claim to forecast GDP or earnings precisely. He claims to recognize where the market is in the cycle — ranging from pessimism and undervaluation to optimism and overvaluation — and to position accordingly: more aggressive when cheap and fearful, more defensive when expensive and greedy.

4. **Controlling the downside is the primary objective.** "If we avoid the losers, the winners take care of themselves." Oaktree's investment philosophy starts with what can go wrong. Expected return is irrelevant if the distribution of outcomes includes ruinous loss scenarios.

5. **Being too early is indistinguishable from being wrong.** In distressed debt and cycle-timing, the critical discipline is not being right about the direction — it's being patient enough to wait until the setup is compelling and not deploying too much capital before the thesis fully develops.

## Worked example: The 2007–2009 credit crisis

In 2005–2007, Marks wrote repeatedly about tightening credit spreads, aggressive underwriting standards, and investor complacency in the high-yield market. He did not know exactly when or how the crisis would manifest. What he knew was that risk premiums were inadequate for the risks being taken.

Oaktree moved to more defensive positioning and reduced high-yield exposure before the crisis. When spreads blew out in 2008–2009, Oaktree deployed capital aggressively into distressed debt — buying corporate bonds at 30–50 cents on the dollar from forced sellers. The Oaktree Opportunities funds from that vintage generated some of the highest returns in the firm's history.

The setup was entirely consistent with Marks's framework: buy when risk premiums are compensating you for risk, sell when they aren't.

## What modern traders can apply

- **Ask "what is priced in?"** For any investment, the relevant question is not whether the story is good or bad — it's whether the current price correctly reflects what you know. A great business at a great price is the goal; a great business at a terrible price is a bad investment.
- **Develop a "temperature gauge" for the market.** Track credit spreads, volatility (VIX), IPO quality, M&A deal terms, and general media tone. These are proxies for where the market sits on the fear-greed spectrum. Act accordingly.
- **In distressed cycles, timing is less important than price.** When buying truly dislocated assets at deep discounts to intrinsic value, being 6 months early costs you. Being right about the discount pays you back many times over.
- **Read Marks's memos.** The public archive at Oaktree is free and spans 35 years. Reading them in sequence — and seeing how his thinking evolved — is one of the most efficient ways to develop a framework for market cycle awareness.

## Common misunderstandings

- **"Marks just sells doom"** — he is explicitly not a perma-bear. His framework calls for *more* aggressiveness when prices are depressed and pessimism is widespread. The contrarian move is as often buying as selling.
- **"Second-level thinking means being contrarian"** — it means being *independently analytical*, which often leads to contrarian conclusions but not always. Sometimes consensus is right.
- **"Marks can predict when the market will peak"** — he explicitly states he cannot and that anyone who claims to is mistaken. His framework identifies when risk is elevated, not when the correction will arrive.
- **"Risk is only about downside"** — Marks notes that the risk of missing upside is also real. Being too defensive when assets are cheap can be as costly as being too aggressive when they're expensive.
