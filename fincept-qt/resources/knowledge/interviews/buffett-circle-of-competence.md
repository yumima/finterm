# Warren Buffett — Circle of Competence

## Who he is

Warren Buffett has compounded Berkshire Hathaway's book value at roughly 20% per year from 1965 to the present, generating the most studied long-term investment track record in history. He learned value investing directly from Benjamin Graham at Columbia, then evolved the approach under Charlie Munger's influence toward paying higher prices for genuinely exceptional businesses.

Buffett's public record — 60+ annual letters to shareholders, decades of annual meeting Q&A, and a handful of long-form interviews — constitutes the most accessible body of investment wisdom ever made public. His framework for what to buy, what to avoid, and how to think about price vs. value is the bedrock of modern long-term investing.

## Core thesis

Buffett's central idea is deceptively simple: **invest only in businesses you understand well, whose competitive advantages you can assess with confidence, and whose long-term economic futures are reasonably predictable.** Everything else — price targets, macro forecasts, sector rotation — is secondary to getting this right.

The concept of the **"circle of competence"** is the practical execution of this idea. Every investor has a genuine edge in some domain — industry knowledge, analytical skill, or pattern recognition built over years. Outside that circle, you are not investing; you are speculating on things others understand better.

## Key principles

1. **Define your circle honestly.** "What you don't know is not the problem. Not knowing what you don't know is." Most investors overestimate the size of their circle — the discipline is in recognizing the boundary, not expanding it carelessly.

2. **Buy moats, not just earnings.** Buffett pays for durable competitive advantages: brand loyalty (Coca-Cola), switching costs (bank deposits, enterprise software), low-cost production (GEICO's direct model in insurance), and network effects. The moat determines whether today's earnings are repeatable in 10 years.

3. **Price is what you pay, value is what you get.** Even a great business at too high a price is a bad investment. The margin of safety concept (from Graham) persists in Buffett's thinking: buy with enough cushion that even if your analysis is partially wrong, you don't lose money.

4. **Time is the friend of wonderful businesses, the enemy of mediocre ones.** Compound returns require not selling. The tax cost of selling a winning position is real; holding allows pre-tax compounding.

5. **Temperament over intellect.** "Success in investing doesn't correlate with IQ once you're above a certain level." What matters is emotional stability — not panicking in bear markets, not getting greedy at peaks, not overreacting to quarterly noise.

## Worked example: Coca-Cola (1988)

In 1988–89, Buffett bought roughly $1B of Coca-Cola stock after the 1987 market crash sent valuations lower. His reasoning was classic: Coca-Cola sold a product consumed 1B+ times per day globally, had a brand valued above all competitors, earned high returns on invested capital, and had pricing power. The business was predictable 10 years out. Buffett held the position for 35+ years.

The key insight was not a complex model — it was a confident answer to the question: "Will people drink more Coca-Cola in 10 years?" Yes, globally, clearly. That certainty, combined with a reasonable price, was all he needed.

## What modern traders can apply

- **Concentrate on your edge.** Trading in sectors you follow closely for work or have studied for years will outperform diversifying into areas you don't understand just for the sake of it.
- **The 20-punch-card concept.** Buffett often uses the thought experiment of a card with 20 holes in a lifetime — you get 20 investments, no more. This forces serious evaluation rather than trading in and out of dozens of positions.
- **Ask "is this a business I want to own for 10 years?"** Not because holding periods should be 10 years, but because it separates genuine business analysis from short-term price prediction.
- **Miss the complicated ones.** Buffett explicitly acknowledges that he missed many great technology businesses because they were outside his circle. He did not lament this — staying within the circle is the discipline.

## Common misunderstandings

- **"Buffett just buys cheap stocks"** — he does not. Buffett evolved beyond Graham's deep value (buying cigar butts at a discount). He explicitly pays fair prices for exceptional businesses. The moat matters more than the multiple.
- **"His approach doesn't work in modern markets"** — his strongest returns on individual investments (Apple, Bank of America, BNPL, BYD) occurred after 2010. The framework adapts; the business-quality-first lens is timeless.
- **"You need billions to invest like Buffett"** — Buffett has explicitly said he could generate 50%+ returns annually with a small capital base by exploiting inefficiencies in small/micro-cap stocks that he can no longer access at Berkshire's scale.
- **"Diversification is always prudent"** — Buffett views over-diversification as protection against ignorance. If you genuinely understand 5 businesses deeply, concentrating in them beats spreading across 50 you don't understand.
