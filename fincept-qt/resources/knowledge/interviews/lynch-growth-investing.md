# Peter Lynch — Tenbaggers and Growth at a Reasonable Price

## Who he is

Peter Lynch managed Fidelity's Magellan Fund from 1977 to 1990, generating a 29.2% average annual return — outperforming the S&P 500 in all but two of those 13 years and turning $1,000 into $28,000. When he retired at 46, Magellan was the largest mutual fund in the world.

Lynch's two books — *One Up on Wall Street* (1989) and *Beating the Street* (1993) — remain among the most widely read investment books ever written. His appeal is practical: he argued that ordinary investors have genuine advantages over professionals by observing the world around them before it shows up in analyst reports.

## Core thesis

Lynch's investing philosophy centers on three interconnected ideas:

1. **Invest in what you know.** Consumers and workers often observe business trends — a new product, a crowded store, a disruptive service — before Wall Street models them. This observation-based edge is most powerful in consumer and retail businesses, less so in highly technical industries.

2. **Tenbaggers.** Lynch popularized the term for stocks that return 10× your investment. His argument: one tenbagger in a portfolio of 10 positions turns a 10% position into a 100% portfolio gain even if all other positions go to zero. This justifies holding growth positions through normal volatility rather than selling at 50% or 100% gains.

3. **Growth at a Reasonable Price (GARP).** Lynch was a growth investor who cared deeply about price. His primary valuation tool was the **PEG ratio** — Price/Earnings divided by Earnings Growth Rate. A PEG of 1 is fair value; below 1 is potentially cheap; above 2 is expensive relative to growth.

## Key principles

1. **Know why you own a stock in two minutes.** If you can't articulate the investment thesis to a 10-year-old in two minutes, you don't understand it well enough to own it. This forces simplicity and clarity — and helps you recognize when the thesis has changed.

2. **The best investment opportunities are often boring.** Lynch's most famous investments were companies like Dunkin' Donuts, Hanes (pantyhose), Pep Boys (auto parts), and Stop & Shop. None of these were exciting technology stories — they were businesses growing steadily in industries no analyst was writing about.

3. **Categorize your stocks.** Lynch classified stocks into six types: Slow Growers, Stalwarts, Fast Growers, Cyclicals, Turnarounds, and Asset Plays. Each category requires different analysis and has different return expectations. Mixing the frameworks (applying growth metrics to cyclicals, for example) produces systematic errors.

4. **Fear and hope are the enemies.** Selling winners to "lock in gains" and holding losers to "get back to breakeven" are the two most common behavioral errors Lynch observed. The math works the opposite way: add to your winners if the thesis holds; sell losers when the thesis breaks.

5. **The edge of individual investors.** Lynch argued retail investors can observe consumer trends 12–18 months before professional analysts model them. The institutional "edge" of information and computing power is real but does not apply to consumer behavior that anyone can see.

## Worked example: Dunkin' Donuts

Lynch noticed that every Dunkin' Donuts location he visited was consistently crowded, the product was simple and consumable (daily repeat purchases), and the franchising model allowed rapid expansion with low capital intensity. He checked the store count growth, comparable-store sales, and franchise economics before writing a report.

The stock was a 10-bagger from his initial purchase. The insight was not financial engineering — it was observation followed by verification of the financial case.

## What modern traders can apply

- **Use the PEG ratio as a first filter.** A fast-growing company at PEG 0.7 (e.g., P/E 14× on 20% growth) is potentially more interesting than a slow grower at P/E 25×. It captures the growth for price tradeoff that pure P/E ignores.
- **Follow your own observations.** If you work in an industry, use tech products, or observe retail trends, you have an information advantage in those areas. Translate observations into investment hypotheses before they become consensus.
- **Hold tenbagger candidates through volatility.** Lynch's biggest mistake in his own telling was selling winners too early. If a business is executing and the thesis is intact, 30% drawdowns in individual stocks during broader market corrections are noise, not signals.
- **Know when the story changes.** The two-minute thesis is your reference point. When circumstances change materially — competitive entry, management change, thesis deterioration — that's when to sell, not in response to price movement alone.

## Common misunderstandings

- **"Invest in what you know means buy consumer brands you like"** — Lynch meant understanding the business economics deeply, not just using the product. He did extensive channel checks, studied franchise economics, and verified his consumer observations with financial analysis.
- **"PEG ratio works for all stocks"** — it's most useful for steady-growth consumer and tech businesses. It breaks down for cyclicals (where earnings can be temporarily inflated at peaks), for asset-heavy businesses, and for very early-stage companies with no earnings.
- **"Lynch's approach can't compete with quant strategies"** — his framework was observation + verification, essentially a bottom-up fundamental process. The advantage he described — being ahead of institutional coverage — still exists in small-cap and underfollowed stocks.
- **"You need a large portfolio to find tenbaggers"** — Lynch ran a large fund by necessity. He himself said individual investors need 8–12 stocks in an actively managed portfolio, not 100+.
