# Asian Financial Crisis — 1997

Beginning in July 1997, a financial crisis swept across Southeast and East Asia, toppling currencies, collapsing banks, and triggering the most severe economic contractions in a generation. The Thai baht's depegging was the starting gun; the race-to-the-bottom in regional currencies lasted 18 months. GDP fell **13.7%** in Indonesia, **7.2%** in Thailand, **5.8%** in South Korea. The IMF deployed over **$110 billion** in emergency loans — its largest interventions ever. The crisis remains the textbook study of what happens when hot money meets fixed exchange rates and opaque banking systems.

## Background: The Setup (1990–1997)

The five most affected countries — Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, and South Korea — had grown at extraordinary rates through the early 1990s. GDP growth of 7–10% annually attracted enormous capital inflows from the developed world seeking higher yields in an era of low Western interest rates.

**Three structural vulnerabilities accumulated:**

**1. Fixed (or heavily managed) exchange rate pegs.** Most regional currencies were pegged to the U.S. dollar. This gave exporters confidence and attracted foreign capital, but it also meant governments were implicitly guaranteeing a conversion rate. When the dollar strengthened significantly in 1995–1996, the pegged Asian currencies became overvalued, hurting export competitiveness. The peg was increasingly a liability.

**2. Currency mismatch in corporate and bank borrowing.** Companies borrowed in dollars (cheap rates, stable-seeming FX) and earned revenues in local currency. Banks borrowed short-term in dollars and lent long-term in local currency. A currency devaluation would immediately increase the dollar-denominated debt burden for every local entity — a maturity AND currency mismatch.

**3. Hot money inflows into asset markets.** Billions flowed into Thai property, Indonesian equities, and Korean chaebol-linked corporate bonds — often with short duration, meaning it could exit quickly. Property bubbles inflated. Loan quality deteriorated.

## The Cascade

**July 2, 1997**: Thailand depletes its foreign reserves defending the baht peg against speculative attacks. The Bank of Thailand is forced to **abandon the peg**. The baht devalues 20% overnight; it falls 60% by year-end.

This detonates the regional crisis through two mechanisms:

**Competitive devaluation pressure.** If Thailand devalued, its exports became cheaper relative to Malaysia, Indonesia, and the Philippines. These countries faced pressure either to devalue too or watch their trade positions erode. Markets speculated on which peg would break next.

**Confidence contagion.** Investors began questioning whether *any* regional currency peg was sustainable. The visible vulnerability of Thailand's dollar reserves triggered outflows from the entire region. Foreign capital reversed direction en masse — from inflows to outflows in weeks.

Sequential peg breaks:
- **August 1997**: Philippines and Indonesia float their currencies
- **October 1997**: Taiwan devalues; Hong Kong dollar attacked (peg held at enormous cost)
- **November 1997**: Korean won comes under pressure
- **December 1997**: South Korea applies for IMF assistance — $57 billion, the largest IMF program to date. The won falls 50%.
- **May 1998**: Indonesian rupiah falls 85% from its pre-crisis level. President Suharto resigns after 32 years in power.

## The IMF and Austerity

The IMF's response was controversial and has been extensively debated since. Its standard prescription — fiscal austerity, high interest rates, rapid bank closures — was designed for countries with fiscal profligacy. The Asian crisis was a private-sector balance sheet crisis, not a government spending crisis.

Thailand, Indonesia, and Korea were forced to raise interest rates dramatically (to defend currencies) just as their economies were contracting. This accelerated bankruptcies, increased unemployment, and deepened recessions. Joseph Stiglitz (then World Bank Chief Economist) was a prominent critic; the IMF later partially acknowledged its prescriptions had been too severe.

## Market Impact and Human Toll

- Regional equity markets: Indonesia -81%, Thailand -75%, Malaysia -79%, Korea -72% in dollar terms (1997–1998)
- Indonesia: unemployment tripled; the rupiah collapse pushed millions back into poverty
- Malaysia: Prime Minister Mahathir imposed capital controls in September 1998 — controversial but arguably effective. Malaysia recovered faster than IMF-program neighbors.
- Hedge funds: George Soros became a villain in regional narratives for his reported short positions in the baht and ringgit. Whether speculative attacks *caused* the crisis or merely *accelerated* inevitable corrections is still debated.
- Global impact: LTCM's 1998 collapse was partly triggered by Russian default, itself a downstream consequence of emerging market contagion from Asia.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Fixed exchange rate pegs require credible reserves and consistent macroeconomic policy.** A peg without sufficient reserves is a subsidy to speculators: unlimited downside for the central bank, defined upside for the short. The longer an overvalued peg is maintained, the more violent its eventual break.

2. **Currency mismatches are hidden leverage.** Borrowing in a foreign currency with local-currency revenues is equivalent to a leveraged short position on your own currency. When the exchange rate moves, it moves the entire balance sheet.

3. **Hot money is not a structural funding source.** Short-duration capital inflows finance long-duration investments at the macro level, creating a duration mismatch for entire economies. When confidence evaporates, the reversal is abrupt.

4. **IMF conditionality is not one-size-fits-all.** The crisis demonstrated that standard fiscal austerity prescriptions can be pro-cyclical and damaging when applied to private-sector balance sheet crises.

5. **Regional financial architectures matter.** The Asian crisis accelerated the creation of the Chiang Mai Initiative (regional swap lines) and a substantial buildup in foreign exchange reserves across Asia — the "never again" response to reserve inadequacy.
