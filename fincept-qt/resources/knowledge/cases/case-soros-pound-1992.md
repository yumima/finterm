# Soros Breaking the Pound — September 1992

On **Wednesday, September 16, 1992** ("Black Wednesday"), the UK was forced to withdraw from the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) after spending £27 billion failing to defend the pound. **George Soros's Quantum Fund made over $1 billion** shorting GBP that day — about 5% of the fund's total return for 1992.

The case is the canonical study of **macro thesis betting against an unsustainable central bank policy**.

## The setup — the ERM

The European Exchange Rate Mechanism was a 1979 agreement: member-state currencies maintained fixed exchange rates against each other within ±2.25% bands. The system was a precursor to the euro.

The UK joined in October 1990 at **DM 2.95 per GBP** — the upper end of the band. Almost immediately, this rate looked too high:
- UK economy was weakening
- Germany was in post-reunification spending surge — Bundesbank kept rates high to fight inflation
- The pound, pegged to the strong DM, was forced into recession-inducing policy

## The thesis (1992)

Soros and his manager **Stanley Druckenmiller** developed the view:

1. The UK couldn't sustain DM 2.95 long-term — economy too weak
2. The Bundesbank wouldn't cut rates to help the UK (they had their own problems)
3. The UK would eventually have to **devalue or exit the ERM**
4. Until that happened, GBP was artificially propped up

The trade structure: **massive short positions in GBP**, financed by selling GBP-denominated bonds and going long DM and other currencies.

## The trigger

By summer 1992:
- UK economic data weakening
- Bundesbank President Helmut Schlesinger gave an interview saying the ERM might need realignment (a public hint that GBP was misvalued)
- Currency speculators piled on; capital flowed out of UK
- Bank of England started intervening to defend the rate

Soros sized up. Bloomberg later estimated Quantum's short GBP position at **~$10 billion notional**.

## Black Wednesday — September 16, 1992

The Bank of England's defense:

```
Morning: BoE buys GBP, sells DM
         Spent reserves furiously
         Pound stayed barely above DM 2.7780 (lower band)

11:00am: BoE raises base rate from 10% to 12%
         Pound bounces briefly, then sells off again

2:15pm:  BoE raises base rate from 12% to 15%
         (a 5-percentage-point hike in one day)
         Pound STILL falling

7:00pm:  Chancellor Norman Lamont announces
         UK is suspending ERM membership
         Sterling free to float
```

The pound dropped 9% over the next few weeks. Soros's short positions paid out spectacularly.

Total cost to the UK: **£3.4B in defense losses** (Treasury figures), plus hidden second-order effects.

## What Soros got right

1. **Identified an unsustainable policy** — the ERM peg was wrong
2. **Identified the trigger probability** — knew when conditions were ripe (after Schlesinger's hint)
3. **Sized for impact** — small positions wouldn't have moved the needle; $10B did
4. **Asymmetric structure** — limited downside (max loss ~ pound moves up modestly), huge upside if peg broke
5. **Didn't capitulate** — even when the pound bounced briefly on rate hikes, held the position

## What the UK got wrong

1. **Joined ERM at wrong rate** (Oct 1990) — entered at the high end of feasibility
2. **Defended a rate the economy couldn't sustain**
3. **Used reserves rather than fundamentals** to defend
4. **Telegraphed weakness** — emergency rate hikes in one day signaled desperation

## Lessons

1. **Central banks can lose.** They have huge resources but ultimately can't override fundamentals indefinitely.
2. **Trade against unsustainable pegs** with structural pressure, but only when the trigger is visible.
3. **Asymmetric currency trades** (one-sided risk) are some of the cleanest in macro.
4. **Sizing for impact** is sometimes appropriate — but only when the thesis is high-conviction and the structure is clean.
5. **"Reflexivity"** (Soros's own theory) — markets and fundamentals affect each other; speculation can sometimes accelerate the unwind of unstable conditions.

## Modern echoes

- **Asia 1997** — currency pegs in Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea broke
- **Argentina 2002** — peso peg to dollar broke after years of speculation
- **Swiss franc 2015** — SNB unexpectedly removed EUR/CHF cap; CHF spiked 30% in minutes
- **Ruble 2022** — Russian capital controls after Ukraine invasion
- **Yen 2024** — BOJ intervention attempts as USD/JPY hit 160

The pattern: pegs are tradable; central banks lose battles they can't win economically.
