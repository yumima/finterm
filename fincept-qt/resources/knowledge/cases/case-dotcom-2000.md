# Dot-Com Bubble — 1999–2002

The Nasdaq Composite rose **400%** between January 1995 and March 2000 — from 750 to 5,048. It then fell **78%** over the next 30 months, bottoming at 1,114 in October 2002. The round trip took nearly 15 years: the Nasdaq didn't reclaim its 2000 peak until 2015. Approximately **$5 trillion in market capitalization** was destroyed. It is the canonical case study for what happens when narrative disconnects completely from valuation.

## Background

The internet was genuinely transformative — that part of the story is true. By 1999, commercial internet adoption was accelerating. Email, e-commerce, and online services were changing daily life. The investor thesis was not irrational in its direction: the internet was going to create enormous value.

The error was in the *magnitude* and *timeline* of value attributed to businesses that had never earned a dollar, combined with a complete abandonment of valuation discipline.

## The Anatomy of the Bubble

**The business model vacuum.** Companies like Pets.com, Webvan, and Kozmo.com raised hundreds of millions of dollars from venture capital and public markets while explicitly not pursuing profitability. The going theory was "get big fast" (the phrase was everywhere): capture market share now, monetize later. Price-to-sales ratios of 50–100x were common. Companies with no revenue were valued on page views.

**Metrics as substitutes for earnings.** In the absence of profits, analysts invented new metrics: "eyeballs," "stickiness," "click-through rates," "monthly uniques." These metrics were easy to generate and impossible to translate into earnings with any rigor. When critics raised valuation concerns, proponents argued that traditional DCF analysis "didn't apply" to new-economy businesses.

**Aswath Damodaran's formulation** was definitive: every company's value is ultimately the present value of its future cash flows. This doesn't go away because the company has a ".com" at the end of its name. The terminal value assumptions buried in dot-com stocks required market domination at margins that no industry had ever sustained — assumptions few people bothered to make explicit.

**The IPO machine.** In 1999 and early 2000, investment banks were taking companies public within months of founding. Typical 1999 tech IPO: raise $100M, first-day pop of 100%+, insiders locked up for 180 days. Book-to-market underwriters were earning 7% fees on explosive deal volumes. The incentive was to get deals done, not to price them rationally.

**Notable examples:**
- **Pets.com** (IPO Feb 2000 at $11; bankrupt Nov 2000 at $0.19) — $300M raised, $82M burned in 9 months on TV advertising including a Super Bowl ad
- **theGlobe.com** (IPO Nov 1998 at $9, first-day close $63.50) — still a record first-day percentage gain; unprofitable throughout; shut down 2008
- **Webvan** (grocery delivery, raised $375M in IPO, burned $830M total before bankruptcy in 2001)
- **Cisco** (a real, profitable company) peaked at $80/share in March 2000 at a P/E of ~130x; fell to $11 in 2002. It didn't recover its 2000 high until 2021.

## What Caused the Peak and Break

**March 10, 2000**: Nasdaq peaks at 5,048.62.

The immediate catalyst for the decline was a series of factors converging:
- The Federal Reserve had raised rates six times in 1999–2000 (fed funds rate from 4.75% to 6.5%)
- A Barron's cover story ("Burning Up") in March 2000 calculated that 51 major internet companies would run out of cash within 12 months
- Several dot-com companies began missing the fundraising targets their burn rates required
- Once confidence in "next-round" funding wavered, the model collapsed — many companies had no path to profitability

The decline was not a single crash but a drawn-out deflation. The Nasdaq fell 34% by May 2000, recovered partially, then continued lower for two more years. **9,000 internet companies** received venture funding during the boom; approximately **4,800 folded**.

## The Real Economy

- **Telecom overbuild**: Enormous fiber-optic networks were laid by companies like WorldCom, Global Crossing, and 360networks, all of which went bankrupt. In 2002, fiber capacity utilization was estimated at 2–3%. The capital destruction in telecom alone exceeded $1 trillion.
- **WorldCom** (later MCI) filed the largest bankruptcy in US history ($104 billion) in July 2002, eclipsing Enron, after revealing a $3.8 billion accounting fraud.
- Unemployment in the tech sector rose sharply; San Francisco Bay Area housing prices fell 20–30%.

## Lessons Learned

1. **"This time it's different" is almost always wrong, and maximally wrong at peaks.** Every bubble produces arguments for why traditional valuation doesn't apply. The arguments are always partially true — the technology was real — but they consistently underestimate the time and capital required for monetization.

2. **Terminal value is where analysis goes to die.** A 10-year DCF where the terminal value is 90%+ of total value is an exercise in making assumptions that are untestable. Be extremely explicit about terminal growth rates and margin assumptions.

3. **Burn rate is a clock.** A company with $50M in cash burning $5M/month has 10 months. When market conditions tighten, that clock accelerates. Revenue is vanity, cash is reality.

4. **Narrative drives positioning; fundamentals determine outcomes.** You can hold a true narrative for years while the stock prices in a hundred years of growth. Being right about the industry and losing money on the stock is entirely possible.

5. **Real companies in bubbles also get overvalued.** Cisco was and is a real, successful business. It was still overvalued by 10x at the peak. Being a "quality company" does not insulate a stock from excess valuation.
