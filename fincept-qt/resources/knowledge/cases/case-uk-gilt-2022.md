# UK Gilt Crisis — September 2022

In the last week of September 2022, UK government bonds (gilts) suffered their worst price decline in modern history. 30-year gilt yields jumped from 3.7% to over 5% in four days. The Bank of England was forced to intervene with emergency bond purchases — pledging £65 billion — to prevent a collapse of pension funds across the UK. A sitting Prime Minister resigned six weeks later, in part over the market verdict on her economic program.

## Background

The crisis has two interlocking causes: fiscal policy overreach and a structural vulnerability in the UK pension industry.

**The mini-budget.** On September 23, 2022, newly installed Chancellor Kwasi Kwarteng announced an unfunded fiscal package of approximately **£45 billion in tax cuts** — the largest since 1972 — including abolition of the 45p top income-tax rate, a stamp-duty cut, and reversal of a planned corporate tax rise. Crucially, no independent Office for Budget Responsibility (OBR) forecast accompanied the announcement. Markets interpreted it as a government willing to borrow massively at a time when UK inflation was already above 10% and the Bank of England was hiking aggressively.

**Liability-Driven Investing (LDI).** UK defined-benefit pension funds had been running a strategy called LDI for over a decade. The core idea: use leveraged gilt positions (held through repo and derivatives) to match the duration of pension liabilities to long-term bond prices. When long-gilt yields fell (as they did for most of the 2010s), the strategy worked smoothly. But LDI also meant pension funds had **collateral structures that required additional cash or assets if gilt yields rose sharply** — essentially hidden margin calls embedded in the pension system.

## What Happened

- **September 23**: Kwarteng delivers mini-budget. Sterling immediately falls 3%. Gilt yields spike 100+ basis points over the week.
- **September 27**: 30-year gilt yield reaches 5.1%, up from 3.7% three days earlier. This is a historic move for a G7 sovereign bond market.
- Behind the scenes: pension funds are receiving **margin calls** on their LDI derivatives positions as yields rise. To meet them, they must sell assets — which means selling gilts — which drives yields higher — which triggers more margin calls. A textbook feedback loop.
- The Bank of England had been actively **selling** gilts (quantitative tightening) as recently as that week. It was now being forced to reverse course.
- **September 28**: BOE announces emergency unlimited gilt purchases in the 20-year+ segment for up to £65 billion through October 14. "Were dysfunction in this market to continue or worsen, there would be a material risk to UK financial stability."
- Yields immediately fell ~100bps on the intervention day.
- **October 3**: Kwarteng reverses the 45p tax cut — the political lynchpin of the mini-budget.
- **October 14**: Kwarteng fired; Jeremy Hunt named Chancellor. Hunt reversed essentially the entire mini-budget.
- **October 20**: Liz Truss resigns as Prime Minister after just 45 days in office.

## The LDI Death Spiral in Detail

At its peak, an estimated **£1.5 trillion** in UK pension assets had some LDI overlay. The typical structure involved pension funds holding leveraged long-gilt positions through repos or interest-rate swaps, with gilts or equities posted as collateral.

The sequence:
1. Gilt yields rise 100bps rapidly
2. LDI derivatives positions lose mark-to-market value
3. Prime brokers issue margin calls requiring additional collateral
4. Pension funds sell gilts (the most liquid asset) to raise cash
5. Additional gilt supply drives yields higher
6. Return to step 2

This is structurally identical to a currency peg defense spiral or the Archegos margin loop — the hedging mechanism becomes the crisis mechanism.

## Market Impact

- **Sterling (GBP/USD)**: Hit all-time low of **$1.0382** on September 26 — briefly lower than during Soros's 1992 ERM attack
- **30-year gilt yields**: +150bps in one week — an unprecedented move
- UK FTSE 250 (domestically oriented): -10% over September/October
- Several LDI funds briefly faced effective insolvency until BOE intervention
- BOE emergency gilt purchases: ultimately deployed ~£19 billion of the £65B pledged before the window closed
- UK mortgage market: hundreds of fixed-rate mortgage products pulled from market as lenders couldn't price risk; rates spiked to 6%+

## Lessons Learned

1. **Unfunded fiscal expansion during tightening cycles is immediately punished.** Solvency concerns compound inflation concerns — the double-whammy is lethal for the currency and bond market.

2. **Leverage inside "safe" institutional structures creates systemic fragility.** Pension funds are supposed to be prudent, long-term investors. LDI introduced hidden leverage that few policymakers had mapped. The crisis existed because of a risk that wasn't publicly visible.

3. **Duration risk is not just for bond traders.** Pension liabilities are duration instruments. When you match them with leveraged gilts, you create a derivative position that needs to be margined. "Matching assets and liabilities" is not risk-free.

4. **Central banks can be forced to reverse QT in days.** The BOE had announced QT weeks earlier. Within days it was buying again. Market dysfunction forced the reversal — a reminder that monetary tightening is conditional on financial stability.

5. **Markets vote faster than politics.** Truss had a mandate; she had a majority. Neither mattered. The bond market rendered its verdict in 72 hours.
