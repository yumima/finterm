# Michael Burry's CDS Shorts (2005–08)

In 2005, **Michael Burry** — a one-eyed neurologist-turned-fund-manager running Scion Capital from a small office — bought billions of dollars in **credit default swaps** on subprime mortgage bonds. By 2008 those positions paid out hundreds of percent and Scion delivered the highest hedge-fund return of the year.

The case is the canonical study of **contrarian conviction backed by deep research** — and the psychological cost of being early.

## The thesis (early 2005)

Burry started reading the actual mortgage-bond prospectuses (almost no one did). He noticed:

- US home prices had risen 40%+ in 5 years; affordability metrics historically extreme
- Loan quality was deteriorating (NINJA loans: No Income, No Job, no Assets)
- "Teaser rate" ARMs would reset higher in 2007–08, triggering payment shock
- Subprime RMBS were rated AAA by agencies based on backward-looking models
- Default rates on recent vintages were already higher than the models assumed

His conclusion: **a wave of subprime defaults would crater the bonds backed by them**, possibly cascading into the broader financial system.

## The trade structure

Direct shorting of mortgage bonds was impractical (no liquid short market). Instead, Burry contracted with investment banks to buy **credit default swaps (CDS)** — insurance contracts that pay out if a specific bond defaults.

```
Position:  Long CDS on $1B of subprime RMBS tranches
Premium:   ~50–250 bps/yr (varied by tranche)
Carry:     Negative — Scion pays premiums every quarter while waiting
Payoff:    Massive if/when the bonds default; ~$0 if they don't
```

The trades were **deeply asymmetric**: capped downside (premium paid) with potentially 10–20× upside.

## The painful middle

2005 → mid-2007: Burry was **right but early**. The trade went against him for almost two years:

- Subprime defaults rose, but bond prices held (markets in denial)
- Premium payments drained Scion's cash
- LPs (limited partners) demanded redemptions
- Burry **suspended redemptions** — wildly controversial, almost ended his career
- Banks that sold him the CDS marked positions in their favor

For 18 months, Burry's main psychological challenge wasn't the analysis — it was withstanding investor and personal pressure to capitulate.

## The payoff (2007–08)

In July 2007, two Bear Stearns subprime hedge funds collapsed — first crack. Through 2008:

- **Subprime CDS prices exploded**: from premiums of 100 bps/yr to **upfront payments of 70%+ of notional**
- Scion's positions delivered **gross returns > 700%**
- Net to investors: ~480%
- Burry shut down Scion in 2008 — exhausted, vindicated

## The wider trade

Burry was one of ~10 sophisticated investors globally who saw it. Others included:
- **John Paulson** (Paulson & Co) — netted ~$15B personally
- **Steve Eisman** at FrontPoint
- **Greg Lippmann** at Deutsche Bank
- **Jamie Mai / Charlie Ledley** at Cornwall Capital
- **Andrew Lahde** at Lahde Capital

The trade became famous when **Michael Lewis wrote *The Big Short*** (2010) and the 2015 film made Burry a household name.

## Lessons

1. **Read primary documents.** Burry read the actual bond prospectuses; almost no one else did.
2. **Asymmetric trades survive being early.** Burry could afford to wait because his max loss per trade was bounded.
3. **Conviction without infrastructure fails.** Burry needed the legal authority to suspend redemptions — without that, he'd have been forced to liquidate before payoff.
4. **Being right and being paid are different.** It took 2+ years between thesis formation and payout.
5. **Markets stay irrational longer than you stay solvent — UNLESS your trade is structured to wait.**

## The follow-up trades

Burry kept finding asymmetric setups in subsequent years (water rights, long Tesla, then short Tesla, then short the index again). His public commentary remains worth reading even when wrong — the analytical process is the value.
