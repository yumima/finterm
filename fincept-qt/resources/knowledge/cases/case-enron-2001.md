# Enron — Anatomy of an Accounting Fraud (2001)

Enron Corporation was, at its peak in 2000, the seventh-largest company in America by revenue — an energy trading and infrastructure giant with a reported market cap of **$68 billion**. By December 2, 2001, it had filed what was then the largest corporate bankruptcy in U.S. history. Its collapse cost investors approximately **$74 billion**, wiped out employee pension funds, and destroyed Arthur Andersen, one of the Big Five accounting firms.

## Background

Enron was founded as a natural gas pipeline company in 1985. Under CEO Kenneth Lay and, from 1996, President Jeffrey Skilling, it reinvented itself as an energy trading company. The transformation relied on a seductive idea: markets for electricity, bandwidth, and other commodities could be made as liquid and price-discoverable as financial markets, with Enron as the central intermediary.

By 2000, Enron had **~20,000 employees**, was active in 40 countries, and had revenues of $111 billion — though these revenues were largely gross trading volumes, not margin. The stock traded at **$90** in mid-2000.

## The Fraud

Enron's fraud operated through three interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs).** Enron created hundreds of off-balance-sheet entities — most notably LJM Cayman L.P. and LJM2 Co-Investment, run by CFO Andrew Fastow. These SPVs served two purposes: (a) buying underperforming Enron assets at inflated prices to recognize gains on Enron's income statement, and (b) hiding debt by moving liabilities off Enron's balance sheet. Fastow personally earned ~$30 million from fees paid by Enron to manage these entities — a direct conflict of interest disclosed nowhere in public filings.

**2. Mark-to-Market Accounting.** In 1991, Enron received SEC permission to use mark-to-market (MTM) accounting for its trading contracts. MTM allows companies to book the full present value of a long-term contract as revenue immediately. A 20-year energy contract worth $100 million in today's PV could be booked as $100M revenue today, even if no cash had arrived.

This was used aggressively and fraudulently. Enron would sign contracts, immediately book revenue at optimistic valuations, and never mark them down even when economic reality worsened. Losses were buried in SPVs. On paper, Enron looked perpetually profitable. In practice, it was hemorrhaging cash.

**3. Manipulation of Reported Earnings.** Skilling and Lay consistently pressured analysts to accept reported "earnings per share" that excluded substantial charges they labeled "non-recurring." Wall Street consensus accepted these numbers. Analyst sell-side coverage was almost uniformly positive — partly because investment banking relationships with Enron generated substantial fees.

## The Unraveling

- **August 14, 2001**: Skilling resigns as CEO, citing "personal reasons." He had been in office for only six months. The resignation was a major red flag; he sold $66 million in stock before the announcement.
- **October 16, 2001**: Enron announces a **$638 million third-quarter loss** and a $1.2 billion reduction in shareholder equity — the first official acknowledgment that the SPV transactions had been improper.
- **October 22**: SEC opens formal inquiry into Enron's related-party transactions.
- **October 31**: Fastow placed on leave; resigns November 8.
- **November 8**: Enron restates **five years of earnings** — reducing reported income by $586 million (1997–2001).
- **November 28**: Credit rating cut to junk. Banks withdraw credit lines. Trading counterparties refuse to do business.
- **December 2, 2001**: Enron files Chapter 11. **4,000 employees lose jobs and pension savings** — the pension fund had been heavily concentrated in Enron stock, which employees were restricted from selling.

## Arthur Andersen

Andersen was Enron's auditor. It had signed off on the financials for years. In the weeks following the scandal breaking, **Andersen employees shredded documents** related to the Enron audit. Andersen was indicted for obstruction of justice in March 2002. It surrendered its CPA license and effectively ceased operations, costing **85,000 employees** their jobs — most of whom had nothing to do with Enron. The Supreme Court later overturned the conviction on narrow procedural grounds, but the firm was gone.

## Convictions

- **Jeffrey Skilling**: 24 years in prison (later reduced to 14; released 2019)
- **Ken Lay**: Convicted; died of heart failure before sentencing in July 2006
- **Andrew Fastow**: 6 years (cooperating witness; released 2011)

## Lessons Learned

1. **Off-balance-sheet vehicles are a permanent red flag.** When a company cannot be analyzed from its reported balance sheet, ask why. Complexity of corporate structure often serves to obscure, not to add value.

2. **Mark-to-market of illiquid assets is opinion, not fact.** When companies self-report the present value of long-duration contracts, the valuation is entirely dependent on assumptions management controls. Audit review of MTM assumptions is the critical check.

3. **Revenues are not earnings.** Enron's $111 billion revenue was trading volume. Earnings per share was a fictional construct. Cash flow from operations — which was negative for years — tells the real story.

4. **Auditor independence matters.** Andersen earned more from Enron in consulting fees than in audit fees. This is now prohibited under Sarbanes-Oxley (2002), which was passed directly in response to Enron.

5. **Analyst incentives matter.** Buy-side pressure to keep coverage positive — to protect investment banking relationships — created a consensus that was systematically wrong. The few who questioned Enron (notably short-seller Jim Chanos and journalist Bethany McLean) were treated as pariahs until the collapse validated them.
