# Yen Carry Trade Unwind — August 2024

In the first week of August 2024, global markets suffered their sharpest multi-day sell-off since March 2020. The Nikkei 225 dropped **12.4% in a single session** on August 5 — its worst one-day decline since Black Monday 1987. The proximate cause was a surprise interest rate hike by the Bank of Japan. The deeper cause was a decade-long crowded trade that unwound all at once.

## Background

The yen carry trade is one of the most durable strategies in global finance. Its mechanics are simple: borrow in Japanese yen at near-zero rates, convert to a higher-yielding currency (often USD, AUD, or emerging market currencies), and invest in higher-returning assets — equities, bonds, real estate. You pocket the interest-rate differential as long as the yen doesn't appreciate enough to wipe out the gain.

For most of the 2010s and early 2020s, the Bank of Japan kept rates at or below zero as part of its "yield curve control" (YCC) policy. The yen steadily weakened, reaching **¥160 per dollar in April 2024** — a level not seen since 1990. Estimates placed total global yen-funded carry positions at **$4 trillion or more**, though the exact figure is unknowable.

## What Happened

The sequence unfolded over ten days:

- **July 31, 2024**: BOJ raised its benchmark rate from 0.1% to **0.25%** — a modest hike, but the guidance was unambiguously hawkish. Governor Kazuo Ueda explicitly said more hikes were coming.
- **August 2**: U.S. July jobs report came in well below expectations (114K vs. 175K forecast). Recession fears spiked. U.S. equity markets fell sharply. This mattered for carry trades because global equity drawdowns force de-risking.
- **August 2–5**: Yen surged from ¥152 to ¥142 per dollar in days — an approximately **7% move** in a currency that normally moves 1% in a month. Every yen strengthening directly shrinks carry-trade P&L and triggers margin calls.
- **August 5**: Nikkei fell 12.4% (-4,451 points). KOSPI (South Korea) fell 8.8%. Taiwan's market hit circuit breakers. VIX spiked to **65** intraday — the highest since COVID's worst days. S&P 500 fell ~3%.

## Why It Happened

Three dynamics collided:

**1. The funding-currency reversal.** Carry traders had borrowed yen assuming it would stay weak. When it strengthened, their positions moved against them simultaneously. There was no staggered exit — everyone saw the same P&L deterioration and faced the same margin calls.

**2. Cross-asset correlation collapse.** Yen-funded money had flowed into U.S. tech stocks, Japanese equities, Indian equities, and crypto. When the yen moved, traders had to liquidate whatever they could, not just Japan-related positions. This is why Nasdaq fell 6% in a week despite no deterioration in U.S. corporate fundamentals.

**3. CTA and options-driven amplification.** Systematic trend-following funds had been long Japanese equities and short yen for months. Stops triggered mechanically. Dealers hedging short-dated options (especially on the Nikkei) were forced to sell as delta moved against them.

The BOJ's Ryozo Himino (Deputy Governor) tried to calm markets on August 7, saying the BOJ would not hike "in unstable markets" — effectively walking back the hawkish signal. The yen partially reversed, and markets recovered most losses over the following two weeks.

## Market Impact

- Nikkei 225: -20% peak-to-trough (July to August 5), recovered to flat by end of August
- VIX: Spiked intraday to ~65, closed around 38 — highest since March 2020
- USDJPY: ¥160 → ¥142 in one month, later stabilized near ¥145
- Nasdaq 100: -8% in ten days, recovered within a month
- Emerging market currencies: broad sell-off as carry unwinding hit EM allocations
- Crypto: Bitcoin fell ~20% alongside risk assets — confirming its carry-funded exposure

## Lessons Learned

1. **Carry trades are liquidity-dependent.** The positive carry looks like free money for years; the exit happens in days. The longer the trade, the more crowded — and violent — the unwind.

2. **Funding-currency moves are underappreciated risk.** Most carry traders focus on the asset return, not the funding cost. A 7% move in yen can wipe out a full year of carry income in hours.

3. **Cross-asset contagion travels through balance sheets.** The Nikkei didn't fall because Japanese earnings worsened. It fell because the same pool of capital owned Japanese stocks AND was funded in yen. Deleveraging hits everything in the portfolio.

4. **VIX as a carry-unwind signal.** Historically, VIX spikes above 30–35 are strongly correlated with yen carry unwinds. Monitoring USDJPY and VIX together is more informative than either alone.

5. **Central bank communication risk is asymmetric.** A hawkish surprise from the BOJ — even a small one — can detonate years of positioning. The BOJ had to partially reverse its communication within a week to stabilize markets.
