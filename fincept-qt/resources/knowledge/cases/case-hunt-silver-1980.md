# Hunt Brothers Silver Corner — 1979–80

**Nelson Bunker Hunt** and **William Herbert Hunt**, two Texas oil heirs, attempted to corner the global silver market over 1979–80. They drove silver from $6/oz to $50/oz before the **CME changed margin rules**, the bubble burst, and the Hunts went from being briefly the world's wealthiest family to bankruptcy.

The case is the canonical study of **what happens when a single-counterparty position becomes too big for the market** — and why exchanges retain the power to change the rules mid-game.

## The setup (mid-1970s)

The Hunt brothers inherited a fortune from their oil-magnate father H.L. Hunt. They were:
- Worth ~$5–6B (1973 dollars; ~$30B today)
- Convinced inflation would destroy paper currencies
- Looking for inflation hedges; gold was forbidden to private US ownership at the time (until 1974)
- Decided **silver** was the inflation hedge of choice

They started accumulating silver in 1974 — bullion plus futures contracts.

## The accumulation (1976–79)

By late 1979:
- Silver price: $11/oz (Sept 1979)
- Hunts had **accumulated ~100M ounces of silver** + futures contracts for delivery of more
- This was approximately **half the world's deliverable silver**

Their strategy: buy futures contracts and **demand physical delivery**. This forced shorts to find physical silver, which was scarce, which drove the price up.

By December 1979: silver at **$32/oz**. The Hunts' position was worth ~$4.5B (against a cost base maybe half that).

## The peak (January 17, 1980)

Silver hit **$48.70/oz** intraday — a 9× rise in 18 months. Mom-and-pop sellers were melting down silverware to deliver. The Hunts' silver holdings (physical + futures) were worth approximately **$10B**.

They were briefly the wealthiest people on earth.

## The CME steps in

By mid-January, **CME (then COMEX)** had several problems:
- Hunts' position represented enormous concentration risk for the exchange
- Many shorts were small businesses (silver dealers, jewelers) who would be ruined
- The bubble threatened the credibility of the silver market itself

CME took several actions:
1. **Limited trading to "liquidation only"** — no new long positions allowed
2. **Raised margin requirements** dramatically (up to 100%+ on some contracts)
3. **Position limits** — capped how much any one entity could hold

The Hunts' position required massive new margin almost immediately.

## Silver Thursday — March 27, 1980

The Hunts couldn't meet a margin call. **Silver crashed from $21 to $10.80** in a single day as the market priced the forced unwind. The Hunts owed brokers $1.7B and didn't have it.

A consortium of banks led by Paul Volcker's Federal Reserve (yes, the Fed) organized a **$1.1B emergency loan** to the Hunts to prevent broker collapses.

By 1988, after years of litigation:
- Both Hunt brothers filed personal bankruptcy
- Found guilty of conspiring to manipulate silver markets in civil court
- Forced to pay $134M in damages
- Lost most of their fortune

Silver took decades to recover from the boom-bust. Real silver prices wouldn't return to 1980 levels until the 2010s.

## What the Hunts did right (in their own model)

- Identified inflation as a real long-term threat (it was)
- Picked an asset with limited supply
- Sized up over years, accumulating gradually
- Used futures markets for leverage

## What they did wrong

- **Position too big** — they became the market in silver
- **No tail-risk planning** — assumed exchange rules would remain unchanged
- **Single counterparty exposure** — when CME changed rules, no one could absorb their selling
- **Underestimated regulatory response** — exchanges retain ultimate control
- **Ignored convergence math** — even at fair value, holding inventory has carrying costs

## Lessons

1. **You can't be larger than the market without becoming the market's risk.** Concentration creates regulatory and clearing-house attention.
2. **Exchanges change rules in extremis.** Anyone trading futures at scale must factor in this possibility.
3. **Delivery demand is a powerful tool** — but only if you can absorb delivery without forced selling.
4. **Macro thesis ≠ trade structure.** The Hunts were right about inflation; the position structure was wrong.
5. **Forced liquidation** is the worst possible exit — your size becomes everyone else's information.

## Modern echoes

- **Amaranth (2006)** — single-trader natural gas position so large it broke when unwound
- **London Whale / JPM (2012)** — one credit-derivatives trader's position so big the market knew
- **Archegos (2021)** — Bill Hwang's family office; concentrated swap positions; brokers bagholders
- **Long-Term Capital Management (1998)** — see [[case-ltcm-1998]]

The pattern: extreme concentration + leverage + illiquidity = forced liquidation cascades.
