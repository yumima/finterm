# COVID Crash — March 2020

The fastest 30%+ decline in S&P 500 history. The index fell from a Feb 19 all-time high to a March 23 low in **just 33 days**, a −34% drawdown. The Treasury market briefly seized. The Federal Reserve responded with the largest and fastest monetary intervention ever attempted.

The case is the canonical study of **how a real-economy shock can cascade through every asset market** — and how policy intervention can break the cascade.

## Pre-crisis (Feb 2020)

US equity markets were near all-time highs:
- S&P 500: 3,386 (Feb 19 close)
- VIX: ~14
- Treasuries: 10y at 1.5%
- Credit spreads: HY at ~360 bps

COVID had been spreading in China since January, but markets had largely shrugged it off. Wuhan was perceived as contained.

## The cascade (Feb 24 – Mar 23)

**Week 1 (Feb 24–28)**: First major US selloff
- Italian outbreak makes Europe-wide spread visible
- S&P −11.5% for the week (worst since 2008)
- VIX rises from 14 → 49

**Week 2 (Mar 2–6)**: Saudi-Russia oil price war
- OPEC+ talks collapse; Saudi launches price war
- WTI crude crashes from $45 → $30
- Oil-related credit spreads explode
- Energy company defaults look imminent

**Week 3 (Mar 9–13)**: Fed cuts emergency rates
- Mar 9: −7.6% S&P, first circuit breaker since 1997
- Mar 11: WHO declares pandemic; Trump suspends European travel
- Mar 12: −9.5% S&P, second circuit breaker
- Mar 13: Fed announces $1.5T repo operations
- Mar 15 (Sun): Fed cuts to ZIRP, $700B QE announced

**Week 4 (Mar 16–20)**: Treasury market starts breaking
- Mar 16: −12% S&P (worst single day since 1987)
- Mar 17: Even Treasuries selling off — flight-to-quality breaking down
- Mar 19: 10y Treasury yield jumps despite stocks falling (unusual)
- Mar 20: Hedge funds force-selling everything liquid; gold falls too

**Week 5 (Mar 23)**: Bottom + Fed bazooka
- Mar 23 morning: Fed announces **unlimited QE + corporate bond buying** + everything else
- Mar 23: S&P closes at 2,237 — the cycle low
- VIX peak: 82.7

The day Fed announced unlimited QE was the bottom — almost exactly. Equity markets rallied 30% over the next month and didn't look back.

## What broke

### Treasury market (mid-March)
The most important market in the world briefly stopped functioning normally:
- Bid-ask spreads on **on-the-run** Treasuries widened to multiples of normal
- Hedge funds running **basis trades** (long off-the-run, short on-the-run, levered) were forced to unwind
- Their selling pushed Treasury prices down even as stocks fell
- Normal "flight to quality" broke

### Bond ETFs decoupled from NAV
- LQD (investment-grade bonds) traded at **5%+ discount to NAV** for days
- HYG (high-yield) at 7%+ discount
- Authorized participant arbitrage broke under stress

### Repo market stress
- Funding costs for primary dealers spiked
- Some dealers reportedly ran out of balance sheet capacity to facilitate trades
- Fed had to provide emergency repo of >$1T

### Credit spreads
- IG spreads: 100 → 380 bps in weeks
- HY spreads: 360 → 1,100 bps
- Spreads on energy HY: > 2,000 bps

## Fed response (the bazooka)

The Fed deployed essentially everything:
- **Cut rates to 0–0.25%** (March 15)
- **Open-ended QE** (March 23)
- **Corporate credit facilities** (PMCCF, SMCCF) — first time Fed bought corporate debt
- **Municipal bond facility**
- **Main Street Lending Program** (small business)
- **Repo operations** at limits never before tried
- **Currency swap lines** to global central banks
- **PPP / fiscal coordination** with Treasury

The Fed's balance sheet went from $4.2T (Feb) to $7.0T (June) — a **+67% expansion in 4 months**.

## What "saved" markets

In approximately this order of importance:
1. **Fed corporate bond purchases** — broke the credit spread spiral
2. **Unlimited QE** — restored Treasury market function
3. **Fiscal stimulus** (CARES Act) — supported real economy
4. **Vaccine development optimism** (later 2020) — reset expected duration of disruption

By December 2020, S&P 500 was at new all-time highs.

## What it taught

1. **Speed matters.** This was the fastest 30%+ drawdown ever. Investors who had pre-committed plans executed mechanically; those who didn't got stuck or panicked.
2. **Treasury markets can break.** "Risk-free" liquidity is conditional, not absolute.
3. **Hedge fund deleveraging** can transmit stress across asset classes that "shouldn't" be correlated.
4. **Fed reaction function shifted permanently** — the threshold for intervention is now lower.
5. **Government policy can override fundamentals briefly.** The recession was real; the equity bull market resumed.

## Lessons (especially for retail)

1. **Pre-commit to a plan.** The investors who had written rules executed them; the others made bad decisions.
2. **Cash buffer is not optional.** Anyone who needed to draw down their portfolio in March 2020 sold at the worst time.
3. **Don't try to call the bottom.** Even if you knew COVID would peak and vaccines would arrive, the bottom was on the day the Fed went infinite.
4. **Quality compounded by patience worked.** Berkshire, MSFT, AAPL, COST, etc. all fully recovered by year-end.
5. **Speculative strategies had different paths.** Some did great (long vol), some failed catastrophically (short vol, short oil at the bottom).

## Modern echoes / similar cascades

- **2008 GFC** — slower-moving, deeper recession
- **August 2007 quant quake** — first crack in subprime
- **2011 Eurozone crisis** — slower; same Fed playbook
- **August 2015 China shock** — short-lived
- **2022 inflation cycle** — different mechanism (rates), similar speed
