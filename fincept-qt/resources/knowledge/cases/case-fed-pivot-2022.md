# The Fed's 2022 Inflation Response

In March 2022, the Federal Reserve began the fastest interest rate hiking cycle since the early 1980s. Over 16 months, it raised the federal funds rate from **0–0.25% to 5.25–5.50%** — an increase of 525 basis points. The cycle forced a historic repricing of virtually every financial asset class and ultimately contributed to the most significant banking stress since 2008, including the failure of Silicon Valley Bank.

## Background: How We Got to Zero

The Fed's journey to zero started in 2008 and was re-entrenched in 2020. Following the COVID pandemic shock in March 2020, the Fed cut rates to zero and launched unlimited QE — purchasing $120 billion per month in Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities. By early 2022, the Fed's balance sheet had swollen to **$9 trillion**, nearly double its pre-COVID level.

Inflation was initially dismissed as "transitory." In November 2021, Jerome Powell was still describing inflation as driven by supply-chain bottlenecks that would resolve naturally. By December 2021, CPI was running at **7%** — the highest in 40 years. In February 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine, pushing energy and food prices higher. By June 2022, CPI reached **9.1%** — a 41-year high.

The Fed was behind. Its own preferred measure (PCE) had been above its 2% target for over a year. The pivot from "transitory" to "we will do what it takes" came in early 2022.

## The Hiking Cycle

| Meeting | Rate | Move |
|---|---|---|
| March 2022 | 0.25–0.50% | +25bps |
| May 2022 | 0.75–1.00% | +50bps |
| June 2022 | 1.50–1.75% | +75bps (first since 1994) |
| July 2022 | 2.25–2.50% | +75bps |
| September 2022 | 3.00–3.25% | +75bps |
| November 2022 | 3.75–4.00% | +75bps |
| December 2022 | 4.25–4.50% | +50bps |
| February 2023 | 4.50–4.75% | +25bps |
| March 2023 | 4.75–5.00% | +25bps |
| May 2023 | 5.00–5.25% | +25bps |
| July 2023 | 5.25–5.50% | +25bps (final hike) |

The **four consecutive 75bps hikes** in summer/fall 2022 were unprecedented in the modern Fed era. The terminal rate of 5.25–5.50% was the highest since 2001.

## Asset Market Repricing

The rate cycle forced a brutal recalculation. In a zero-rate world, the present value of distant cash flows (long-duration stocks, long-term bonds, speculative growth companies) was exceptionally high. As rates rose, discount rates rose — compressing multiples and collapsing valuations.

**Equities:**
- S&P 500: -19.4% in 2022 (worst year since 2008)
- Nasdaq 100: -32.6% in 2022 (worst year since 2000)
- ARK Innovation ETF (ARKK): -66% in 2022 (down ~75% from peak)
- Growth stocks with no earnings: many down 80–90% from 2021 peaks

**Fixed Income:**
- Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index: -13.1% in 2022 — its worst year on record
- 30-year Treasury: -39.2% total return — worst long-bond performance since the 1970s
- The traditional "60/40 portfolio" returned approximately -16% in 2022 — one of its worst years in a century, because stocks AND bonds fell together

**Crypto:**
- Bitcoin: -65% in 2022 (from $47K to $16K)
- The correlation to risk assets, made plain in March 2020, was confirmed again

**Real Estate:**
- 30-year mortgage rates rose from ~3% to ~7.5% by late 2023
- Existing home sales fell to 30-year lows as affordability collapsed
- Housing prices initially held due to low supply, but transaction volumes cratered

## The SVB Fallout

The most acute financial stability consequence was Silicon Valley Bank (March 2023 — see [[case-svb-2023|SVB 2023]]). SVB had invested deposit inflows from the 2020–2021 tech boom into long-duration Treasuries and MBS. As rates rose, those bonds lost ~15–20% of their value. When VC-backed depositors began withdrawing, SVB was forced to crystallize losses, triggering the run. The failed banks — SVB, Signature, First Republic — together had **$500 billion in assets**.

The Fed's emergency Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP, March 2023) allowed banks to borrow against Treasuries at *par value* rather than market value — a backstop specifically designed to prevent more SVB-style failures.

## Did It Work?

CPI peaked at 9.1% in June 2022 and fell to 3.2% by October 2023. Core PCE — the Fed's preferred measure — fell from a peak of 5.6% to around 3.3% by late 2023. The Fed achieved a near-"soft landing" — reducing inflation substantially without triggering a recession (though the debate continued). GDP growth remained positive throughout.

Whether the Fed would have avoided the SVB crisis with earlier, more gradual tightening — or whether the crisis was an inevitable consequence of ZIRP-era bank behavior — remains debated.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Zero rates create fragility that only becomes visible when rates rise.** SVB's duration mismatch, the -39% long-bond loss, ARK's collapse — all were latent consequences of zero rates that didn't matter until they did.

2. **"Transitory" is a dangerous word.** The Fed's credibility damage from persistent use of "transitory" in 2021 may have forced it to hike more aggressively in 2022 to re-anchor expectations. The cost of a communication error was 75bps hikes.

3. **Stocks AND bonds can both fall in inflationary regimes.** The 2022 experience reminded investors that the 60/40 "diversification" relies on bonds being a risk-off hedge. In a supply-driven inflation shock, both assets can lose simultaneously.

4. **Duration risk is not just a bond problem.** Long-duration equities — companies with profits far in the future — behave like long-duration bonds. A 300bps increase in discount rates collapses the PV of cash flows 10–20 years out.

5. **Central bank policy is the primary risk factor in all asset markets.** Every asset class — equities, bonds, real estate, crypto — repriced together in 2022 because the single dominant variable was the risk-free rate.
